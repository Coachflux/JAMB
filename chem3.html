Introduction to Kinetic Theory of Matter

The Kinetic Theory says all matter is made of tiny particles (atoms or molecules) that are always moving randomly. Their movement explains how solids, liquids, and gases behave differently.

Key Ideas (from Ababio's postulates):

  • Particles move faster with higher temperature (more kinetic energy).
  • In solids: Particles vibrate in fixed spots (strong forces hold them).
  • In liquids: Particles slide around but stay close (weaker forces).
  • In gases: Particles zoom freely and far apart (very weak forces).
  • Collisions are elastic (like bouncy balls‚Äîno energy lost).

This helps distinguish states: Solids keep shape and volume; liquids keep volume but flow; gases spread out and can be squeezed.

Brownian Movement: Proof of the theory‚Äîtiny particles (like dust in air) jiggle randomly because invisible gas molecules bump them (Ababio example: Pollen in water under a microscope).

Phenomena Supporting Kinetic Theory

State changes happen when heat changes particle speed (kinetic energy) against holding forces.

  1. Melting: Solid to liquid. Heat makes particles move more, breaking fixed positions to flow.
  2. Vaporization (Evaporation): Liquid to gas below boiling point. Fast surface particles escape forces.
  3. Boiling: Liquid to gas at boiling point. Particles move so fast that bubbles form everywhere.
  4. Freezing: Liquid to solid. Cooling slows particles; forces lock them in place.
  5. Condensation: Gas to liquid. Cooling slows particles; forces pull them together into drops.

Inference: More heat = more motion = easier state change (e.g., solid to liquid to gas). Brownian movement shows constant random hits.

Gas Laws

These describe how gases respond to changes in P, V, T, or amount (n). From kinetic theory: Pressure comes from particles hitting walls; higher T means faster hits.

Ideal and Real Gases

Ideal: Follow laws perfectly (no forces, tiny particles). Good at high T, low P.

Real: Deviate (e.g., attractions at low T reduce P; volume at high P reduces V).

Factors: High P (particles take space); low T (sticky forces); polar gases deviate more.
