1️⃣ Kinetic Theory of Matter (Intro)

Kinetic theory says all matter is made of tiny particles (atoms/molecules) that are always moving randomly. Their motion explains the different behavior of solids, liquids, and gases.

Particle model: solid (packed), liquid (close but mobile), gas (far apart, fast)

Key ideas (postulates)

  • Higher temperature → particles move faster → higher kinetic energy
  • Gas pressure is due to particle collisions with container walls
  • Collisions are elastic (no net energy loss)
  • Solids: vibration in fixed positions (strong forces)
  • Liquids: particles slide (moderate forces)
  • Gases: particles move freely (very weak forces)
🧠 One-line memory

More heat = faster particles = more pressure/expansion + easier state change.

🧫 Brownian Movement (Evidence)

Brownian movement is the random zig-zag movement of tiny particles (like smoke/dust/pollen) because invisible molecules of a fluid constantly collide with them.

Brownian motion: tiny particles jitter due to random molecular collisions

Why it supports kinetic theory: It shows particles of matter are in constant random motion.

🔥 Phenomena Supporting Kinetic Theory (State Changes)

State changes happen when heat changes particle speed enough to overcome (or strengthen) the forces holding them together.

Phase changes: melting, boiling/evaporation, condensation, freezing

Quick explanations

  • Melting: solid → liquid (particles gain energy, break fixed positions)
  • Evaporation: liquid → gas below bp (fast surface particles escape)
  • Boiling: liquid → gas at bp (bubbles form throughout liquid)
  • Freezing: liquid → solid (cooling slows particles, forces lock them)
  • Condensation: gas → liquid (cooling slows particles into droplets)

⚠️ Kelvin Reminder (very important)

  • Convert °C to K: K = °C + 273
  • For Charles and Gay-Lussac, always use Kelvin.

2️⃣ Gas Laws (with meaning + examples)

(i) Boyle’s Law

Statement: For a fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, pressure is inversely proportional to volume.

Formula: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂

Meaning: P ↑ → V ↓ (gas is compressed)

Boyle’s law graph: P–V curve (hyperbola)

Example

  • P₁ = 2 atm, V₁ = 500 cm³, P₂ = 5 atm
  • V₂ = (P₁V₁)/P₂ = (2×500)/5 = 200 cm³

(ii) Charles’s Law

Statement: For a fixed mass of gas at constant pressure, volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature (Kelvin).

Formula: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ (T in K)

Meaning: T ↑ → V ↑

Charles’s law graph: V vs T (straight line in Kelvin)

Example

  • V₁ = 200 mL at 27°C → T₁ = 300 K
  • T₂ = 127°C → 400 K
  • V₂ = 200 × (400/300) = 266.7 mL

(iii) Avogadro’s Law

Statement: At constant pressure and temperature, volume is directly proportional to number of moles (n).

Formula: V ∝ n (so V/n = constant)

At STP: 1 mole gas occupies 22.4 L

More moles (particles) → larger volume at same T and P

Example (STP)

  • V = 11.2 L → n = 11.2/22.4 = 0.5 mol

(iv) Gay-Lussac’s Law

Statement: At constant volume, pressure is directly proportional to Kelvin temperature.

Formula: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂

Meaning: T ↑ → P ↑ (faster collisions)

Gay-Lussac graph: P vs T (straight line in Kelvin)

Example

  • P₁ = 1 atm at 300 K, T₂ = 600 K
  • P₂ = 1 × (600/300) = 2 atm

(v) Graham’s Law of Diffusion

Statement: The rate of diffusion is inversely proportional to the square root of molar mass.

Formula: r₁/r₂ = √(MM₂/MM₁)

Meaning: lighter gas → faster diffusion

NH₃ diffuses faster than HCl → ring forms closer to HCl end

Example

  • MM(HCl)=36.5, MM(NH₃)=17
  • Rate NH₃/HCl = √(36.5/17) ≈ 1.46

(vi) Dalton’s Law of Partial Pressures

Statement: Total pressure of a mixture of non-reacting gases equals the sum of their partial pressures.

Formula: Ptotal = P₁ + P₂ + …

Total pressure is the sum of pressures each gas would exert alone

Example

  • O₂ = 0.21 atm, N₂ = 0.78 atm, others = 0.01 atm
  • Total = 1.00 atm

📐 Ideal Gas Equation (PV = nRT)

PV = nRT combines gas laws. Use it when you have P, V, T, and n (moles).

Ideal gas equation links P, V, n, T

Constants you should know

  • R = 0.0821 L·atm·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹ (when P in atm, V in L, T in K)
  • You can also use other R values if units change (but JAMB often uses 0.0821)

Example: n=0.5 mol, T=300 K, P=2 atm

V = (nRT)/P = (0.5×0.0821×300)/2 ≈ 6.16 L

⚠️ Unit check before solving

  • Temperature must be Kelvin
  • If you use R=0.0821, use L and atm

🧪 Vapour Density (VD) & Relative Molecular Mass (RMM)

Relationship: VD = RMM / 2 (vapour density compared to hydrogen gas, H₂).

Heavier gases (higher RMM) are denser (higher VD)

Examples

  • VD = 32 → RMM = 64
  • If RMM = 40 → VD = 20

🧊 Ideal vs Real Gases

Ideal gas: obeys gas laws perfectly (particles have no volume, no forces of attraction). Best assumption at high T and low P.

Real gas: deviates due to attraction between molecules and the finite volume of molecules.

Real gases deviate from ideal behavior, especially at high pressure and low temperature

When real gases deviate more

  • High pressure: molecules are close, volume matters
  • Low temperature: attractions become important (“stickiness”)
  • Polar gases often deviate more than non-polar gases

✅ Quick Revision (JAMB)

Must-know bullets

  • Pressure comes from gas molecules hitting container walls
  • Boyle: inverse P–V (T constant)
  • Charles: direct V–T (P constant; Kelvin)
  • Gay-Lussac: direct P–T (V constant; Kelvin)
  • Avogadro: V ∝ n (P and T constant)
  • PV=nRT for general problems
  • Graham: lighter gas diffuses faster
  • Dalton: total pressure = sum of partial pressures
  • VD = RMM/2
