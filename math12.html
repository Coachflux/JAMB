Locus in 2D Geometry (Easy Explanation for JAMB)

A locus is simply the path (or set of all points) that a moving point follows when it obeys a specific rule or condition. Think of it like "all the places where something can be if it must stay exactly 5 cm from a wall" — that path is the locus. In 2D (plane geometry), loci are usually straight lines, parallel lines, perpendicular bisectors, angle bisectors, or circles (or combinations of them). JAMB focuses on these four main types. I'll explain each one simply, with pictures (diagrams), then give you real JAMB-style examples with full solutions.

1. Locus Relating to Parallel Lines

Rule: Points at a fixed distance from a given straight line.

What it looks like: Two straight lines parallel to the given line, one on each side, exactly that distance away.

  • If the condition is "exactly 5 cm from line LM", the locus is two parallel lines 5 cm away (one above, one below LM).
  • If the condition is "equidistant from two parallel lines" (e.g. y=5 and y=3), the locus is the middle parallel line (y=4).

    • Easy way to remember: Imagine drawing with a compass set to 5 cm and sliding it along the line — you get two parallel lines.

    2. Locus Relating to Perpendicular Bisectors

    Rule: Points equidistant from two fixed points (say A and B).

    What it looks like: The perpendicular bisector of the line segment joining the two points (cuts the segment exactly in half at 90°).

  • Every point on this line is exactly the same distance from A and from B.

    • Easy construction: Find midpoint of AB, then draw line at 90° through it.

    3. Locus Relating to Angle Bisectors

    Rule: Points equidistant from two intersecting straight lines.

    What it looks like: The two angle bisectors of the angles formed by the two lines (they cross at 90° to each other).

    Easy way to remember: The point is "equally far" from both walls → it must lie on the bisector lines (like the centre line in a V-shaped road).

    4. Locus Relating to Circles

    Rule: Points at a fixed distance from one fixed point (the centre).

    What it looks like: A circle with that fixed point as centre and the fixed distance as radius.

    Easy way to remember: "All points exactly 4 cm from P" → just draw a circle radius 4 cm centred at P.

    10 Real JAMB Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

    Here are 10 actual/past JAMB-style questions (taken from real past papers and very similar ones) covering all four types. I grouped them and gave clear solutions.

    Circle Examples

    1. What is the locus of a distance 4 cm from a given point P?
    Step 1: The rule is points at fixed distance 4 cm from P.
    Step 2: Fixed distance from one point = circle.
    Step 3: Centre P, radius 4 cm.
    Answer: A circle of radius 4 cm
    2. The locus of all points having a distance of 1 unit from a fixed point O is
    Step 1: Fixed distance 1 unit from O.
    Step 2: That's the definition of a circle with centre O and radius 1.
    Answer: A circle with centre O and radius 1 unit

    Parallel Lines Examples

    3. The locus of a point which is 5 cm from the line LM is
    Step 1: Points at fixed distance 5 cm from line LM.
    Step 2: From a line, locus is two parallel lines, one on each side.
    Step 3: Each 5 cm away from LM.
    Answer: Pair of lines on opposite sides of LM and parallel to it, each distance 5 cm from LM
    4. Find the locus of point equidistant from two straight lines y – 5 = 0 and y – 3 = 0
    Step 1: y=5 and y=3 are parallel horizontal lines.
    Step 2: Equidistant from two parallels = middle line.
    Step 3: Distance to y=5 is |y-5|, to y=3 is |y-3|.
    Step 4: |y-5| = |y-3| → y=4 (middle).
    Answer: y – 4 = 0
    5. The locus of the points which is equidistant from the line PQ forms a
    Step 1: Equidistant from line PQ.
    Step 2: Fixed distance from a line = two parallel lines.
    Step 3: On both sides of PQ.
    Answer: Pair of parallel lines to PQ

    Perpendicular Bisector Examples

    6. The locus of a point P which is equidistant from two given points S and T is
    Step 1: Equidistant from S and T: PS = PT.
    Step 2: By definition, perpendicular bisector of ST.
    Answer: The perpendicular bisector of ST
    7. The locus of a point equidistant from two points P(6,2) and R(4,2) is a perpendicular bisector of PR passing through
    Step 1: Midpoint of P(6,2) and R(4,2) = ((6+4)/2, (2+2)/2) = (5,2)
    Step 2: PR is horizontal (y=2), so perpendicular is vertical x=5.
    Step 3: Passes through (5,2).
    Answer: (5,2)

    Angle Bisector Examples

    8. The locus of a point which moves so that it is equidistant from two intersecting straight lines is the
    Step 1: Equidistant from two intersecting lines.
    Step 2: Locus is the angle bisectors of the angles formed.
    Answer: Angle bisector of the two lines
    9. Find the locus of a particle which moves in the first quadrant so that it is equidistant from the lines x = 0 and y = 0
    Step 1: x=0 is y-axis, y=0 is x-axis, intersect at origin.
    Step 2: Equidistant: |x| = |y|.
    Step 3: In first quadrant, x = y (the bisector).
    Answer: The line x = y
    10. Two lines PQ and ST intersect at 75°. The locus of points equidistant from PQ and ST is
    Step 1: Two intersecting lines at 75°.
    Step 2: Equidistant locus = angle bisectors.
    Step 3: Bisects the 75° and the 105° angles (supplementary).
    Answer: The pair of angle bisectors of the angles formed at the intersection
