1️⃣ EQUILIBRIUM OF PARTICLES

What is Equilibrium? 🤔

An object is in equilibrium when the net force acting on it is zero. This means:

  • Static equilibrium: Object is at rest (velocity = 0)
  • Dynamic equilibrium: Object moves with constant velocity (no acceleration)

First Condition of Equilibrium

The vector sum of all forces must equal zero:

∑F⃗ = 0

In component form:
∑Fx = 0 and ∑Fy = 0

Triangle and Polygon of Forces

🔺 Triangle Law

If three forces acting at a point are in equilibrium, they can be represented by the three sides of a triangle taken in order.

📐 Polygon Law

For more than three forces in equilibrium, they form a closed polygon when drawn head-to-tail.

⚡ Lami's Theorem (The "CBT Shortcut")

When three forces act at a point and keep it in equilibrium:

F₁/sin α = F₂/sin β = F₃/sin γ

Where α, β, γ are the angles opposite to forces F₁, F₂, F₃

💡 JAMB Tip: This is the fastest way to solve three-force equilibrium problems without resolving components!

🔢 Example 1: Lami's Theorem Application

Question: A weight of 100 N is suspended by two strings making angles of 30° and 60° with the horizontal. Find the tensions in the strings.

Solution:
1. Sketch diagram: Weight acts downward, tensions T₁ and T₂ act upward at angles.

2. Identify angles between forces:
• Angle opposite to weight (100 N) = 180° - 30° - 60° = 90°
• Angle opposite to T₁ = 180° - 60° = 120°
• Angle opposite to T₂ = 180° - 30° = 150°

3. Apply Lami's Theorem:
100/sin 90° = T₁/sin 120° = T₂/sin 150°

4. Calculate:
• 100/1 = 100
• T₁ = 100 × sin 120° = 100 × 0.866 = 86.6 N
• T₂ = 100 × sin 150° = 100 × 0.5 = 50 N
Answers: T₁ = 86.6 N, T₂ = 50 N

🔢 Example 2: Resolution of Forces

Question: A 50 N force acts at 37° above the horizontal. Find its horizontal and vertical components.

Solution:
• Horizontal: Fx = 50 cos 37° = 50 × 0.8 = 40 N
• Vertical: Fy = 50 sin 37° = 50 × 0.6 = 30 N

Note: 37° is a standard JAMB angle where sin 37° ≈ 0.6, cos 37° ≈ 0.8
Answers: Fx = 40 N, Fy = 30 N
2️⃣ PRINCIPLES OF MOMENTS

Moment of a Force 🔄

The turning effect of a force about a point (pivot):

Moment = Force × Perpendicular distance
τ = F × d

Unit: Newton-meter (Nm)

Principle of Moments

For a body in equilibrium:

∑Clockwise moments = ∑Anticlockwise moments

Couple (Torque)

A pair of equal and opposite forces acting parallel to each other but not along the same line:

Torque = Force × Perpendicular distance between forces
τ = F × d

🔢 Example 3: Moments Principle

Question: A uniform meter rule of mass 0.5 kg is pivoted at the 40 cm mark. Where must a 2 N weight be placed to balance the rule horizontally? (g = 10 m/s²)

Solution:
1. Weight of rule acts at center (50 cm mark):
W = 0.5 × 10 = 5 N

2. Clockwise moment (from rule weight):
• Distance from pivot = 50 - 40 = 10 cm = 0.1 m
• Moment = 5 × 0.1 = 0.5 Nm

3. Anticlockwise moment needed = 0.5 Nm

4. Let x = distance from pivot to 2 N weight:
2 × x = 0.5
x = 0.25 m = 25 cm

5. Position: 40 - 25 = 15 cm mark
Answer: 15 cm mark
3️⃣ CONDITIONS FOR EQUILIBRIUM OF RIGID BODIES

Two Essential Conditions ⚖️

Condition Mathematical Expression Meaning
1st Condition (Translational) ∑Fx = 0 and ∑Fy = 0 No linear acceleration
2nd Condition (Rotational) ∑τ = 0 No angular acceleration

Resultant and Equilibrant

  • Resultant: The single force that produces the same effect as all forces combined
  • Equilibrant: The single force that brings a system to equilibrium (equal and opposite to the resultant)
Finding Resultant:
R = √(Fx² + Fy²)
tan θ = Fy/Fx

🔢 Example 4: Complete Equilibrium Problem

Question: A 6 m uniform beam weighing 200 N rests on two supports at its ends. A 300 N weight is placed 2 m from the left end. Find the reactions at the supports.

Solution:
1. 1st Condition: R_A + R_B = 200 + 300 = 500 N

2. 2nd Condition (take moments about A):
• Clockwise moments: (200 × 3) + (300 × 2) = 600 + 600 = 1200 Nm
• Anticlockwise moment: R_B × 6

6R_B = 1200
R_B = 200 N

3. From 1st condition:
R_A = 500 - 200 = 300 N
Answers: R_A = 300 N, R_B = 200 N
4️⃣ CENTER OF GRAVITY AND STABILITY

Center of Gravity (C.G.) 📍

The point where the entire weight of a body appears to act.

Types of Equilibrium

Type Description Example Energy State
Stable Returns to original position after slight displacement Bowl at bottom Minimum potential energy
Unstable Moves away from original position after displacement Balanced cone on tip Maximum potential energy
Neutral Stays in new position after displacement Ball on flat surface Constant potential energy

Stability Factors

  1. Base area: Larger base = more stable
  2. Height of C.G.: Lower C.G. = more stable
  3. Weight of object: Heavier = more stable

🎯 JAMB Exam Tips

Common Question Types:

  1. Three-force problems → Use Lami's Theorem (fastest method)
  2. Beam balancing → Use Principle of Moments
  3. Inclined planes → Resolve weight: mgsinθ (parallel), mgcosθ (perpendicular)
  4. Pulley systems → Tension is constant throughout the string

Quick Checklist for Equilibrium Problems:

  • Draw clear force diagram
  • Identify all forces (tension, weight, normal, friction)
  • Check if Lami's theorem applies (3 forces, concurrent)
  • Resolve forces if more than 3 or not concurrent
  • Apply moment equation if there are pivots
  • Check both conditions for rigid bodies

📝 Practice Questions

Q1: Two forces 3 N and 4 N act at right angles. What is the magnitude of their equilibrant?

Answer: 5 N (forms 3-4-5 right triangle)

Q2: A uniform rod of length 2 m and weight 50 N is pivoted at one end. What force is needed at the other end to keep it horizontal?

Answer: 25 N (moment about pivot: 50 × 1 = F × 2, so F = 25 N)

Q3: Which of the following is NOT a condition for stable equilibrium?

A) Low center of gravity
B) Wide base
C) High center of gravity
D) Heavy base

Answer: C) High center of gravity (this causes instability)
