BIOLOGY

🌍 Chapter 13: Distribution of Organisms

Abiotic + Biotic factors, measuring tools, practice Q&A, and mnemonics

🎯 Overview

Distribution of organisms refers to where living things are found and why they are found there.

Different biomes (habitats) support different organisms

Two Main Categories

  • Abiotic factors (non-living)
  • Biotic factors (living interactions)

🔵 Part A: Abiotic Factors (Non-Living)

Definition: Abiotic factors are the physical and chemical (non-living) conditions of the environment that affect survival, growth, and distribution of organisms.

Abiotic conditions shape habitats (e.g., deserts vs rainforests)

1. Temperature

AspectDetails
EffectAffects metabolic rate, enzyme activity, and survival
Distribution patternTropical organisms → warm regions; Arctic organisms → cold regions; migration to avoid extremes
AdaptationsFur/feathers; hibernation/aestivation; cold-blooded vs warm-blooded

Key Rule

  • Temperature drops about 6.5°C per 1000m ascent (lapse rate)

Altitude creates vegetation zones on mountains

6. Salinity

7. Turbidity (Water Cloudiness)

Definition: Turbidity is the cloudiness of water caused by suspended particles, reducing light penetration.

Secchi disc: used to measure turbidity/water clarity

AspectDetails
EffectBlocks sunlight → reduces photosynthesis in deeper water
Distribution patternClear lakes: deeper photosynthesis zone; estuaries: limited plant growth
MeasurementSecchi disc lowered until it becomes invisible

8. pH (Acidity/Alkalinity)

Definition: pH measures how acidic or alkaline an environment is, affecting enzymes and nutrient availability.

pH scale: acidic (<7), neutral (7), alkaline (>7)

9. Edaphic Factors (Soil Conditions)

Definition: Edaphic factors are soil properties (texture, pH, nutrients, depth, mineral content, topography) that affect plant growth and therefore animal distribution too.

Soil texture (sand/silt/clay) affects drainage and plant types

Factor Effect on Distribution
Soil texture Sandy → drainage (cacti); Clay → waterlogging (rice paddies)
Soil pH Acidic → conifers; Neutral → many crops
Soil nutrients Fertile alluvial soils → dense vegetation/agriculture
Soil depth Shallow → grasslands; Deep → forests
Topography Slope affects drainage and erosion
Mineral content Laterite → savanna; Loamy → crop fields

🟢 Part B: Measuring Abiotic Factors (JAMB Focus)

Definition: Measuring abiotic factors means using instruments to record environmental conditions that influence organism distribution.

Environmental instruments help measure abiotic factors

Equipment Measures How to Use
Thermometer Temperature Place in shade, wait, read scale
Rain gauge Rainfall Collect in open area; read mm collected
Secchi disc Turbidity Lower until pattern disappears; note depth
Hygrometer Relative humidity Wet-bulb vs dry-bulb temperature difference
Wind vane / Anemometer Wind direction / speed Vane points to wind source; cups rotate for speed
pH meter pH Calibrate, dip probe, read display
Soil testing kit Soil pH, NPK Mix soil with water; compare color change

🧠 Equipment Mnemonic:

"TRH-SA" → Thermometer, Rain gauge, Hygrometer, Secchi disc, Anemometer

🟡 Part C: Biotic Factors (Living)

Definition: Biotic factors are living interactions between organisms that affect survival and determine where organisms can live.

Biotic interactions: competition, predation, parasitism, mutualism

Biotic Factor Explanation Examples
Competition Organisms compete for limited resources Lions vs hyenas for prey; oak vs maple for sunlight; male seals for territory; maize for nutrients
Predation One organism kills and eats another Lion → wildebeest; snake → mouse; hawk → rabbit; frog → insect
Parasitism One benefits at the expense of another Tapeworm in humans; ticks on cattle; mistletoe on trees; Plasmodium in blood
Mutualism Both organisms benefit Bees & flowers; Rhizobium & legumes; oxpecker & rhino; lichens
Human activities Actions that alter habitats Deforestation; urbanization; pesticides; pollution; invasive species (water hyacinth)

Mutualism example: Rhizobium bacteria in legume root nodules

⚡ Exam Tips

  • Know the equipment: Thermometer, Rain gauge, Secchi disc
  • Always link factor → adaptation (how organisms cope)
  • Human activities: know clear examples (deforestation, pollution, invasive species)
  • Altitude lapse rate: 6.5°C per 1000m
  • Edaphic shortcut: T.R.O.P.H.I.C (Texture, pH, Rock type, Organic matter, Parent material, Humus, Ion exchange, Climate/Topography)

🎯 Practice Questions (With Answers)

1) Coral reefs distribution

Coral reefs thrive in shallow, clear, warm waters

Answer: Coral reefs need shallow water (sunlight for zooxanthellae), clear water (low turbidity), and warm tropical temperatures (20–28°C) for metabolism and CaCO₃ deposition.

2) Desert plants reduced leaves

Spines = reduced leaves to reduce water loss

Answer: Reduced leaves lower surface area and reduce transpiration. Thick cuticle and sunken stomata also reduce water loss in dry conditions.

3) Measuring turbidity (Secchi disc)

Answer: Lower the Secchi disc until it disappears; record that depth. Shallow disappearance = high turbidity; deep disappearance = clear water.

4) Human activities reducing forests

Deforestation reduces forest habitats and distribution

Answer: Deforestation, urbanization, agricultural expansion (also: pollution, bush burning, mining).

5) Freshwater fish die in marine water

Osmosis causes water/salt movement across membranes

Answer: Sudden change causes osmotic shock. Freshwater fish (body saltier than water) aren’t adapted to salty water; they lose water and gain salt too fast → dehydration and organ failure.
