📚 Chapter 23: Light Energy

🎯 Learning Objectives: Distinguish between luminous and non-luminous objects, identify natural and artificial sources of light, use the wave equation v = fλ, explain formation of shadows and eclipses, understand pin-hole camera principles, and solve magnification problems.

1️⃣ Sources of Light ☀️

Natural vs Artificial Sources

Natural Sources Artificial Sources
Sun (primary source) Electric bulbs
Stars Fluorescent tubes
Fireflies (bioluminescence) Candles
Lightning LEDs (Light Emitting Diodes)
Volcanic lava Lasers
💡 Key Point: The Sun is the ultimate natural source of light for Earth. Artificial sources require energy input (electricity or chemical energy) to produce light.

Luminous vs Non-Luminous Objects

  • Luminous Objects: Produce their own light (e.g., Sun, stars, electric bulb, firefly, candle flame)
  • Non-Luminous Objects: Do not produce their own light but reflect/scatter light from other sources (e.g., Moon, table, human body, planets)
⚠️ Important JAMB Trap: The Moon is NON-LUMINOUS—it only reflects sunlight! Many students make this mistake in JAMB.

2️⃣ Propagation of Light 💡

Speed, Frequency, and Wavelength Relationship

Light travels as an electromagnetic wave. The fundamental relationship is:

v = f × λ

Where:
• v = velocity of light (speed)
• f = frequency (Hz)
• λ = wavelength (m)

Key Values to Memorize

Medium Speed of Light
Vacuum/Air c = 3.0 × 10⁸ m/s
Water 2.25 × 10⁸ m/s
Glass 2.0 × 10⁸ m/s

🔢 Calculation 1: Wave Equation

Question: A light wave has frequency 6.0 × 10¹⁴ Hz. Calculate its wavelength in vacuum.

Using c = fλ:
3.0 × 10⁸ = 6.0 × 10¹⁴ × λ
λ = (3.0 × 10⁸)/(6.0 × 10¹⁴)
λ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁷ m = 500 nm
Answer: 500 nm

3️⃣ Formation of Shadows and Eclipses 🌑

Shadows are formed when light travels in straight lines and is blocked by an opaque object.

Parts of a Shadow

  • Umbra: Total darkness (complete shadow)
  • Penumbra: Partial darkness (partial shadow)

Eclipses (Astronomical Shadows)

Solar Eclipse Lunar Eclipse
Moon between Sun and Earth Earth between Sun and Moon
Occurs during daytime (new moon) Occurs during nighttime (full moon)
Sun is blocked Moon enters Earth's shadow

Types of Solar Eclipse

  1. Total: Observer is in Moon's umbra
  2. Partial: Observer is in Moon's penumbra
  3. Annular: Moon is too far to completely cover Sun (ring of fire visible)

Types of Lunar Eclipse

  1. Total: Moon completely enters Earth's umbra
  2. Partial: Moon partially enters Earth's umbra

4️⃣ The Pin-Hole Camera 📷

Principle: Light travels in straight lines. When light passes through a small hole (aperture), it forms an inverted image on the opposite screen.

Key Formula:
(Image height)/(Object height) = (Image distance)/(Object distance)

Or: hᵢ/hₒ = v/u = m (magnification)

Where:
• hᵢ = image height
• hₒ = object height
• v = image distance (pinhole to screen)
• u = object distance (pinhole to object)

Characteristics of Pin-Hole Camera Images

  • ✅ Real
  • ✅ Inverted (upside down)
  • ✅ Laterally inverted (left-right reversed)
  • ✅ Magnification depends on distances

🔢 Calculation 2: Pin-Hole Camera

Question: An object 2m tall is placed 4m from a pin-hole camera. If the screen is 0.5m from the pinhole, calculate the height of the image formed.

Using hᵢ/hₒ = v/u:
hᵢ/2 = 0.5/4
hᵢ = 2 × (0.5/4) = 2 × 0.125 = 0.25 m = 25 cm
Answer: 25 cm (or 0.25 m)

🔢 Calculation 3: Finding Object Height

Question: A pin-hole camera has a screen 10cm from the pinhole. An object 5m away produces an image 2cm tall. Calculate: (a) The height of the object, (b) The magnification.

(a) Using hᵢ/hₒ = v/u:
2/hₒ = 10/500 (convert 5m to 500cm)
hₒ = (2 × 500)/10 = 100 cm = 1 m

(b) Magnification:
m = hᵢ/hₒ = 2/100 = 0.02
OR m = v/u = 10/500 = 0.02
Answer: (a) 1 m, (b) 0.02

🔢 Calculation 4: Finding Screen Distance

Question: A pin-hole camera is used to photograph an object 6m high, placed 12m away. If the image formed is 10cm high, how far is the screen from the pinhole?

Using hᵢ/hₒ = v/u:
0.1/6 = v/12 (convert 10cm to 0.1m)
v = (0.1 × 12)/6 = 0.2 m = 20 cm
Answer: 20 cm (or 0.2 m)

🎯 JAMB Exam Tips

  1. Remember: Light travels fastest in vacuum (3 × 10⁸ m/s) and slows down in denser media

  2. Common trap: The Moon is NOT a luminous object—it's non-luminous!

  3. Units matter: Always convert to consistent units (meters) before calculating

  4. Image orientation: Pin-hole camera images are always inverted (upside down and left-right reversed)

  5. Eclipse safety: Never look directly at a solar eclipse—it can damage your eyes!

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Calling the Moon a luminous object (it only reflects sunlight!)
  • ❌ Forgetting that pin-hole camera images are inverted
  • ❌ Mixing up solar eclipse (moon between sun-earth) with lunar eclipse (earth between sun-moon)
  • ❌ Using wrong units in calculations (cm vs m)
  • ❌ Forgetting that light slows down in denser media (water, glass)
