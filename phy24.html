📚 Chapter 24: Reflection of Light at Plane and Curved Surfaces

🎯 Learning Objectives: Interpret the laws of reflection, illustrate image formation by plane, concave and convex mirrors, apply the mirror formula, determine linear magnification, and apply laws to instruments like periscope, kaleidoscope, and sextant.

1️⃣ Laws of Reflection 🔬

What is Reflection?

Reflection is the bouncing back of light rays when they hit a smooth surface (mirror). The surface that reflects light is called a mirror.

The Two Laws of Reflection

  1. First Law: The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal all lie in the same plane.

  2. Second Law: The angle of incidence (i) equals the angle of reflection (r).
    i = r

Key Terms

  • Incident Ray: The light ray approaching the mirror
  • Reflected Ray: The light ray bouncing off the mirror
  • Normal: Imaginary line perpendicular to the mirror surface at the point where light hits
  • Angle of Incidence (i): Angle between incident ray and normal
  • Angle of Reflection (r): Angle between reflected ray and normal

2️⃣ Plane Mirrors (Flat Mirrors) 🪞

Characteristics of Images in Plane Mirrors

  1. Virtual (cannot be projected on a screen)
  2. Erect/Upright (not inverted)
  3. Same size as the object
  4. Laterally inverted (left appears right, right appears left)
  5. Image distance = Object distance from mirror

Lateral Inversion Explained

When you look in a mirror, your right hand appears as the left hand of your image. This is why "AMBULANCE" is written in reverse on emergency vehicles—drivers see it correctly in their rear-view mirrors.

Multiple Reflections

When two plane mirrors are placed at an angle θ to each other:

n = (360°/θ) - 1

Where n = number of images formed

Special Cases

Angle (θ) Number of Images (n)
Parallel (0°) Infinite images
Perpendicular (90°) n = (360/90) - 1 = 3 images
60° n = (360/60) - 1 = 5 images

🔢 Calculation 1: Multiple Images

Question: Two plane mirrors are inclined at 72°. Calculate the number of images formed.

Using n = (360°/θ) - 1:
n = (360°/72°) - 1
n = 5 - 1 = 4 images
Answer: 4 images

🔢 Calculation 2: Distance in Plane Mirror

Question: A man stands 2m from a plane mirror. He walks 0.5m towards the mirror. Calculate: (a) Initial distance between man and image, (b) New distance after walking.

(a) Initial:
• Object distance = 2m
• Image distance = 2m (behind mirror)
• Distance between man and image = 2 + 2 = 4m

(b) After walking 0.5m:
• New object distance = 2 - 0.5 = 1.5m
• New image distance = 1.5m
• Distance between man and image = 1.5 + 1.5 = 3m
Answer: (a) 4m, (b) 3m

🔢 Calculation 3: Minimum Mirror Height

Question: A man is 1.8m tall with eyes 10cm below the top of his head. Calculate the minimum mirror length required to see his full image.

• Distance from eyes to top = 10cm = 0.1m
• Distance from eyes to feet = 1.8 - 0.1 = 1.7m

Mirror must cover:
• From: 0.1/2 = 0.05m above eye level
• To: 1.7/2 = 0.85m below eye level

Minimum mirror height = 0.05 + 0.85 = 0.9m

Alternative: Height/2 = 1.8/2 = 0.9m
Answer: 90 cm (or 0.9 m)

3️⃣ Curved Mirrors (Spherical Mirrors) 🔭

Types of Curved Mirrors

Concave Mirror (Converging) Convex Mirror (Diverging)
Caved inward (inside of spoon) Curved outward (back of spoon)
Reflecting surface: Inner/concave side Reflecting surface: Outer/convex side
Converges parallel rays to focus Diverges parallel rays from focus behind
Uses: Shaving mirrors, headlights, telescopes Uses: Rear-view mirrors, security mirrors

Key Terms for Curved Mirrors

Term Symbol Definition
Pole (Vertex) P Center point Center point of the mirror surface
Centre of Curvature C Center of the sphere from which mirror was "cut"
Radius of Curvature R Distance from pole to center of curvature
Principal Axis — Line passing through P and C
Principal Focus F Point where parallel rays converge (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex)
Focal Length f Distance from pole to focus: f = R/2

Sign Convention (Cartesian Convention)

  • All distances measured from the pole (P)
  • Real is positive, virtual is negative (for object/image distances)
  • Distances measured against light direction are negative
  • Distances measured with light direction are positive
  • Concave mirror: f and R are negative
  • Convex mirror: f and R are positive

🔢 Calculation 4: Finding Focal Length from Radius

Question: A concave mirror has radius of curvature 60 cm. Calculate its focal length.

Using f = R/2:
f = 60/2 = 30 cm

For concave mirror: f = -30 cm
Answer: 30 cm (or -30 cm using sign convention)

🔢 Calculation 5: Finding Radius from Focal Length

Question: A convex mirror has focal length 20 cm. Calculate its radius of curvature.

Using R = 2f:
R = 2 × 20 = 40 cm

For convex mirror: R = +40 cm, f = +20 cm
Answer: 40 cm

4️⃣ Ray Diagrams for Curved Mirrors 📐

Rules for Drawing Ray Diagrams - Concave Mirrors

  1. Ray parallel to principal axis → Passes through focus after reflection
  2. Ray passing through focus → Emerges parallel to principal axis
  3. Ray passing through center of curvature → Retraces its path after reflection
  4. Ray striking the pole → Reflects at equal angle to principal axis

Rules for Drawing Ray Diagrams - Convex Mirrors

  1. Ray parallel to principal axis → Appears to come from focus behind mirror
  2. Ray heading toward focus → Emerges parallel to principal axis
  3. Ray heading toward center of curvature → Retraces its path after reflection

Image Formation Summary - Concave Mirror

Object Position Image Position Nature Size Orientation
At infinity At F Real, point Diminished Inverted
Beyond C Between C and F Real Diminished Inverted
At C At C Real Same size Inverted
Between C and F Beyond C Real Magnified Inverted
At F At infinity Real, highly magnified — —
Between F and P Behind mirror Virtual Magnified Erect

Image Formation Summary - Convex Mirror

  • Position: Between P and F (behind mirror)
  • Nature: Virtual
  • Size: Diminished
  • Orientation: Erect/Upright
  • Always same result regardless of object position!

5️⃣ The Mirror Formula & Magnification 🧮

Mirror Formula:
1/f = 1/u + 1/v

Alternative forms:
1/v = 1/f - 1/u
uv = f(u + v)

Where:
• f = focal length (negative for concave, positive for convex)
• u = object distance (always negative for real objects)
• v = image distance (negative for virtual, positive for real)
Linear Magnification (m):
m = Image height/Object height = hᵢ/hₒ = -v/u

Interpretation:
• |m| > 1: Image is magnified (larger than object)
• |m| < 1: Image is diminished (smaller than object)
• |m| = 1: Image is same size as object
• m is positive: Image is erect (virtual for mirrors)
• m is negative: Image is inverted (real for mirrors)

🔢 Calculation 6: Concave Mirror - Real Image

Question: An object is placed 30 cm from a concave mirror of focal length 10 cm. Find the image position and magnification.

Given: u = -30 cm, f = -10 cm (concave)

Using 1/f = 1/u + 1/v:
1/(-10) = 1/(-30) + 1/v
-1/10 + 1/30 = 1/v
(-3 + 1)/30 = 1/v
-2/30 = 1/v
v = -15 cm

Magnification:
m = -v/u = -(-15)/(-30) = -15/30 = -0.5
Answer: Image at 15 cm in front of mirror, real, inverted, half the size (m = -0.5)

🔢 Calculation 7: Convex Mirror

Question: A convex mirror has radius of curvature 40 cm. An object is placed 20 cm from the mirror. Find the image position and magnification.

Given: R = +40 cm, so f = R/2 = +20 cm
u = -20 cm

Using 1/f = 1/u + 1/v:
1/20 = 1/(-20) + 1/v
1/v = 1/20 + 1/20 = 2/20 = 1/10
v = +10 cm

Magnification:
m = -v/u = -(10)/(-20) = +0.5
Answer: Image at 10 cm behind mirror, virtual, erect, m = +0.5

🔢 Calculation 8: Concave Mirror with Virtual Image

Question: An object is placed 10 cm from a concave mirror of focal length 15 cm. Calculate the image position and magnification.

Given: u = -10 cm, f = -15 cm (concave)

Using 1/f = 1/u + 1/v:
1/(-15) = 1/(-10) + 1/v
1/v = -1/15 + 1/10 = (-2 + 3)/30 = 1/30
v = +30 cm

Magnification:
m = -v/u = -(30)/(-10) = +3
Answer: Image at 30 cm behind mirror, virtual, erect, magnified 3×

🔢 Calculation 9: Finding Focal Length from Magnification

Question: An object placed 20 cm from a concave mirror produces a real image twice the size of the object. Find the focal length.

Given: u = -20 cm, Real image means m = -2

Using m = -v/u:
-2 = -v/(-20)
v/20 = -2
v = -40 cm

Using mirror formula:
1/f = 1/(-20) + 1/(-40) = -2/40 - 1/40 = -3/40
f = -40/3 = -13.33 cm
Answer: 13.33 cm (negative confirms concave mirror)

🔢 Calculation 10: Object at Center of Curvature

Question: An object 3 cm tall is placed at the center of curvature of a concave mirror with R = 40 cm. Find the image position, nature, and size.

Given: R = -40 cm, f = -20 cm
u = -40 cm (at C), hₒ = 3 cm

Using 1/f = 1/u + 1/v:
1/(-20) = 1/(-40) + 1/v
1/v = -1/20 + 1/40 = (-2 + 1)/40 = -1/40
v = -40 cm

Magnification: m = -v/u = -(-40)/(-40) = -1

Image height: hᵢ = m × hₒ = -1 × 3 = -3 cm
Answer: Image at 40 cm in front of mirror, real, inverted, 3 cm tall (same size)

🔢 Calculation 11: Finding Object Position for Specific Image

Question: Where should an object be placed in front of a concave mirror (f = 20 cm) to produce a real image magnified 3 times?

Given: f = -20 cm, Real image means m = -3

Using m = -v/u = -3:
v/u = 3, so v = 3u

Using mirror formula:
1/(-20) = 1/u + 1/(3u)
-1/20 = (3 + 1)/(3u) = 4/(3u)
3u = -80
u = -26.67 cm
Answer: Object at 26.67 cm (or 26⅔ cm) in front of mirror

🔢 Calculation 12: Speed of Image in Plane Mirror

Question: A boy runs towards a plane mirror at 2 m/s. Calculate the speed at which his image approaches him.

• Boy approaches mirror at 2 m/s
• Image approaches mirror at 2 m/s (same speed)
• Relative speed between boy and image = 2 + 2 = 4 m/s
Answer: 4 m/s

6️⃣ Applications of Reflection 🛠️

1. Periscope

  • Uses two plane mirrors at 45° angles
  • Allows viewing over obstacles
  • Used in submarines and trenches

2. Kaleidoscope

  • Uses three plane mirrors at 60° to each other
  • Creates beautiful symmetrical patterns
  • Based on multiple reflections

3. Sextant

  • Navigation instrument using mirrors
  • Measures angle between celestial object and horizon
  • Uses double reflection principle

4. Other Applications

  • Concave mirrors: Solar cookers, satellite dishes, makeup mirrors, ophthalmoscopes
  • Convex mirrors: Security mirrors, vehicle side mirrors ("Objects in mirror are closer than they appear")

🔢 Calculation 13: Solar Cooker Application

Question: A solar cooker uses a concave mirror with R = 2 m. Where should the cooking pot be placed for maximum heating?

For maximum heating, pot must be at the focus where all parallel sun rays converge.

Using f = R/2:
f = 200/2 = 100 cm = 1 m
Answer: 1 m from the mirror (at the focus)

📋 Quick Revision Tips 🎯

✅ Memory Aids

  • "VIRTUAL IS VIP" — Virtual images are In Plane and convex mirrors (always erect)

  • Sign Convention: "Real is Positive, Virtual is Negative" (for distances in some conventions)

  • Concave: f is negative (it "caves in" the light, so negative)

  • Convex: f is positive (it "vexes" you by being positive)

⚠️ Common JAMB Traps

  1. Forgetting sign conventions — Always check if it's concave or convex first
  2. Confusing R and f — Remember f = R/2
  3. Real vs Virtual images — Real images can be projected on screens; virtual cannot
  4. Magnification interpretation — |m| gives size ratio; sign gives orientation
