Definition: Nutrition

Nutrition is the process by which living organisms obtain and utilize food for energy, growth, repair, and maintenance.

a. Modes of Nutrition

i. Autotrophic (Self-feeding / Producer mode)

Organisms synthesize their own food from inorganic substances using energy.

Examples: Green plants (flowering: maize, bean; non-flowering: algae like Spirogyra, ferns).

ii. Heterotrophic (Dependent / Consumer mode)

Organisms obtain ready-made organic food from external sources.

Examples: Animals (man, sheep), fungi, some plants (Loranthus).

Comparison: Autotrophs are independent producers; heterotrophs are dependent consumers.

b. Types of Nutrition (Mainly heterotrophic types with other/common alternative names)

Holozoic (Holophytic in older texts; ingestion of solid food)

Involves ingestion of complex solid/liquid food, internal digestion.

Subtypes: Herbivores (plant-eaters), Carnivores (meat-eaters), Omnivores (both).

Examples: Sheep (herbivore), man (omnivore).

Parasitic (Parasitism mode)

Absorb nutrients from living host (host harmed).

Examples: Tapeworm, roundworm (in gut), Loranthus (on plants).

Saprophytic (Saprotrophic / Saprobiontic nutrition)

Feed on dead/decaying organic matter (external digestion).

Examples: Rhizopus (bread mould), mushroom (on decaying wood).

Carnivorous (Insectivorous / Predatory in plants)

Trap and digest living prey (usually insects).

Examples: Sundew (sticky leaves), bladderwort (suction traps in water).

c. Plant Nutrition

i. Photosynthesis

  • Light reaction: Produces ATP, NADPH, O‚ÇÇ (water splitting).
  • Dark reaction: CO‚ÇÇ fixed into glucose (Calvin cycle).

Photosynthesis Experiments (Starch Test ‚Äì Key Evidence):

  • Aim: Prove starch formation and factor necessity.
  • Procedure: Destarch plant (48 hrs dark) ‚Üí expose to test conditions ‚Üí boil leaf ‚Üí ethanol ‚Üí iodine test (blue-black = starch).
  • Results: Variegated leaf (starch only in green parts), covered part (no starch), CO‚ÇÇ-free (no starch).

ii. Chemosynthesis (Chemoautotrophic nutrition)

Use chemical energy (e.g., oxidation of NH‚ÇÉ, H‚ÇÇS) to make food (no light).

Examples: Nitrifying bacteria, sulphur bacteria.

Comparison: Photosynthesis (light energy); chemosynthesis (chemical energy).

iii. Mineral Requirements

Macro-nutrients: N, P, K, Ca, Mg, S.

Micro-nutrients: Fe, Mn, Zn, Cu, B, Mo, Cl.

Deficiency Symptoms (key JAMB ones):

  • Nitrogen: Chlorosis (yellow older leaves), stunted growth.
  • Phosphorus: Purple/dark leaves, poor roots.
  • Potassium: Scorched/brown leaf edges, weak stems.
  • Magnesium: Interveinal chlorosis (yellow between green veins).
  • Iron: Chlorosis in young leaves (green veins).

d. Animal Nutrition

i. Classes of Food Substances & Sources/Importance/Deficiency

  • Carbohydrates: Energy (rice, yam). Deficiency: Fatigue.
  • Proteins: Growth/repair (meat, beans). Deficiency: Kwashiorkor.
  • Fats & Oils: Reserve energy (oils, nuts). Deficiency: Dry skin.
  • Vitamins: e.g., C (fruits) ‚Üí scurvy; D ‚Üí rickets.
  • Mineral Salts: e.g., Fe ‚Üí anaemia; Ca ‚Üí weak bones.
  • Water: Transport/solvent.

Balanced diet: Prevents deficiencies, supports health.

ii. Food Tests

  • Starch: Iodine ‚Üí blue-black.
  • Reducing sugar: Benedict's ‚Üí brick-red.
  • Protein: Biuret ‚Üí violet.
  • Fat: Sudan III ‚Üí red / grease spot.

iii. Mammalian Tooth

Structure: Enamel, dentine, pulp, cement.

Types: Incisors (cut), Canines (tear), Premolars/Molars (crush/grind).

Dental Formulae:

  • Man: 2:1:2:3 / 2:1:2:3 (32 teeth).
  • Sheep: 0:0:3:3 / 3:1:3:3 (32 teeth).
  • Dog: 3:1:4:2 / 3:1:4:3 (42 teeth).

iv. Mammalian Alimentary Canal

Mouth (saliva/amylase) ‚Üí Oesophagus ‚Üí Stomach (pepsin/HCl) ‚Üí Small intestine (enzymes, absorption via villi) ‚Üí Large intestine (water) ‚Üí Anus.

Accessories: Liver (bile), Gall bladder, Pancreas (trypsin, lipase, amylase).

v. Nutrition Process

  • Ingestion: Food intake.
  • Digestion: Carbs ‚Üí glucose; Proteins ‚Üí amino acids; Fats ‚Üí fatty acids + glycerol.
  • Absorption: Into blood (ileum).
  • Assimilation: Use by cells.
  • Egestion: Waste out.
