Structural, Functional, and Behavioural Adaptations of Organisms

Adaptations are features (structural, functional, or behavioural) that enable organisms to survive, reproduce, and thrive in their specific environment and way of life.

1. Adaptive Colouration and Its Functions

Adaptive colouration involves colours or patterns that improve survival.

Countershading (Cryptic/Concealing Colouration)

Darker dorsal (upper) side blends with environment from above; lighter ventral (lower) side blends with light/sky from below ‚Üí reduces visibility to predators.

Examples: Fish (sharks, herring ‚Äî dark back, silvery belly), toads (dark upper body, pale underside), snakes (many arboreal species).

Key benefit: Makes silhouette hard to see from above or below.

Warning Colouration (Aposematic Colouration)

Bright, conspicuous colours signal toxicity, bad taste, or danger ‚Üí predators learn to avoid.

Examples: Poisonous mushrooms (bright red/orange/yellow caps), poison dart frogs (vivid blue/yellow/black patterns).

Key benefit: Predator avoidance through learned association.

Mimicry

An organism resembles another organism (or object) for protection, food, or mating.

Batesian Mimicry ‚Äî Harmless species mimics harmful one.

Examples: Hoverflies mimic bees/wasps (yellow-black stripes, no sting); non-venomous king snake mimics venomous coral snake ("Red touch yellow, kill a fellow").

M√ºllerian Mimicry ‚Äî Multiple harmful species share similar warning patterns.

Examples: Monarch and viceroy butterflies (both toxic, similar orange-black wings); wasps and bees (both stinging, yellow-black bands).

2. Behavioural Adaptations in Social Animals

Behavioural adaptations are actions/responses that enhance survival. Social animals show division of labour in colonies.

Castes in Social Insects (e.g. Termites, Bees, Ants)

Reproductives (King & Queen) ‚Äî Found new colonies; queen lays eggs, king mates.

Workers ‚Äî Sterile; forage, feed colony, care for young, build/repair nest (most numerous).

Soldiers ‚Äî Sterile with large jaws/chemical defences; protect colony from invaders.

Other behaviours: Communication (bee waggle dance for food location), cooperation, group defence.

3. Structural Adaptations in Organisms

Physical features suited to environment/way of life.

Obtaining Food

Birds: Eagle (hooked beak + talons for tearing), parrot (strong curved beak for seeds), heron (long pointed beak for fish), duck (flat beak for filtering).

Insects: Mosquito (piercing/sucking proboscis for blood), butterfly/moth (long coiled proboscis for nectar).

Protection and Defence

Stick insects ‚Äî Resemble twigs/leaves (camouflage + mimicry).

Praying mantis ‚Äî Body blends with plants; raptorial forelegs grasp prey (camouflage + ambush).

Toad ‚Äî Warty skin with poison glands; dull colour for camouflage.

Securing Mates

Agama lizards ‚Äî Males develop bright red heads in breeding season to attract females/intimidate rivals.

Birds ‚Äî Peacock displays elaborate, colourful tail feathers during courtship.

Regulating Body Temperature

Reptiles (e.g., lizards) ‚Äî Scales reduce water loss; behavioural basking in sun.

Birds ‚Äî Feathers insulate, trap air; waterproofing.

Mammals ‚Äî Fur/hair insulates; sweating/panting for cooling.

Conserving Water

Plants ‚Äî Spines (cactus) reduce transpiration surface; thick cuticle, sunken stomata.

Mammals ‚Äî Thick skin/fur minimizes loss; efficient kidneys (concentrated urine); nocturnal habits.

4. Additional Behavioural Examples

Basking in Lizards ‚Äî Ectotherms sit in sun to raise body temperature.

Territorial Behaviour ‚Äî Defend resources (e.g., birds sing, lions roar) under scarce conditions.

Hibernation ‚Äî Reduced metabolism in cold seasons (e.g., bats, some mammals) to save energy.

Aestivation ‚Äî Dormancy in hot/dry seasons (e.g., snails seal shell, lungfish burrow) to survive drought.

5. Evolution of Adaptations

Adaptations evolve gradually through natural selection (Darwin's mechanism):

  • Variation in population (mutations, recombination).
  • Environmental pressures select favourable traits.
  • Individuals with advantageous traits have higher fitness ‚Üí survive/reproduce more ‚Üí traits become common.

Examples:

Peppered Moth (Industrial Melanism) ‚Äî Light form camouflaged on lichen-covered trees pre-industry; dark form survived better on polluted dark trees ‚Üí frequency increased.

Darwin's Finches (Gal√°pagos) ‚Äî Different beak shapes evolved for varied food (seeds, insects) ‚Üí adaptive radiation.

Antibiotic resistance in bacteria; lactose tolerance in human populations.

Key Point for JAMB: All adaptations (structural like beaks/spines, functional like poison glands, behavioural like mimicry/basking) arise via natural selection favouring traits that improve survival/reproduction in specific habitats.
