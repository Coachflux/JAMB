📈 Chapter 17: Ecology of Populations

Population density + competition + population factors + succession (JAMB-ready)

🎯 Overview

Population ecology studies how population size, density, and distribution change over time, and what factors affect them.

Population size changes with births, deaths, immigration, and emigration

Key JAMB Targets

  • Calculate population density
  • Explain overcrowding + effects
  • Differentiate intra- vs inter-specific competition
  • Relate competition to succession and niche differentiation
  • Explain biotic + abiotic factors affecting population size
  • Trace primary vs secondary succession to climax community

1️⃣ Population Density & Overcrowding

Population Density

Definition: Population density is the number of organisms per unit area (or per unit volume in aquatic habitats).

Formula: Population density = Number of organisms ÷ Unit area (or volume)

Example: Density = Total number / Area (per m² or km²)

Quadrat method: used to estimate population density in plants/small organisms

Overcrowding

Definition: Overcrowding occurs when population density becomes too high, leading to limited resources (food, space, water) and stronger competition.

Effects of Overcrowding

  • Food shortage
  • Competition and stress
  • Faster spread of diseases (e.g., cholera, malaria)
  • High death rate and migration
  • Environmental degradation (deforestation, pollution)

🇳🇬 JAMB Focus (Nigeria/Africa)

  • Reasons for rapid human population growth: high birth rate, improved healthcare, reduced infant mortality, cultural factors
  • Consequences: unemployment, slums, disease outbreaks, food shortage, pollution

Example Calculation

Question: If 500 tilapia fish are in a 250 m² pond, find density.

Solution: Density = 500 ÷ 250 = 2 fish per m².

High fish density can lead to overcrowding and reduced oxygen/food

2️⃣ Adaptation for Survival (Competition)

Definition (Adaptation): Adaptation is any structural, physiological, or behavioral feature that increases an organism’s chance of survival and reproduction in its environment.

Competition example: lions and hyenas may compete for food

(i) Factors that bring about competition

Limited Resources

  • Food and water
  • Space/territory
  • Mates
  • Shelter
  • Light (plants)
  • Nutrients (plants)

(ii) Intra-specific vs Inter-specific Competition

Intra-specific
  • Meaning: competition within the same species
  • Examples: male lions fighting; maize plants competing for nutrients
Inter-specific
  • Meaning: competition between different species
  • Examples: lions vs hyenas; grasses vs weeds

Intra-specific competition: maize plants compete for light, water, nutrients

(iii) Relationship between competition and succession

Key idea: Competition helps drive ecological succession. Early species change the environment, allowing other species to replace them over time until a stable climax community forms.

Succession logic (simple): Pioneers arrive → change habitat → later species outcompete them → climax community.

Niche differentiation (Key JAMB point)

Definition: Niche differentiation is when species use resources differently (different food, time, space, or method) to reduce competition.

Niche example: birds feed in different parts of trees to reduce competition

✅ JAMB Objectives Covered

  • Link high population → shortage of food/space → competition
  • Differentiate intra- and inter-specific competition
  • Explain niche differentiation as a way to reduce competition
  • Relate competition to succession

3️⃣ Factors Affecting Population Sizes

Definition: Population size changes due to births, deaths, immigration, and emigration — and factors that influence them.

Population size is affected by: births, deaths, immigration, emigration

(i) Biotic factors (Living)

  • Food availability: more food → higher survival/reproduction
  • Predation: predators reduce prey numbers
  • Disease & pests: kill/weaken organisms
  • Competition: reduces survival/reproduction
  • Reproductive ability: high fertility → faster growth

Predation is a biotic factor that controls population size

(ii) Abiotic factors (Non-living)

  • Temperature: extremes reduce survival
  • Rainfall/water: drought → food shortage
  • Light: affects photosynthesis
  • Space/territory: limits growth
  • Topography: altitude/slope affects oxygen/temperature
  • pH: affects nutrient uptake
  • Pressure: deep water/high altitude effects

Drought (abiotic) can trigger food shortage and migration (biotic effects)

Interactions example (JAMB loves this)

Chain reaction: Drought → plants die → food shortage → animals starve/migrate → overcrowding in remaining areas → competition ↑ → disease ↑.

✅ JAMB Objective

  • Deduce effects of biotic/abiotic factors on population size
  • Explain interactions between biotic and abiotic factors

4️⃣ Ecological Succession

Definition: Ecological succession is the gradual, orderly change in a community over time from a bare area to a stable climax community.

Succession: pioneer species → shrubs → trees → climax community

(i) Primary succession

Definition: Primary succession starts on a lifeless surface with no soil (e.g., bare rock, new sand dune, lava flow).

Pioneer species (lichens) help break rocks to form soil

Typical Sequence

  • Lichens/mosses (pioneers) → thin soil formation
  • Small herbs/grasses
  • Shrubs
  • Small trees
  • Forest (climax)

(ii) Secondary succession

Definition: Secondary succession starts where soil already exists but the community was removed (bush fire, abandoned farmland, logging). It is faster than primary succession.

Secondary succession: soil remains, so recovery is faster

Sequence: grasses → shrubs → young trees → mature forest (climax).

🎓 Climax Community (Definition)

  • Stable, self-perpetuating community
  • Maximum biomass and diversity
  • Little change unless disturbed

✅ JAMB Objective

  • Trace the sequence in succession to the climax stage
