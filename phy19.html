📚 Chapter 19: Heat Transfer

🎯 Learning Objectives: Differentiate between conduction, convection, and radiation; solve numerical problems on thermal conductivity; understand surface effects on radiation; compare material conductivities; explain thermos flask operation; differentiate land/sea breezes; analyze engine principles.

1️⃣ Three Modes of Heat Transfer 🔥

Mode Mechanism Medium Required Examples
Conduction Heat flows through direct molecular contact (vibrations passed molecule to molecule) Solids (best), liquids, gases Metal spoon in hot soup, iron rod in fire
Convection Heat transfer by actual movement of fluid particles (currents) Fluids only (liquids & gases) Boiling water, sea breeze, hot air rising
Radiation Heat transfer through electromagnetic waves (infrared) No medium needed (vacuum OK) Sun's heat reaching Earth, warmth from fire

✅ Key Differences to Remember

  • Conduction: Molecules stay in place, just vibrate more
  • Convection: Molecules themselves move, carrying heat
  • Radiation: Travels at speed of light; can pass through vacuum

2️⃣ Temperature Gradient, Conductivity & Heat Flux 📏

Temperature Gradient

  • Definition: Rate of change of temperature with distance
  • Formula: ΔT/Δx or dT/dx (°C/m or K/m)
  • Concept: Heat flows hot→cold. Steeper gradient = faster heat flow

Thermal Conductivity (k)

  • Definition: Measure of how well a material conducts heat
  • Symbol: k (or λ)
  • Unit: W m⁻¹ K⁻¹
  • High k: Good conductors (copper: 400 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹)
  • Low k: Insulators (wood, air: 0.02-0.1 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹)

Heat Flux (q)

Fourier's Law:
q = Q/A = -k(dT/dx)

Unit: W m⁻² (watts per square meter)

Total Heat Flow Rate:
Q/t = kA(T₁ - T₂)/d

Where:
• Q/t = heat flow rate (W)
• A = cross-sectional area (m²)
• (T₁ - T₂) = temperature difference (K)
• d = thickness/distance (m)
💡 JAMB Tip: Remember the formula Q/t = kAΔT/d! Questions often ask you to calculate heat flow through a slab.

3️⃣ Effect of Surface Nature on Radiation 🎨

Surface Type Absorption Emission Examples
Black/Dull/Matte Good absorber Good emitter Black car gets hotter; black radiator emits efficiently
White/Shiny/Polished Poor absorber (reflects) Poor emitter White clothes in summer; polished kettle stays hot

✅ Practical Applications

  • Solar collectors: Black surfaces absorb maximum solar energy
  • Thermos flask: Silvered surfaces minimize heat loss by radiation
  • Firefighters' suits: Shiny/reflective to reflect radiant heat

Key Principle: Good absorbers are good emitters; poor absorbers are poor emitters (Kirchhoff's law)

5️⃣ The Thermos Flask (Vacuum Flask) 🧴

The thermos minimizes heat transfer by all three modes:

Component Function Prevents
Double-walled glass with vacuum No air molecules = no conduction or convection Conduction & Convection
Silvered surfaces (inside walls) Reflects infrared radiation back Radiation
Cork/Plastic stopper Poor conductor, seals tightly Conduction & Convection
Plastic/Metal outer case Protects glass, provides insulation Mechanical damage
  • Hot liquids: Vacuum prevents heat escaping; silvering reflects radiant heat back
  • Cold liquids: Vacuum prevents heat entering; silvering reflects external radiation away
💡 JAMB Objective: You must relate each component to the mode of heat transfer it prevents.

6️⃣ Land and Sea Breeze 🌊

Natural convection currents caused by different specific heat capacities of land and water.

Sea Breeze (Daytime)

  • When: Day (sunny)
  • Why: Land heats faster than sea (lower specific heat capacity)
  • Result: Air over land → warm → rises → low pressure; Air over sea → cool → sinks → high pressure; Wind blows from sea to land

Land Breeze (Nighttime)

  • When: Night
  • Why: Land cools faster than sea
  • Result: Air over sea → relatively warmer → rises → low pressure; Air over land → cool → sinks → high pressure; Wind blows from land to sea
💡 Memory Aid: "Sea by day, Land by night"

7️⃣ Engines 🚀

Internal Combustion Engines

  • Principle: Burns fuel inside engine cylinder
  • Heat transfer: Conduction through engine block (needs coolant), convection in coolant circulation, radiation from hot surfaces

Jet Engines

  • Principle: Air compressed, mixed with fuel, ignited; hot gases expelled backward (Newton's 3rd law)
  • Heat transfer: Intense radiation and convection from combustion chamber

Rockets

  • Principle: Carries own oxidizer; works in vacuum
  • Heat transfer: Conduction through nozzle walls, radiation in space (no convection/conduction in vacuum), convection inside combustion chamber
💡 JAMB Focus: Understand that engines convert heat energy to mechanical work, and heat transfer principles are used in cooling systems.

🔢 7 Essential Calculations for JAMB

Calculation 1: Basic Heat Conduction

Question: Calculate heat flow rate through a brick wall (area 20 m², thickness 0.3 m) when temperatures are 35°C and 15°C. k = 0.6 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹.

Using Q/t = kA(T₁ - T₂)/d:
k = 0.6 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹
A = 20 m²
ΔT = 35 - 15 = 20 K
d = 0.3 m

Q/t = (0.6 × 20 × 20)/0.3
Q/t = 240/0.3 = 800 W
Answer: 800 W (or 800 J/s)

Calculation 2: Finding Temperature Difference

Question: A copper rod (length 2 m, area 0.5 cm²) conducts heat at 100 W. k = 400 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹. Find temperature difference.

Convert area: 0.5 cm² = 0.5 × 10⁻⁴ m² = 5 × 10⁻⁵ m²

Rearranging: ΔT = (Q/t × d)/(k × A)
ΔT = (100 × 2)/(400 × 5 × 10⁻⁵)
ΔT = 200/0.02 = 10,000 K
Answer: 10,000 K (or 10,000°C)

Calculation 3: Heat Flux

Question: A metal plate has temperature gradient of 50°C per cm. k = 200 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹. Calculate heat flux.

Convert gradient: 50°C/cm = 50 × 100 = 5000 K/m

Using q = k × temperature gradient:
q = 200 × 5000 = 1,000,000 W m⁻²
Answer: 10⁶ W m⁻² (or 1 MW/m²)

Calculation 4: Comparing Materials

Question: Two identical rods: copper (k=400) and iron (k=80). Length 1 m, area 2 cm², ΔT = 50°C. Find ratio of heat flow rates.

Since A, d, and ΔT are same:
(Q/t)copper/(Q/t)iron = kcopper/kiron
Ratio = 400/80 = 5
Answer: 5:1 (copper conducts 5× faster)

Calculation 5: Composite Wall (Series)

Question: Wall has brick (0.2 m, k=0.6) and plaster (0.05 m, k=0.3). Outer brick at 30°C, outer plaster at 10°C. Find heat flux.

Total thermal resistance R = R₁ + R₂:
R = d₁/k₁ + d₂/k₂ = 0.2/0.6 + 0.05/0.3
R = 0.333 + 0.167 = 0.5 m² K W⁻¹

Heat flux: q = ΔT/R = (30-10)/0.5 = 20/0.5
Answer: 40 W m⁻²

Calculation 6: Time to Transfer Energy

Question: How long for 5000 J to pass through aluminum window (1.5 m², 4 mm thick, ΔT=25°C)? k = 237 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹.

Convert thickness: 4 mm = 0.004 m

Heat flow rate:
Q/t = kAΔT/d = (237 × 1.5 × 25)/0.004
Q/t = 8887.5/0.004 = 2,221,875 W

Time: t = Q/(Q/t) = 5000/2,221,875
Answer: ≈ 0.00225 s (or 2.25 ms)

Calculation 7: Finding Thickness

Question: Freezer wall must limit heat gain to 50 W. External 25°C, internal -5°C. Area 4 m², k = 0.04 W m⁻¹ K⁻¹. What thickness?

ΔT = 25 - (-5) = 30 K

Rearranging: d = kAΔT/(Q/t)
d = (0.04 × 4 × 30)/50
d = 4.8/50 = 0.096 m
Answer: 9.6 cm (or approximately 10 cm)

⚠️ Common JAMB Question Patterns

  • ❌ "Which material is best conductor?" → Look for metal with highest k (silver/copper)
  • ❌ "Why does thermos have silvered surfaces?" → To prevent radiation
  • ❌ "Calculate heat flow through wall..." → Use Q/t = kAΔT/d
  • ❌ "Wind direction at noon near coast?" → Sea breeze (sea to land)
  • ❌ "Why are solar panels black?" → Good absorbers of radiation

🎯 Final Tips for Success

  • Master the formula: Q/t = kAΔT/d — appears frequently
  • Understand the thermos: Classic JAMB question about minimizing all three heat transfer modes
  • Visualize convection: Hot air/water rises, cool sinks — explains breezes and heating systems
  • Distinguish carefully: Conduction needs contact, convection needs fluid movement, radiation needs nothing
  • Watch units: Convert cm² to m² (×10⁻⁴), mm to m (×10⁻³), cm to m (×10⁻²) before calculating
