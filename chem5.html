    Step 1: Difference Between Ordinary Chemical Reaction and Nuclear Reaction

    JAMB objective (i)

    Feature Ordinary Chemical Reaction Nuclear Reaction
    What changes Only electrons (outer shells) Protons and neutrons in the nucleus
    Element changes? No (same element) Yes (transmutation – new element formed)
    Mass change Very tiny (not measurable) Huge (measurable)
    Energy released Small (e.g. burning fuel) Extremely large (millions of times bigger)
    Example 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O 23592U + 10n → fission products + energy

    Key point to remember: In chemical reactions the nucleus stays the same. In nuclear reactions the nucleus itself breaks or changes.

    Step 2: Radioactivity – Types, Properties and Detection of Radiations

    JAMB objective (i) & (iii)

    What is radioactivity? Unstable nuclei spontaneously emit radiation to become stable.

    Three main types of radiation (you MUST know all three):

    • α-particles (Alpha) – Helium nucleus. Symbol: 42He or α
    • β-particles (Beta) – High-speed electron. Symbol: 0-1e (beta-minus) or β⁻
    • γ-rays (Gamma) – Electromagnetic waves. Symbol: γ

    Comparison of properties (memorise this table – it appears every year)

    Property Alpha (α) Beta (β) Gamma (γ)
    Charge +2 –1 0
    Mass 4 u Almost 0 0
    Speed \~10 % of light \~90 % of light Speed of light
    Penetrating power Very low (stopped by paper/skin) Medium (stopped by 1 mm aluminium) Very high (needs lead or concrete)
    Ionising power Very high Medium Very low
    Deflection in magnetic field Deflected (positive) Deflected (opposite to α) Not deflected

    Detection of radiations

    • Photographic film (blackens)
    • Cloud chamber (shows tracks)
    • Scintillation counter
    • Geiger-Muller (G-M) counter – most important for JAMB (clicks or counts rate)

    Step 3: Natural and Artificial Radioactivity

    JAMB objective (ii)

    Natural radioactivity: Happens by itself in nature. No human help. Example: Uranium-238 in rocks decays slowly to lead.

    Artificial (induced) radioactivity: Man-made. Stable nucleus is bombarded with particles in a reactor or cyclotron. Example: 147N + 42He → 178O + 11H

    Easy way to remember: Natural = spontaneous + occurs in nature. Artificial = caused by man + needs particle accelerator/reactor.

    Step 4: Nuclear Stability and Radioactive Decay

    JAMB objective (iii)

    Stability depends on neutron/proton (n/p) ratio. If wrong, nucleus decays.

    Main types of decay (with equations):

    • Alpha decay (heavy elements): 22688Ra → 22286Rn + 42He
    • Beta-minus decay (too many neutrons): 146C → 147N + 0-1e
    • Gamma emission: No change in mass or atomic number. Just energy released.

    Step 5: Nuclear Reactions – Fission and Fusion

    JAMB objective (iv) part of (v)

    Nuclear fission (splitting): Heavy nucleus splits. Example: 23592U + 10n → fission products + 3 neutrons + energy. Used in power stations.

    Nuclear fusion (joining): Light nuclei combine. Example: 21H + 31H → 42He + neutron + energy. Happens in the sun.

    Step 6: Half-Life Calculations

    JAMB objective (iv)

    Half-life (T½) = time taken for half the radioactive atoms to decay.

    Formula: Fraction remaining = (1/2)n where n = number of half-lives.

    Example Calculation Result
    T½ = 4 days, after 12 days n = 12/4 = 3 Fraction left = 1/8
    16 g I-131, T½ = 8 days, after 24 days n = 3 Mass left = 2 g

    Step 7: Balancing Simple Nuclear Equations

    JAMB objective (v)

    Rules: Mass numbers and atomic numbers must balance.

    • 23892U → 23490Th + 42He
    • 146C → 147N + 0-1e

    Step 8: Applications of Radioactivity

    JAMB objective (vi)

    • Medicine: I-131 (thyroid scan), Co-60 (cancer radiotherapy)
    • Industry: Thickness gauges, sterilisation
    • Dating: Carbon-14 (archaeology), Uranium-lead (rocks)
    • Energy: Nuclear power stations
    • Agriculture: Tracers for fertilisers, mutation breeding

    3 JAMB Examples & Explanations

    JAMB Example 1: Half-Life Calculation

    Question: A radioactive sample has a half-life of 5 days. What fraction remains after 20 days?

    Explanation: n = 20/5 = 4 half-lives. Fraction left = (1/2)4 = 1/16. JAMB loves this exact calculation style.

    JAMB Example 2: Balancing Nuclear Equation

    Question: Complete: 22688Ra → ? + 42He

    Explanation: Mass: 226 = x + 4 → x=222. Atomic: 88 = y + 2 → y=86. So 22286Rn. JAMB always tests mass/atomic balance.

    JAMB Example 3: Applications

    Question: Which radioisotope is used for cancer treatment?

    Explanation: Cobalt-60 (γ-rays). JAMB asks applications in medicine, industry, dating – know the common ones.
