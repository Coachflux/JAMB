📚 Chapter 22: Characteristics of Sound Waves

🎯 Learning Objectives: Understand the nature of sound waves, distinguish between pitch, loudness and quality, calculate speed of sound in different media, apply echo formulas, analyze Doppler effect, understand resonance in pipes, and solve beat frequency problems.

1️⃣ Nature of Sound Waves 🔊

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave that requires a medium to travel. It consists of:

  • Compressions (C): Regions of high pressure/density
  • Rarefactions (R): Regions of low pressure/density

Key Properties

  • ❌ Cannot travel through a vacuum
  • ✅ Travels fastest through solids, then liquids, then gases
  • 📏 Speed in air at 20°C ≈ 343 m/s (or ~330 m/s for JAMB)

2️⃣ Characteristics of Sound Waves 🎵

A. Pitch

  • Determined by frequency of the sound wave
  • Higher frequency = higher pitch
  • Lower frequency = lower pitch
  • Human hearing range: 20 Hz – 20,000 Hz

B. Loudness (Intensity)

  • Determined by amplitude of the wave
  • Greater amplitude = louder sound
  • Measured in decibels (dB)

C. Quality (Timbre)

  • Allows us to distinguish between sounds of same pitch and loudness
  • Depends on number and relative intensity of overtones/harmonics
  • Example: Piano vs. violin playing same note at same volume sound different

3️⃣ Speed of Sound ⚡

The speed of sound depends on:

  • Medium (solid, liquid, gas)
  • Temperature (in gases)
Formula for speed in gases:
v = √(γRT/M)

Where:
• γ = ratio of specific heats (≈1.4 for air)
• R = gas constant
• T = absolute temperature (K)
• M = molar mass

Simplified relationship:
v ∝ √T

If v₁ at T₁ and v₂ at T₂:
v₁/v₂ = √(T₁/T₂)

4️⃣ Echo and Reverberation 🔁

Echo

  • Reflection of sound from a distant surface
  • Heard as distinct repetition
  • Minimum distance for echo: 17 m (time delay > 0.1s)
Distance calculation:
d = (v × t)/2

The factor of 2 accounts for the round trip (to surface and back)

Reverberation

  • Multiple reflections causing prolonged sound
  • Desirable in concert halls (to some extent)
  • Too much reverberation causes sound to become "muddy"

5️⃣ Doppler Effect 🚗🔊

Change in frequency due to relative motion between source and observer.

Scenario Observed Frequency
Approaching source Higher frequency (higher pitch)
Receding source Lower frequency (lower pitch)
Formula when source moves:
f' = f[v/(v ± vₛ)]

Formula when observer moves:
f' = f[(v ± v₀)/v]

Where:
• f' = observed frequency
• f = source frequency
• v = speed of sound
• vₛ = speed of source (+ if receding, – if approaching)
• v₀ = speed of observer (+ if approaching, – if receding)

6️⃣ Resonance and Stationary Waves in Pipes 🎺

Open Pipes (Both Ends Open)

Fundamental: f₀ = v/2L
Harmonics: fₙ = nf₀ (n = 1, 2, 3, ...)
All harmonics present

Closed Pipes (One End Closed)

Fundamental: f₀ = v/4L
Harmonics: fₙ = nf₀ (n = 1, 3, 5, ...)
Only odd harmonics present

7️⃣ Beats 🥁

When two sounds of slightly different frequencies are heard together:

Beat frequency:
f_beat = |f₁ - f₂|

Number of beats per second = difference in frequencies

✅ Applications of Beats

  • Tuning musical instruments
  • Detecting frequency differences
  • Police radar (indirectly related principle)

🔢 7 Essential Calculations for JAMB

Calculation 1: Speed and Wavelength

Question: A sound wave of frequency 500 Hz travels through air at 340 m/s. Calculate: (a) The wavelength, (b) The period.

(a) Using v = fλ:
λ = v/f = 340/500 = 0.68 m

(b) Period T = 1/f:
T = 1/500 = 0.002 s = 2 ms
Answer: (a) 0.68 m, (b) 2 ms

Calculation 2: Echo Calculation

Question: A man stands 510 m from a cliff and fires a gun. How long to hear the echo? (v = 340 m/s)

Total distance = 2 × 510 = 1,020 m

Using t = distance/speed:
t = 1,020/340 = 3 seconds
Answer: 3 seconds

Calculation 3: Temperature Effect on Speed

Question: Speed of sound is 330 m/s at 0°C (273 K). What is the speed at 27°C (300 K)?

Using v₁/v₂ = √(T₁/T₂):
330/v₂ = √(273/300) = √0.91 = 0.954
v₂ = 330/0.954 = 346 m/s
Answer: 346 m/s

Calculation 4: Doppler Effect

Question: A train moving at 30 m/s sounds a whistle of frequency 500 Hz. What frequency is heard by a stationary observer as the train approaches? (v = 340 m/s)

Using f' = f[v/(v - vₛ)]:
f' = 500 × [340/(340 - 30)]
f' = 500 × (340/310)
f' = 500 × 1.097 = 548.4 Hz
Answer: 548.4 Hz

Calculation 5: Resonance in Open Pipe

Question: An open pipe is 0.5 m long. Calculate the fundamental frequency. (v = 340 m/s)

Using f₀ = v/2L:
f₀ = 340/(2 × 0.5) = 340/1 = 340 Hz
Answer: 340 Hz

Calculation 6: Beats

Question: Two tuning forks produce 5 beats per second. One fork has frequency 256 Hz. What are the possible frequencies of the second fork?

Using f_beat = |f₁ - f₂|:
5 = |256 - f₂|

Therefore:
f₂ = 256 + 5 = 261 Hz
OR
f₂ = 256 - 5 = 251 Hz
Answer: 261 Hz or 251 Hz

Calculation 7: Closed Pipe

Question: A closed pipe of length 0.4 m resonates at its fundamental frequency. Calculate this frequency. (v = 320 m/s)

Using f₀ = v/4L:
f₀ = 320/(4 × 0.4) = 320/1.6 = 200 Hz
Answer: 200 Hz

📋 Summary Table: Key Formulas

Concept Formula
Wave speed v = fλ
Echo distance d = (vt)/2
Open pipe fundamental f = v/2L
Closed pipe fundamental f = v/4L
Beat frequency f_beat = |f₁ - f₂|
Doppler effect (moving source) f' = f[v/(v ± vₛ)]
Temperature relationship v₁/v₂ = √(T₁/T₂)

🎯 JAMB Exam Tips

  1. Remember standard values:
    • Speed of sound in air ≈ 330-340 m/s
    • Speed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s (much faster!)

  2. For echo problems: Always remember sound travels to obstacle AND back (factor of 2)

  3. Doppler effect:
    • Approaching = higher frequency (use minus in denominator)
    • Receding = lower frequency (use plus in denominator)

  4. Pipes:
    • Open pipes: all harmonics present
    • Closed pipes: only odd harmonics

  5. Units: Always convert to SI units (meters, seconds, Hz) before calculating

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Forgetting the factor of 2 in echo calculations
  • ❌ Using Celsius instead of Kelvin in temperature ratio formulas
  • ❌ Confusing open pipe formula (v/2L) with closed pipe (v/4L)
  • ❌ Getting the signs wrong in Doppler effect (approaching vs. receding)
  • ❌ Thinking beat frequency is the average of two frequencies (it's the difference)
