Need for Support and Movement in Organisms

Support: Maintains shape, resists gravity/wind/external forces; positions organs (e.g., leaves for photosynthesis, upright posture in animals).

Movement: Enables escape from danger, foraging for food/water/light, reproduction (e.g., pollen dispersal), and stimulus responses (e.g., tropisms).

JAMB Example: "The need for support in tall plants is primarily to: A. Absorb water B. Resist wind C. Store food D. Produce seeds" (Ans: B).

a. Tropic, Tactic, Nastic, and Sleep Movements in Plants

Tropic (Tropisms): Directional growth to unidirectional stimulus (auxin-driven unequal elongation). E.g., Phototropism (shoots +ve to light), Geotropism (roots +ve to gravity), Hydrotropism (roots to water), Thigmotropism (tendrils to touch).

Tactic (Taxes): Whole organism/cell movement. E.g., Positive phototaxis (Euglena to light).

Nastic (Nasties): Non-directional, turgor-based. E.g., Seismonasty (Mimosa leaf folding on touch).

Sleep (Nyctinastic): Rhythmic day-night responses. E.g., Legume leaves fold at night to reduce transpiration.

Regions of growth: Shoot apex (meristem), root tip (behind cap) ‚Äì auxins control tropisms.

JAMB Example: "The response of Mimosa pudica to touch is an example of: A. Phototropism B. Geotropism C. Thigmotropism D. Seismonasty" (Ans: D).

Another: "Auxins are produced in the: A. Root cap B. Shoot tip C. Leaf base D. Stem cortex" (Ans: B).

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Phototropism):

  • Light source: Unidirectional stimulus
  • Auxin accumulation: More on shaded side ‚Üí cell elongation
  • Bending: Toward light (shoot +ve phototropism)

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Mimosa pudica):

  • Leaf before touch: Open leaflets
  • Leaf after touch: Folded leaflets (seismonasty)
  • Pulvinus: Swellable motor organ (turgor change)

Auxin Mechanism in Tropisms (Cholodny-Went Theory)

Auxin (IAA) synthesized at shoot tip; polar transport (basipetal via AUX/LAX influx & PIN efflux proteins).

Acid Growth Hypothesis: Auxin binds receptors ‚Üí H‚Å∫ pumps activate ‚Üí cell wall pH drops ‚Üí expansins loosen cellulose ‚Üí wall becomes extensible ‚Üí osmosis causes water entry ‚Üí irreversible cell elongation.

Shoots: Auxin accumulates on shaded/lower side ‚Üí promotes elongation ‚Üí bends toward light/upward.

Roots: Auxin on lower side ‚Üí inhibits elongation ‚Üí bends downward.

JAMB Example: "In phototropism, the bending of shoots toward light is due to: A. Equal auxin on both sides B. More auxin on lit side C. More auxin on shaded side D. No auxin involvement" (Ans: C).

b. Supporting Tissues in Plants

Collenchyma: Living cells, uneven pectin-cellulose walls ‚Äì flexible support in growing parts (e.g., young stems, petioles, leaf veins).

Sclerenchyma: Dead, thick lignified walls ‚Äì rigid support (fibres: long, in bundles; sclereids: short, in nutshells/pears).

Xylem fibres: Lignified tracheids/vessels ‚Äì mechanical support + water transport.

Phloem fibres: Sclerenchyma in phloem ‚Äì added strength.

Distribution:

  • Root: Xylem central (star-shaped in dicots), sclerenchyma around vascular tissue.
  • Stem: Vascular bundles (xylem inner, phloem outer) with collenchyma below epidermis (young); sclerenchyma caps in older stems.
  • Leaf: Collenchyma/sclerenchyma in midrib/veins; xylem/phloem in bundles.

JAMB Example: "The tissue providing flexible support in herbaceous stems is: A. Xylem B. Phloem C. Collenchyma D. Sclerenchyma" (Ans: C).

Another: "Lignified walls are characteristic of: A. Parenchyma B. Collenchyma C. Sclerenchyma D. Epidermis" (Ans: C).

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Plant Supporting Tissues):

  • Collenchyma: Living, unevenly thickened corners, flexible support
  • Sclerenchyma fibres: Dead, thick lignified walls, rigid support
  • Sclereids: Short, thick-walled cells (e.g., in nutshells)

c. Types and Functions of the Skeleton

Hydrostatic Skeleton

Fluid-filled cavity (coelom) with antagonistic muscles (circular/longitudinal) ‚Äì uses incompressible fluid pressure for support/movement.

Examples: Earthworm (peristalsis for burrowing), jellyfish (bell pulsing), sea anemone (tentacle extension).

Advantages: Flexible, no moulting; Disadvantages: Limited in large/dry environments.

JAMB Example: "The skeleton in earthworms is: A. Exoskeleton B. Endoskeleton C. Hydrostatic D. Cartilaginous" (Ans: C).

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Earthworm Hydrostatic Skeleton):

  • Coelom (fluid-filled cavity): Provides pressure for support
  • Circular muscles: Contract ‚Üí lengthen body
  • Longitudinal muscles: Contract ‚Üí shorten body
  • Setae: Anchor during movement

i. Exoskeleton

External covering (chitin in arthropods like insects; CaCO‚ÇÉ shells in molluscs).

Functions: Protection (from predators/desiccation), support, muscle attachment (internal); moulting for growth.

JAMB Example: "Moulting in insects is necessary because the exoskeleton: A. Grows with the body B. Does not grow C. Is too soft D. Absorbs water" (Ans: B).

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Insect Exoskeleton):

  • Cuticle: Outer protective layer (chitin + proteins)
  • Epidermis: Secretes cuticle
  • Muscle attachment: Internal to exoskeleton

ii. Endoskeleton

Internal framework (cartilage/bone in vertebrates).

Functions: Support, continuous growth, muscle attachment (external), organ protection.

Materials: Cartilage (flexible, e.g., shark skeleton, human ears/nose), bone (rigid, mineralized).

JAMB Example: "The endoskeleton in mammals is mainly composed of: A. Chitin B. Cartilage and bone C. Fluid D. Shell" (Ans: B).

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Human Skeleton):

  • Axial skeleton: Skull, vertebral column, ribs, sternum (central support & protection)
  • Appendicular skeleton: Limbs & girdles (locomotion & manipulation)
  • Skull: Protects brain
  • Ribs & sternum: Protect heart & lungs
  • Pelvic girdle: Supports lower limbs & organs

iii. Functions of the Skeleton in Animals (Mammalian Focus)

  • Support: Maintains shape/posture against gravity.
  • Locomotion: Bones act as levers with muscles/joints for movement.
  • Protection: Skull (brain), ribs (heart/lungs), vertebrae (spinal cord).
  • Respiration: Ribs/sternum expand/contract chest for breathing.
  • Other: Blood production (red marrow), mineral storage (Ca/P), pH balance.

Application to Wellbeing: Strong bones prevent fractures/osteoporosis; healthy joints aid mobility/avoid arthritis; supports respiration for oxygen supply.

JAMB Example: "A major function of the mammalian skeleton is: A. Photosynthesis B. Protection of organs C. Water conduction D. Seed dispersal" (Ans: B).

Another: "The ribs and sternum aid in: A. Digestion B. Respiration C. Excretion D. Reproduction" (Ans: B).

Structure and Layout of Mammalian Skeleton

Axial Skeleton: Central axis ‚Äì skull, vertebral column, ribs, sternum (support/protection/respiration).

Appendicular Skeleton: Limbs/girdles ‚Äì pectoral (shoulders), pelvic (hips), arms/legs (locomotion).

Total Bones in Human Body: Adults have 206 bones (newborns ~270; fuses during growth, e.g., sacral vertebrae fuse).

Distribution:

  • Axial (80 bones): Skull (22), ear ossicles (6), hyoid (1), vertebral column (26), ribs (24), sternum (1).
  • Appendicular (126 bones): Pectoral girdles (4), upper limbs (60), pelvic girdles (2), lower limbs (60).

JAMB Example: "The total number of bones in an adult human is: A. 200 B. 206 C. 210 D. 220" (Ans: B).

Another: "The axial skeleton includes the: A. Limbs B. Skull and vertebrae C. Pelvic girdle D. Arms" (Ans: B).

Types of Joints

Functional Classification: Synarthroses (fixed, e.g., skull sutures), Amphiarthroses (slightly movable, e.g., pubic symphysis), Diarthroses (freely movable/synovial, e.g., knee).

Structural Classification: Fibrous (fixed), Cartilaginous (slightly movable), Synovial (freely movable; fluid-filled).

6 Types of Synovial Joints:

  • Plane (Gliding): Sliding (e.g., wrist carpals).
  • Hinge: Flexion/extension (e.g., elbow).
  • Pivot: Rotation (e.g., neck atlas-axis).
  • Condyloid: Biaxial (e.g., knuckles).
  • Saddle: Thumb opposition (e.g., carpometacarpal).
  • Ball-and-Socket: Multiaxial (e.g., shoulder).

JAMB Example: "The joint allowing rotation in the neck is: A. Hinge B. Pivot C. Ball-and-socket D. Gliding" (Ans: B).

Another: "Ball-and-socket joints are found in the: A. Elbow B. Knee C. Shoulder D. Ankle" (Ans: C).

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Synovial Joints):

  • Ball-and-socket: Shoulder/hip ‚Äì multiaxial movement
  • Hinge: Elbow/knee ‚Äì flexion/extension
  • Pivot: Neck atlas-axis ‚Äì rotation
  • Saddle: Thumb ‚Äì opposition
  • Plane: Carpals ‚Äì gliding
