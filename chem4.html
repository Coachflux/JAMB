– JAMB Topic: Atomic Structure and Bonding

Concepts of Atoms, Molecules, and Ions

Atom: The smallest particle of an element that can take part in a chemical reaction. It's neutral and consists of a nucleus (protons + neutrons) surrounded by electrons.

Molecule: Two or more atoms bonded together, e.g., H₂ (hydrogen molecule) or CO₂.

Ion: A charged atom or molecule formed by gaining or losing electrons. Cations are positive (e.g., Na⁺), anions are negative (e.g., Cl⁻).

Key skill: Distinguish them—atoms are neutral and single; molecules are neutral groups; ions are charged.

Step 1: Historical Development – Works of Key Scientists

These scientists built the modern atomic model through experiments:

  • Dalton (1808): Proposed atoms as indivisible, solid spheres; all atoms of an element are identical; compounds form by combining atoms in fixed ratios.
  • Thompson (1897): Discovered electrons using cathode ray tubes; proposed "plum pudding" model (positive sphere with embedded negative electrons).
  • Rutherford (1911): Gold foil experiment showed atoms have a small, dense positive nucleus; most space is empty.
  • Millikan (1909): Oil drop experiment measured electron charge (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C).
  • Moseley (1913): X-ray studies established atomic number as number of protons, organizing the periodic table.
  • Bohr (1913): Electrons orbit nucleus in fixed energy levels (shells); explained hydrogen spectrum.

Key skill: Identify their contributions—e.g., Rutherford disproved Thompson's model.

Step 2: Atomic Structure, Numbers, and Isotopes

Structure: Nucleus (protons: positive, neutrons: neutral) with electrons in orbitals around it.

Atomic number (Z): Number of protons (equals electrons in neutral atom).

Mass number (A): Protons + neutrons.

To find neutrons: A - Z.

Isotopes: Atoms of same element (same Z) but different A (different neutrons). They have identical chemical properties but different physical ones (e.g., density).

Examples (elements 1-20): Hydrogen (¹H, ²H); Carbon (¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C); Oxygen (¹⁶O, ¹⁸O). Common isotopes: Cl (³⁵Cl, ³⁷Cl).

Calculations: Average atomic mass = (abundance1 × mass1 + abundance2 × mass2) / 100. E.g., For Cl (75% ³⁵Cl, 25% ³⁷Cl): (75×35 + 25×37)/100 = 35.5.

Key skill: Deduce protons/neutrons/electrons from Z and A; relate isotopy to mass number; calculate averages.

Step 3: Quantum Numbers, Orbitals, and Electron Configuration

Four quantum numbers govern electron position:

  • Principal (n): Energy level (1, 2, 3...).
  • Azimuthal (l): Subshell (0=s, 1=p, 2=d, 3=f).
  • Magnetic (m_l): Orbital orientation (-l to +l).
  • Spin (m_s): +½ or -½.

Principles for arrangement:

  • Aufbau: Fill lowest energy first (order: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d...).
  • Pauli exclusion: Max 2 electrons per orbital, opposite spins.
  • Hund's rule: Fill orbitals singly before pairing.

Electron configuration (elements 1-20): E.g., H: 1s¹; He: 1s²; Li: 1s²2s¹; C: 1s²2s²2p²; Na: 1s²2s²2p⁶3s¹; Ca: 1s²2s²2p⁶3s²3p⁶4s².

Key skill: Apply rules to write configurations; note elements aim for noble gas structure (stable octet).

Step 4: Shapes of s and p Orbitals

s orbital: Spherical, symmetric around nucleus. Holds 2 electrons.

p orbitals: Dumbbell-shaped, three orientations (p_x, p_y, p_z) along axes. Each holds 2 electrons; total 6 for p subshell.

Step 5: Periodic Table and Periodicity

Arranged by increasing atomic number. Rows (periods), columns (groups).

Families:

  • Group 1 (alkali metals: reactive, e.g., Na)
  • Group 2 (alkaline-earth: less reactive, e.g., Ca)
  • Group 17 (halogens: nonmetals, e.g., Cl)
  • Group 18 (noble gases: inert, e.g., He)
  • Groups 3-12 (transition metals: variable valency, e.g., Fe)

Trends (variations):

Property Across Period (left to right) Down Group
Ionization energy Increases (smaller size, stronger pull) Decreases (larger size)
Ionic radii Decreases (more protons pull electrons closer) Increases (more shells)
Electron affinity Increases Decreases
Electronegativity Increases Decreases
Conductivity Decreases (metals to nonmetals) Variable

Key skill: Relate position to properties; explain trends (e.g., Na has lower ionization energy than Cl).

Step 6: Chemical Bonding

Elements bond to achieve noble gas configuration (stable octet).

  • Electrovalent (ionic): Electron transfer from metal to nonmetal, e.g., Na (loses e⁻ to form Na⁺) + Cl (gains to Cl⁻) → NaCl. Results in high MP, conductive in solution.
  • Covalent: Electron sharing, e.g., H₂, Cl₂. Can be polar (unequal share) or nonpolar.
  • Hydrogen bonding: Strong dipole-dipole in H-F, H-O, H-N, e.g., H₂O (high BP).
  • Metallic: Delocalized electrons in metals, e.g., Cu; explains conductivity.
  • Coordinate (dative covalent): One atom donates pair, e.g., [Cu(NH₃)₄]²⁺, [Fe(CN)₆]³⁻.
  • Van der Waals: Weak attractions between molecules, e.g., noble gases.

Key skill: Deduce bond type from configurations (e.g., large electronegativity difference = ionic); relate to properties (ionic: brittle solids; covalent: gases/liquids).

Step 7: Shapes of Simple Molecules (VSEPR Theory)

Shapes depend on electron pairs around central atom (repel to minimize energy).

  • Linear: 2 bonding pairs, no lone pairs, e.g., H₂, O₂, Cl₂, HCl, CO₂ (bond angle 180°).
  • Non-linear (bent): 2 bonding + 2 lone pairs, e.g., H₂O (104.5°).
  • Tetrahedral: 4 bonding pairs, no lone, e.g., CH₄ (109.5°).
  • Pyramidal: 3 bonding + 1 lone pair, e.g., NH₃ (107°).
Molecule Electron Pairs Shape Bond Angle Example
BeCl₂, CO₂ 2 bonding, 0 lone Linear 180° CO₂
H₂O 2 bonding, 2 lone Bent 104.5° Water
CH₄ 4 bonding, 0 lone Tetrahedral 109.5° Methane
NH₃ 3 bonding, 1 lone Pyramidal 107° Ammonia

3 JAMB-Style Examples & Explanations

Example 1: Atomic Structure

JAMB Question Type: An atom has atomic number 11 and mass number 23. How many protons, neutrons, and electrons does it have?

Explanation: Protons = Z = 11; Electrons = 11 (neutral atom); Neutrons = A - Z = 23 - 11 = 12. This is sodium (Na). JAMB tests basic calculations from Z and A.

Example 2: Isotopes & Average Atomic Mass

JAMB Question Type: Chlorine has two isotopes: ³⁵Cl (75%) and ³⁷Cl (25%). Calculate the relative atomic mass.

Explanation: Average = (75 × 35 + 25 × 37) / 100 = (2625 + 925) / 100 = 3525 / 100 = 35.25. JAMB often asks for average mass from isotopes and abundances.

Example 3: Electron Configuration & Periodic Table

JAMB Question Type: Write the electron configuration of element with atomic number 20. In which period and group is it?

Explanation: Ca (Z=20): 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ 4s². Period 4 (highest n=4), Group 2 (valence electrons in s subshell = 2). JAMB tests Aufbau rule and periodic trends.

Final Tips for JAMB

  • Memorize: 1-20 configurations, bond types, VSEPR shapes, periodic trends.
  • Practice: Calculate neutrons, average mass, draw orbital shapes, predict molecule shapes.
  • Key phrase: "Atoms bond to achieve stable octet." Relate everything to electron configuration.
