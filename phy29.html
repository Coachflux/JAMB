📚 Chapter 29: Capacitors

🎯 Learning Objectives: Determine uses of capacitors, analyze parallel plate capacitors, calculate capacitance, understand factors affecting capacitance, solve series/parallel arrangements, and calculate energy stored.

1️⃣ What is a Capacitor? 🔋

A capacitor is a device that stores electric charge and electrical energy in an electric field.

Basic Structure

  • Two conducting plates (electrodes)
  • Dielectric: Insulating material between plates (air, paper, mica, ceramic, etc.)
  • When connected to a battery: one plate becomes positive (+Q), the other negative (−Q)
Symbol:
    │ │
    │ │
  ──┴─┴──

2️⃣ Capacitance (C) 📐

Definition

Capacitance is the ability of a capacitor to store charge. It measures how much charge a capacitor can hold per unit voltage.

C = Q/V

Where:
• C = Capacitance (Farads, F)
• Q = Charge stored (Coulombs, C)
• V = Potential difference/Voltage (Volts, V)
Units:
• Farad (F) – SI unit
• μF (microfarad, 10⁻⁶ F)
• nF (nanofarad, 10⁻⁹ F)
• pF (picofarad, 10⁻¹² F)

💡 1 Farad is huge! Most capacitors are μF or pF range.

3️⃣ Parallel Plate Capacitor ⚡

This is the most important type for JAMB. It consists of two parallel metal plates separated by distance d.

The Magic Formula:
C = εA/d

Where:
• ε = Permittivity = ε₀ × εᵣ
• ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F/m (permittivity of free space)
• εᵣ = Relative permittivity/dielectric constant (dimensionless)
• A = Area of one plate (m²)
• d = Separation between plates (m)

Special Cases

Medium Formula Notes
Air/Vacuum C = ε₀A/d εᵣ = 1
With dielectric C = ε₀εᵣA/d εᵣ > 1

Factors Affecting Capacitance

Factor Relationship Effect
Area (A) C ∝ A Larger area = Larger C
Separation (d) C ∝ 1/d Closer plates = Larger C
Dielectric (εᵣ) C ∝ εᵣ Higher εᵣ = Larger C

🔢 Calculation 1: Basic Capacitance

Question: A parallel plate capacitor has plates of area 0.04 m² separated by 2 mm in air. Calculate its capacitance.

Given: A = 0.04 m², d = 2 mm = 0.002 m, ε₀ = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² F/m

C = ε₀A/d
C = (8.85 × 10⁻¹² × 0.04)/0.002
C = (3.54 × 10⁻¹³)/0.002
C = 1.77 × 10⁻¹⁰ F = 177 pF
Answer: 177 pF (or 1.77 × 10⁻¹⁰ F)

🔢 Calculation 2: Effect of Dielectric

Question: A capacitor has capacitance 50 pF in air. What is its capacitance when a wax dielectric (εᵣ = 2.2) fills the space between plates?

Cnew = Cair × εᵣ
Cnew = 50 pF × 2.2
Cnew = 110 pF
Answer: 110 pF

4️⃣ Capacitors in Series and Parallel 🔗

A. Series Connection

  ┌─────┬─────┬─────┐
  │ C₁ │ C₂ │ C₃ │
  └──┬──┴──┬──┴──┬──┘
     │     │     │
     └─────┴─────┘
  • Same charge on all capacitors: Q = Q₁ = Q₂ = Q₃
  • Total voltage adds up: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃
1/Ceq = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + ...

⚠️ Result is LESS than the smallest individual capacitor!

B. Parallel Connection

       ┌─────────┐
   ───┤   C₁   ├───
       ├─────────┤
   ───┤   C₂   ├───
       ├─────────┤
   ───┤   C₃   ├───
       └─────────┘
  • Same voltage across all: V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃
  • Total charge adds up: Q = Q₁ + Q₂ + Q₃
Ceq = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + ...

✅ Result is the SUM – always larger than any individual capacitor!

🔢 Calculation 3: Series Combination

Question: Three capacitors (2μF, 4μF, and 6μF) are connected in series to a 12V battery. Find the equivalent capacitance and charge on the 4μF capacitor.

1/Ceq = 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/6 = (6+3+2)/12 = 11/12
Ceq = 12/11 ≈ 1.09 μF

Total charge: Q = Ceq × V = 1.09 × 10⁻⁶ × 12
Q = 13.09 μC

In series, all capacitors have the same charge!
Answer: Ceq = 1.09 μF, Charge = 13.09 μC

🔢 Calculation 4: Parallel Combination

Question: Two capacitors (3μF and 6μF) are in parallel across a 9V supply. Calculate the total energy stored.

Ceq = 3 + 6 = 9 μF

E = ½CeqV²
E = 0.5 × 9 × 10⁻⁶ × 9²
E = 0.5 × 9 × 10⁻⁶ × 81
E = 364.5 × 10⁻⁶ J = 364.5 μJ
Answer: 364.5 μJ

🔢 Calculation 5: Complex Circuit

Question: In the circuit below, find the equivalent capacitance between points X and Y: C₁ = 6μF and C₂ = 3μF in series, with this combination in parallel with C₃ = 4μF.

First, series combination of C₁ and C₂:
1/Cseries = 1/6 + 1/3 = (1+2)/6 = 3/6 = 1/2
Cseries = 2 μF

Now parallel with C₃:
Ceq = 2 + 4 = 6 μF
Answer: 6 μF

5️⃣ Energy Stored in a Capacitor 💾

A charged capacitor stores electrical potential energy in its electric field.

Three Equivalent Formulas:

E = ½QV = ½CV² = Q²/2C

Where E = Energy stored (Joules, J)

💡 Use the version that matches the given variables!

6️⃣ Types and Uses of Capacitors 🔧

Common Types

Type Dielectric Features Uses
Paper Oiled paper Cheap, general purpose Coupling circuits
Mica Mica mineral Stable, high voltage Radio frequency
Ceramic Ceramic material Small size, cheap High frequency, bypass
Electrolytic Electrolyte High capacitance, polarized Power supply filtering
Variable Air/plastic Adjustable capacitance Tuning radios

Key Functions/Uses

  1. Energy storage (camera flashes, defibrillators)
  2. Smoothing/filtering (removing ripples in power supplies)
  3. Coupling (allowing AC but blocking DC between circuits)
  4. Tuning (radio frequency selection)
  5. Timing circuits (with resistors in RC circuits)
  6. Power factor correction (in electrical motors)

🎯 JAMB Exam Tips

  1. Series: 1/C = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ → Result is smaller than smallest

  2. Parallel: C = C₁ + C₂ → Result is larger than largest

  3. Energy: Always positive, stored in the electric field

  4. Dielectric insertion: Increases capacitance by factor εᵣ

  5. Disconnected battery: Charge Q remains constant if dielectric changed

  6. Connected battery: Voltage V remains constant if dielectric changed

  7. Quick Check: If plate separation increases while connected to battery:
    • C decreases (C ∝ 1/d)
    • Q decreases (Q = CV, V constant)
    • E decreases (E = ½CV²)

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Forgetting units conversion (mm to m, μF to F)
  • ❌ Confusing series (reciprocal sum) with parallel (direct sum)
  • ❌ Using wrong energy formula for given variables
  • ❌ Forgetting that series capacitors have same charge
  • ❌ Forgetting that parallel capacitors have same voltage
  • ❌ Mixing up disconnected vs connected battery scenarios
