🧪 Separation of Mixtures & Purification

Elements vs compounds vs mixtures • Physical vs chemical changes • Purity tests • Separation methods (with diagrams)

🧱 (a) Elements, Compounds & Mixtures

A quick visual: elements (one type), compounds (chemically joined), mixtures (physically mixed)

Element

Definition: A pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. It contains only one type of atom.

Examples

  • Oxygen (O), Iron (Fe), Gold (Au), Hydrogen (H), Carbon (C)

Compound

Definition: A pure substance formed when two or more elements combine chemically in a fixed ratio by mass. It can only be separated by chemical reactions.

Examples

  • Water (H₂O), Sodium chloride (NaCl), Carbon dioxide (CO₂), Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)

Mixture

Definition: A physical combination of two or more substances in any proportion. Components keep their properties and can be separated by physical methods.

Homogeneous
  • Uniform throughout
  • Examples: salt solution, air, brass
Heterogeneous
  • Not uniform
  • Examples: sand + water, oil + water, soil
🧠 Easy Memory

Element = one type of atom • Compound = fixed chemical ratio • Mixture = any ratio (physical mix)

🔁 (b) Physical vs Chemical Changes

Physical change: no new substance • Chemical change: new substance formed

Physical Change

No new substance is formed—only state/shape/size changes. Usually reversible.

Examples

  • Melting ice, boiling water, dissolving sugar, crushing chalk

Chemical Change

New substance(s) formed with new properties. Often irreversible and involves big energy change.

Examples

  • Burning paper, rusting iron, digestion, iron + sulphur reaction

How to tell quickly: If a new substance forms (gas, colour change, precipitate, heat/light) → chemical change.

🧼 (c) Pure & Impure Substances

Pure substance: fixed composition (element/compound). Has sharp melting and boiling points.

Impure substance: mixture. Melting point becomes lower and wider range; boiling point becomes higher and wider range.

Melting point determination is a common lab test for purity

🔥 (d) Purity Criteria (mp, bp, density)

  • Pure: fixed mp/bp (e.g., ice melts at 0°C; water boils at 100°C at 1 atm)
  • Impure: mp depressed (lower + range), bp elevated (higher + range)
  • Density: pure substances have constant density; impurities change it

🧠 Quick Rule

Always ask: “What physical property is different?” Then choose the method.

💨 Evaporation

Principle: solvent evaporates (volatile), leaving non-volatile solute behind.

Used for: getting soluble solid from solution (e.g., salt from brine).

Real-life evaporation: salt is obtained from seawater in evaporation ponds

🌡️ Simple Distillation

Principle: separates liquids with a large boiling point difference (about >25–30°C).

Simple distillation setup (basic labelled diagram)

Parts (know these)

  • Flask: holds mixture
  • Thermometer: measures vapour temperature
  • Liebig condenser: cools vapour to liquid
  • Receiver: collects distillate

⚠️ Exam Tip

  • If boiling points are close, do NOT use simple distillation → use fractional distillation.

🏭 Fractional Distillation

Principle: separates miscible liquids with close boiling points using a fractionating column (many repeated vaporization-condensation steps).

Fractional distillation: includes a fractionating column

Used for

  • Crude oil fractions (petrol, kerosene, diesel)
  • Liquid air separation (N₂, O₂)
Crude oil is separated into fractions by boiling point

🌫️ Sublimation

Principle: some solids change directly from solid → gas on heating, then condense back to solid on cooling.

Sublimation separates sublimable solids (iodine, naphthalene) from non-sublimable solids

Example: iodine/naphthalene from sand.

💎 Recrystallization

Principle: solubility increases with temperature. Dissolve impure solid in hot solvent → cool → pure crystals form.

Recrystallization steps: dissolve hot → filter → cool → crystals

Used for

  • Purifying solids like sugar, salts (e.g., copper(II) sulphate)

🖍️ Paper Chromatography

Principle: different components travel at different speeds due to different solubility in solvent (mobile phase) and adsorption on paper (stationary phase).

Paper chromatography separates dyes/pigments into different spots

Used for: dyes, inks, plant pigments, amino acids.

Rf value (quick meaning)

  • Rf helps compare how far a spot moved compared to solvent front.

🧱 Column Chromatography

Principle: similar to paper chromatography but uses a packed column (silica/alumina) for larger-scale separation.

Column chromatography: useful for complex mixtures and larger quantities

🧲 Magnetization

Principle: separates magnetic substances from non-magnetic substances.

Magnetic separation: iron filings can be removed from sand/sulphur

Example: iron filings from sulphur or sand.

🫗 Decantation & 🧻 Filtration

Decantation

Principle: based on density/settling. Pour off the top liquid carefully.

  • Used for sediment + liquid (e.g., water from settled sand) or immiscible liquids

Filtration

Principle: particle size difference. Filter traps insoluble solid (residue); liquid passes (filtrate).

Filtration: residue stays on paper, filtrate passes through

Example: sand from water; coffee filtering.

🌀 Centrifugation

Principle: rapid spinning creates a force that makes denser particles settle faster.

Centrifugation separates components by density (e.g., blood cells from plasma)

Used for

  • Separating cream from milk
  • Separating blood into plasma and cells

🏠 Everyday / Real-Life Applications

  • Water purification: filtration + distillation
  • Petrol/diesel: fractional distillation of crude oil
  • Food colours/inks: paper chromatography
  • Salt production: evaporation
  • Recycling: magnetization to separate metals
🧠 Fast Exam Strategy

When asked “How would you separate…?”, write: property used → method → brief steps → example.
