Separation of Mixtures and Purification of Chemical Substances

(a) Elements, Compounds and Mixtures

Element ‚Äî A pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. It consists of only one type of atom.

Examples: Oxygen (O), Iron (Fe), Gold (Au), Hydrogen (H), Carbon (C).

Compound ‚Äî A pure substance formed when two or more different elements combine chemically in a fixed ratio by mass. It has new properties different from its elements and can only be separated by chemical reactions.

Examples: Water (H‚ÇÇO), Sodium chloride (NaCl), Carbon dioxide (CO‚ÇÇ), Glucose (C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÇO‚ÇÜ).

Mixture ‚Äî A physical combination of two or more substances (elements or compounds) in any proportion. Components keep their individual properties and can be separated by physical methods.

Types:

  • Homogeneous (uniform composition throughout) ‚Äî e.g., salt solution, air, alloys like brass.
  • Heterogeneous (non-uniform) ‚Äî e.g., sand + water, oil + water, soil.

Key Differences:

  • Elements & compounds = pure, fixed composition.
  • Mixtures = impure, variable composition.

(b) Chemical and Physical Changes

Physical Change ‚Äî No new substance is formed. Only physical properties change (e.g., state, shape, size). Usually easy to reverse; small energy change.

Examples: Melting of ice, Boiling of water, Dissolving sugar in water, Crushing chalk.

Chemical Change ‚Äî One or more new substances with different properties are formed. Usually difficult or impossible to reverse; large energy change (heat, light, gas, sound).

Examples: Burning of paper, Rusting of iron, Digestion of food, Reaction of iron with sulphur.

How to Distinguish:

  • Physical: Reversible, no new substance.
  • Chemical: Irreversible (often), new substance formed.

(c) Pure and Impure Substances

Pure substance ‚Äî Has definite/fixed composition and properties (element or compound). Sharp and fixed melting point and boiling point.

Examples: Distilled water, Pure sodium chloride, Pure oxygen gas.

Impure substance ‚Äî A mixture containing more than one component. Melting point is depressed (lower) and occurs over a range. Boiling point is elevated (higher) and occurs over a range.

Examples: Tap water, Sea water, Crude oil.

(d) Boiling, Density, Freezing and Melting Points

These properties are used as criteria for purity:

  • Pure substances have sharp, fixed melting point (mp) and boiling point (bp) at standard pressure (1 atm).
  • Example: Pure ice melts sharply at 0¬∞C; pure water boils at 100¬∞C.
  • Impure substances show: Melting point range (depressed and broader). Boiling point range (elevated and broader).
  • Density: Pure substances have constant density; impurities change it.

Practical Use: Melting point determination is a common lab test for purity of solids.

(e) Separation Processes

Separation depends on differences in physical properties (e.g., boiling point, solubility, particle size, density, magnetism, sublimation).

Evaporation

Principle: Solvent is volatile and evaporates, leaving non-volatile solute.

Used for: Soluble solids from solutions.

Example: Obtaining salt from seawater.

Simple Distillation

Principle: Large difference in boiling points (>25‚Äì30¬∞C). Lower-boiling liquid vaporizes first, condenses separately.

Used for: Miscible liquids with big bp difference.

Example: Separating water from ethanol (rough separation).

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Simple Distillation):

  • Round-bottom flask: Contains mixture
  • Thermometer: Measures vapor temperature
  • Liebig condenser: Cools vapor to liquid
  • Receiver: Collects distilled liquid

Fractional Distillation

Principle: Small difference in boiling points (<25‚Äì30¬∞C). Uses fractionating column for repeated vaporization-condensation cycles.

Used for: Crude oil fractions (petrol, kerosene, diesel), separating liquid air (N‚ÇÇ, O‚ÇÇ).

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Fractional Distillation):

  • Fractionating column: Repeated condensation/vaporization
  • Round-bottom flask: Mixture heated
  • Thermometer: Monitors temperature
  • Condenser: Cools vapor
  • Fraction receiver: Collects fractions

Sublimation

Principle: Solid sublimes directly to vapour on heating, then condenses on cooling.

Used for: Sublimable solids from non-sublimable ones.

Example: Iodine or naphthalene from sand.

Recrystallization (Simple / Fractional Crystallization)

Principle: Solubility increases with temperature; cool hot solution to form pure crystals (impurities remain dissolved).

Used for: Purifying solids.

Example: Purifying impure sugar or salts.

Paper Chromatography

Principle: Components have different solubilities and adsorption on paper; travel at different rates (different Rf values).

Used for: Separating dyes, plant pigments, amino acids.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Paper Chromatography):

  • Chromatography paper: Stationary phase
  • Solvent: Mobile phase rises by capillary action
  • Spots: Separated components (different Rf)
  • Origin line: Starting point of sample

Column Chromatography

Principle: Similar to paper but uses packed column with adsorbent (e.g., alumina, silica).

Used for: Larger-scale or complex mixture separation.

Magnetization

Principle: Difference in magnetic properties.

Used for: Magnetic substances from non-magnetic.

Example: Iron filings from sulphur or sand.

Decantation

Principle: Difference in density or settling.

Used for: Immiscible liquids or sediment from liquid.

Example: Pouring off water from settled sand.

Filtration

Principle: Particle size difference; filter traps solids, liquid passes through.

Used for: Insoluble solids from liquids.

Example: Sand from water, coffee making.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Filtration):

  • Filter paper / funnel: Traps solid residue
  • Residue: Insoluble solid left on filter
  • Filtrate: Liquid that passes through
  • Beaker: Collects filtrate

Centrifugation

Principle: Rapid spinning creates centrifugal force; denser particles settle faster.

Used for: Emulsions, suspensions, blood components.

Example: Separating cream from milk.
