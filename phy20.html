1️⃣ Production and Propagation of Waves 🌊

What is Wave Motion?

A wave is a disturbance that travels through a medium, transferring energy from one point to another without transferring matter.

💡 Key Concept: Imagine spectators doing "the wave" in a stadium—people stay in place but the disturbance moves around. This is exactly how waves work!

Vibrating Systems as Sources

  • Plucked guitar string → sound waves
  • Dropped pebble in water → water ripples
  • Radio transmitter → electromagnetic waves

Waves as Mode of Energy Transfer

Waves transfer energy, not matter. When you shout:

  • Energy travels through air to listener's ears
  • Air molecules oscillate but don't travel from your mouth to their ear

Particle Motion vs. Wave Motion

Particle Motion Wave Motion
Individual particles oscillate about fixed positions The disturbance travels through the medium
Particles don't travel far Energy travels long distances
Example: Water molecule moves up and down Example: The ripple spreads outward

The Fundamental Wave Equation ⭐

V = fλ

Where:
• V = wave velocity (speed) in m/s
• f = frequency in Hz (cycles per second)
• λ (lambda) = wavelength in meters

Memory tip: "Very Fast Lambda" or "Velocity = Frequency × Lambda"

Phase Difference, Wave Number, and Wave Vector

Phase Difference (φ): The difference in position between two points on a wave at the same time, measured in degrees or radians.

  • 0° or 0 rad = in phase (points at same position)
  • 180° or π rad = completely out of phase
  • 360° or 2π rad = one full wavelength apart (back in phase)
Wave Number (k):
k = 2π/λ
Unit: rad/m

Wave Vector: Points in direction of wave propagation with magnitude equal to wave number.

Progressive Wave Equation ⭐

y = A sin[(2π/λ)(vt ± x)]

Or equivalently:
y = A sin(ωt ± kx)

Where:
• y = displacement at position x and time t
• A = amplitude (maximum displacement)
• v = wave velocity
• t = time
• x = position
• ω (omega) = angular frequency = 2πf
• Use (+) for waves traveling in negative x-direction
• Use (−) for waves traveling in positive x-direction

2️⃣ Classification of Waves 🔄

Mechanical vs. Electromagnetic Waves

Mechanical Waves Electromagnetic Waves
Need a material medium Can travel through vacuum
Examples: sound, water waves, waves on strings Examples: light, radio waves, X-rays
Travel slower (sound: 340 m/s) Travel at speed of light (3 × 10⁸ m/s)

Longitudinal vs. Transverse Waves

Longitudinal Waves:

  • Particle vibration is parallel to wave direction
  • Consist of compressions and rarefactions
  • Examples: sound waves, waves in a slinky (pushed)

Transverse Waves:

  • Particle vibration is perpendicular to wave direction
  • Consist of crests and troughs
  • Examples: light waves, waves on a string, water waves

Stationary (Standing) vs. Progressive Waves

Progressive Waves Stationary (Standing) Waves
Travel through medium Do not travel; appear to stand still
Energy is transmitted Energy is stored (no net transfer)
Same amplitude everywhere Amplitude varies (nodes and antinodes)
Example: wave on an open string Example: wave on string fixed at both ends
  • Nodes: Points of zero amplitude (no movement)
  • Antinodes: Points of maximum amplitude
  • Distance between consecutive nodes = λ/2

Examples from Different Sources

Source Type of Wave Observation
Spring (slinky) Longitudinal (pushed) / Transverse (shaken) Compression or sideways oscillation
Rope/Stretched string Transverse Up-and-down or side-to-side motion
Ripple tank Transverse (water waves) Circular patterns from point source
Tuning fork Longitudinal (sound) Creates compressions in air

3️⃣ Characteristics/Properties of Waves 🎨

Reflection, Refraction, Diffraction, and Polarization

  • Reflection: Wave bounces off a surface. Angle of incidence = Angle of reflection. Echo is reflection of sound.
  • Refraction: Wave changes direction when entering different medium (due to change in speed). Light bends when entering water.
  • Diffraction: Wave spreads out when passing through openings or around obstacles. More noticeable when opening size ≈ wavelength.
  • Plane Polarization: (Transverse waves only) Restricting vibrations to one plane. Polaroid sunglasses use this principle.

Superposition of Waves (Interference)

When two waves meet, the resultant displacement equals the sum of individual displacements.

  • Constructive Interference: Waves in phase → Amplitude increases (bright spots/loud sound)
  • Destructive Interference: Waves out of phase → Amplitude decreases (dark spots/quiet sound)

Beats ⭐

When two waves of slightly different frequencies interfere, they produce alternating loud and soft sounds called beats.

Beat Frequency:
f_beat = |f₁ - f₂|

Applications:
• Tuning musical instruments
• Police radar speed detection
• Medical ultrasound

Doppler Effect (Qualitative)

The apparent change in frequency when source and observer move relative to each other.

Scenario Observed Frequency Example
Source approaching Higher (f' > f) Ambulance siren approaching
Source receding Lower (f' < f) Ambulance siren moving away
Source stationary, observer moving Changes accordingly Passenger in moving car

✅ Real-World Applications

  • Radar speed guns
  • Medical Doppler ultrasound
  • Astronomy (redshift of galaxies)

🔢 8 Essential Calculations for JAMB

Calculation 1: Basic Wave Equation

Question: A wave has frequency 50 Hz and wavelength 2 m. Calculate its velocity.

Using V = fλ:
V = 50 × 2 = 100 m/s
Answer: 100 m/s

Calculation 2: Finding Wavelength

Question: A radio wave travels at 3 × 10⁸ m/s with frequency 100 MHz. Find its wavelength.

Convert frequency: f = 100 MHz = 100 × 10⁶ Hz = 10⁸ Hz

Using λ = V/f:
λ = (3 × 10⁸)/(10⁸) = 3 m
Answer: 3 m

Calculation 3: Progressive Wave Parameters

Question: A wave is described by y = 0.5 sin[2π(4t - 0.5x)]. Find: (a) Amplitude, (b) Frequency, (c) Wavelength, (d) Wave velocity.

Compare with y = A sin[2π(ft - x/λ)]:

Given: y = 0.5 sin[2π(4t - 0.5x)] = 0.5 sin[2π(4t - x/2)]

(a) Amplitude A = 0.5 m
(b) Frequency f = 4 Hz
(c) From x/2: 1/λ = 0.5, so λ = 2 m
(d) V = fλ = 4 × 2 = 8 m/s
Answer: (a) 0.5 m, (b) 4 Hz, (c) 2 m, (d) 8 m/s

Calculation 4: Phase Difference

Question: Two points on a wave are separated by 0.5 m. The wavelength is 2 m. Calculate the phase difference.

Using φ = (2π/λ) × Δx:
φ = (2π/2) × 0.5
φ = π × 0.5 = π/2 rad = 90°
Answer: π/2 rad (or 90°)

Calculation 5: Beat Frequency

Question: Two tuning forks produce sounds of frequencies 256 Hz and 260 Hz. What is the beat frequency?

Using f_beat = |f₁ - f₂|:
f_beat = |256 - 260| = 4 Hz

This means 4 beats (loud-soft cycles) per second
Answer: 4 Hz

Calculation 6: Standing Waves on a String

Question: A string of length 1 m is fixed at both ends. Wave speed is 100 m/s. Find the fundamental frequency.

For fundamental mode: λ = 2L = 2 × 1 = 2 m

Using f = V/λ:
f = 100/2 = 50 Hz
Answer: 50 Hz

Calculation 7: Wave Number and Angular Frequency

Question: A wave has wavelength 0.5 m and frequency 100 Hz. Calculate: (a) Wave number, (b) Angular frequency, (c) Wave speed.

(a) Wave number: k = 2π/λ = 2π/0.5 = 4π ≈ 12.57 rad/m

(b) Angular frequency: ω = 2πf = 2π × 100 = 200π ≈ 628.3 rad/s

(c) Wave speed: V = fλ = 100 × 0.5 = 50 m/s

Verify: V = ω/k = 200π/4π = 50 m/s ✓
Answer: (a) 4π rad/m, (b) 200π rad/s, (c) 50 m/s

Calculation 8: Sound Wave Parameters

Question: A sound wave in air has frequency 440 Hz. Speed of sound is 340 m/s. Calculate: (a) Wavelength, (b) Period, (c) Oscillations in 2 seconds.

(a) Wavelength: λ = V/f = 340/440 = 0.773 m ≈ 77.3 cm

(b) Period: T = 1/f = 1/440 = 0.00227 s ≈ 2.27 ms

(c) Number of oscillations: N = f × t = 440 × 2 = 880 complete oscillations
Answer: (a) 0.773 m, (b) 2.27 ms, (c) 880 oscillations

📋 Key Formulas to Memorize

• V = fλ (Wave velocity)
• k = 2π/λ (Wave number)
• ω = 2πf (Angular frequency)
• f_beat = |f₁ - f₂| (Beat frequency)
• y = A sin(ωt ± kx) (Progressive wave)
• φ = (2π/λ) × Δx (Phase difference)

Wave number rad/m
