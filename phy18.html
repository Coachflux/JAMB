📚 Chapter 18: Structure of Matter and Kinetic Theory

🎯 Learning Objectives: Differentiate atoms and molecules, use molecular theory to explain Brownian motion, diffusion, surface tension, capillarity, adhesion, cohesion, and angle of contact, examine kinetic theory assumptions, interpret kinetic theory to explain gas laws and phase changes.

1️⃣ Atoms and Molecules ⚛️

Term Definition Key Points
Atom Smallest particle of an element that can take part in a chemical reaction Cannot be divided by chemical means; consists of protons, neutrons, electrons
Molecule Smallest particle of a substance that can exist independently and retain chemical properties Monoatomic (He), diatomic (O₂), or polyatomic (H₂O)
💡 Memory Tip: Atom = single unit; Molecule = group of atoms bonded together

2️⃣ Molecular Theory (Kinetic Molecular Theory) 🔬

This theory explains how tiny, invisible particles (molecules) behave and cause observable phenomena.

a) Brownian Motion

  • What it is: Random, zigzag movement of tiny particles suspended in a fluid
  • Explanation: Molecules of fluid are in constant, random motion and collide with suspended particles
  • Evidence for: Molecules are in continuous motion
  • Discovered by: Robert Brown (botanist) in 1827

b) Diffusion

  • What it is: Spreading of molecules from high concentration to low concentration until uniform distribution
  • Examples: Perfume smell spreading, ink in water, aroma of food cooking
  • Factors affecting: Temperature (higher = faster), molecular mass (lighter = faster), medium (gas > liquid > solid)

c) Surface Tension

  • What it is: Force acting per unit length at right angles to an imaginary line on liquid surface
  • Explanation: Molecules at surface are pulled inward by cohesive forces, creating a "skin" effect
  • Applications: Insects walking on water, droplets forming spheres, capillary action

d) Capillarity (Capillary Action)

  • What it is: Rise or fall of liquids in narrow tubes
  • Explanation: Balance between cohesive forces (liquid-liquid) and adhesive forces (liquid-solid)
  • Examples: Water rising in glass tube (adhesion > cohesion), mercury falling in glass (cohesion > adhesion), water in plant stems, blotting paper

e) Adhesion vs. Cohesion

Property Definition Example
Adhesion Attraction between molecules of different substances Water sticking to glass; glue on paper
Cohesion Attraction between molecules of same substance Water droplets forming spheres; mercury globules

f) Angle of Contact

  • Definition: Angle between tangent to liquid surface and solid surface, measured inside the liquid
  • Water-glass: Acute angle (< 90°) — liquid wets the surface
  • Mercury-glass: Obtuse angle (> 90°) — liquid doesn't wet the surface

3️⃣ Real-Life Applications 🌍

Concept Real-Life Application
Surface tension Detergents reducing surface tension to clean; waterproofing materials
Capillarity Oil rising in lamp wicks; water absorption by towels
Diffusion Exchange of gases in lungs; making tea
Adhesion Painting walls; sticking tape; welding
Cohesion Formation of water streams; droplet formation

4️⃣ Kinetic Theory of Gases 🎲

Assumptions of Kinetic Theory

  1. Gases consist of tiny particles in continuous, random motion
  2. Volume of molecules is negligible compared to container volume
  3. No intermolecular forces except during collisions
  4. Collisions are perfectly elastic (no kinetic energy lost)
  5. Average kinetic energy is directly proportional to absolute temperature

5️⃣ Explaining Physical Phenomena 🌡️

a) Pressure Exerted by a Gas

  • Explanation: Gas pressure results from molecules colliding with container walls
  • Formula: P = ⅓ρc² where ρ = density, c = root mean square velocity
  • Factors increasing pressure: More molecules, higher temperature, smaller volume

b) Boyle's Law (Kinetic Theory Explanation)

Statement: For fixed mass of gas at constant temperature, pressure is inversely proportional to volume
Formula: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂ or P ∝ 1/V

Kinetic theory explanation: When volume decreases, molecules hit walls more frequently, increasing pressure

c) Charles's Law (Kinetic Theory Explanation)

Statement: For fixed mass of gas at constant pressure, volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature
Formula: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂ or V ∝ T

Kinetic theory explanation: Higher temperature = faster molecular motion = molecules push harder on walls = volume expands to maintain constant pressure

d) Melting (Solid → Liquid)

When heated, molecules vibrate more vigorously until they break free from fixed positions but remain close together.

e) Boiling (Liquid → Gas)

  • Occurs throughout the liquid at boiling point
  • Molecules gain enough energy to overcome atmospheric pressure and intermolecular forces
  • Forms bubbles of vapor throughout the liquid

f) Vaporization/Evaporation

  • Occurs at the surface at any temperature
  • Fast-moving molecules at surface escape into the air
  • Cooling effect: Escaping molecules take away heat energy, lowering temperature of remaining liquid (why sweating cools you down)

g) Temperature Changes

  • Temperature is a measure of average kinetic energy of molecules
  • Increase in temperature = molecules move faster (higher kinetic energy)
  • Decrease in temperature = molecules move slower (lower kinetic energy)

🔢 Sample Calculation

Charles's Law Application

Question: A gas occupies 300 cm³ at 27°C. What is its volume at 127°C if pressure is constant?

Solution:
Convert to Kelvin: T₁ = 27 + 273 = 300 K, T₂ = 127 + 273 = 400 K
Using Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
300/300 = V₂/400
V₂ = 400 cm³
Answer: 400 cm³

⚠️ Common JAMB Exam Traps

  • ❌ Confusing atoms and molecules: O₂ is a molecule (two atoms), O is an atom
  • ❌ Diffusion vs. Osmosis: Diffusion is general molecular spreading; osmosis is specific to water through semi-permeable membrane
  • ❌ Evaporation vs. Boiling: Evaporation happens at any temperature at surface; boiling at specific temperature throughout liquid
  • ❌ Adhesion vs. Cohesion: Adhesion = different substances; Cohesion = same substance
  • ❌ Assumptions of kinetic theory: Remember molecules have negligible volume and no intermolecular forces (in ideal gases)
