    Step 1: What is Solubility? (Definition you MUST know)

    Solubility is the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve in 1 dm³ (1000 cm³) of solvent at a given temperature to form a saturated solution.

    It is expressed in mol/dm³ (moles per cubic decimetre) – exactly as the JAMB syllabus and New School Chemistry require.

    Step 2: Types of Solutions

    The book classifies solutions into three types based on how much solute is dissolved:

    Type Meaning Can more solute dissolve? Stability
    Unsaturated Less solute than the maximum possible at that temperature Yes Stable
    Saturated Exactly the maximum amount of solute that can dissolve at that temperature No Stable
    Supersaturated More solute than the saturated amount (usually made by cooling a hot saturated solution slowly without disturbance) No (but unstable) Unstable – crystals separate easily on shaking or adding a crystal

    Easy way to remember: Unsaturated = “room for more”, Saturated = “full”, Supersaturated = “over-full and nervous” (it will crash out crystals).

    Step 3: Solubility Curves

    New School Chemistry shows solubility curves as graphs of solubility (mol/dm³) on the y-axis against temperature (°C) on the x-axis.

    • If the curve slopes upwards (e.g., KNO₃, NaNO₃): solubility increases with temperature → dissolution is endothermic.
    • If the curve slopes downwards (e.g., CaSO₄, Li₂CO₃): solubility decreases with temperature → dissolution is exothermic.
    • If almost flat (e.g., NaCl): solubility changes very little with temperature.

    Simple deductions you can make (JAMB loves these):

    • Solubility is temperature-dependent.
    • To recrystallise a substance, dissolve it in minimum hot solvent and cool.
    • You can compare solubilities of different substances at the same temperature.

    Step 4: Simple Calculations

    Solubility (mol/dm³) = moles of solute / volume of solution in dm³

    Example 1 (Mass → mol/dm³)

    At 40 °C, 20.0 g of potassium nitrate (KNO₃, molar mass = 101 g/mol) dissolves in 250 cm³ of water. Calculate solubility.

    Moles = 20.0 / 101 = 0.198 mol
    Volume = 0.250 dm³
    Solubility = 0.198 / 0.250 = 0.792 mol/dm³

    Example 2 (Finding mass)

    Solubility of NaCl at 25 °C is 6.15 mol/dm³. What mass dissolves in 400 cm³? (Molar mass = 58.5 g/mol)

    Moles = 6.15 × 0.400 = 2.46 mol
    Mass = 2.46 × 58.5 = 143.91 g

    Step 5: Solvents for Fats, Oils, Perspiration and Paints + Stain Removal

    “Like dissolves like”

    • Fats and oils (non-polar) → petrol, kerosene, tetrachloromethane (CCl₄), dry-cleaning fluid.
    • Perspiration stains → water + detergent or alcohol.
    • Oil-based paints → turpentine, white spirit or xylene.

    Stain removal tips (JAMB favourite):

    • Grease/oil stains → petrol or dry-cleaning solvent, then wash with detergent.
    • Paint stains → turpentine or white spirit (never water).
    • Ball-point pen ink → methylated spirit.

    Step 6: True Solutions vs False Solutions (Suspensions & Colloids)

    New School Chemistry separates them by particle size:

    Property True Solution Suspension (False) Colloid (False)
    Particle size < 1 nm > 1000 nm 1 nm – 1000 nm
    Appearance Clear, transparent Cloudy, opaque Usually clear but scatters light
    Tyndall effect No No Yes
    Settling on standing Never Yes No
    Pass through filter paper Yes No Yes

    Examples:

    • Suspensions: Harmattan haze, water paints.
    • Colloids: Fog (aerosol), milk (emulsion), blood, emulsion paints, rubber solution.
