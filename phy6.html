Friction

Friction is a force that opposes relative motion between two surfaces in contact. It acts parallel to the surfaces and opposite to the direction of (or tendency for) motion.

(i) Static and Dynamic (Kinetic) Friction

Static friction (also called limiting friction when at maximum): This prevents an object from starting to move when a force is applied. It adjusts its value up to a maximum to match the applied force (as long as no motion occurs).

Maximum static friction = μₛ × N (where μₛ = coefficient of static friction, N = normal reaction).

Static friction is always ≥ kinetic friction for the same surfaces.

Dynamic (kinetic) friction: This acts when the object is already sliding. It is roughly constant and does not depend much on speed (for most cases in JAMB).

Kinetic friction = μₖ × N (μₖ = coefficient of kinetic friction).

Usually μₛ > μₖ.

(ii) Coefficient of Limiting Friction and Its Determination

The coefficient of limiting friction (μ) is the ratio of the maximum frictional force (f_max) to the normal reaction (N):

μ = f_max / N

It is a dimensionless number that depends on the nature of the two surfaces (e.g., rubber on concrete ≈ 0.6–0.9, ice on ice ≈ 0.03).

Determination (common JAMB experiment method):

  • Use an inclined plane. Gradually increase the angle θ until the object just starts to slide (limiting case).
  • At this point: mg sinθ = μ mg cosθ
  • μ = tanθ
  • (where θ is the angle of repose or limiting angle).

(iii) Advantages and Disadvantages of Friction

Advantages:

  • Enables walking (feet grip ground without slipping).
  • Allows vehicles to brake, accelerate, and turn (tires grip road).
  • Helps in holding objects, writing with pen/pencil, striking matches, etc.
  • Machines can transmit force (e.g., belts on pulleys).

Disadvantages:

  • Causes wear and tear on machine parts (reduces lifespan).
  • Wastes energy as heat (lowers efficiency).
  • Causes resistance to motion (more fuel needed for vehicles).
  • Produces unwanted heat in engines.

(iv) Reduction of Friction

  • Lubrication (oil, grease) → forms a thin film between surfaces.
  • Use ball bearings or rollers → change sliding to rolling (rolling friction << sliding friction).
  • Smooth/polish surfaces.
  • Streamline shapes (for fluids/air).
  • Use air cushions or magnetic levitation.

(v) Qualitative Treatment of Viscosity and Terminal Velocity

Viscosity is the internal friction in a fluid that opposes relative motion between layers (fluid's "stickiness").

High viscosity (e.g., honey) → flows slowly. Low viscosity (e.g., water) → flows easily.

Factors affecting viscosity: Temperature (viscosity decreases with rise in temperature for liquids), nature of fluid.

Terminal velocity: When an object falls through a fluid, initially it accelerates due to gravity. Drag (viscous) force increases with speed until drag + buoyancy = weight. Net force becomes zero → constant velocity called terminal velocity (v_t).

No further acceleration.

v_t depends on: size/mass of object, density difference, viscosity of fluid, shape (higher for streamlined).

(vi) Stoke’s Law

Stoke’s law gives the viscous drag force on a small spherical object moving slowly through a viscous fluid (laminar flow, low Reynolds number):

F_d = 6πη r v

where:

  • η = viscosity of fluid
  • r = radius of sphere
  • v = velocity

At terminal velocity, net force = 0:

Weight – buoyancy – drag = 0

m g – (4/3)π r³ σ g – 6π η r v_t = 0

(where σ = fluid density, ρ = object density)

Terminal velocity:

v_t = 2 r² g (ρ - σ) / (9 η)

(Assumes sphere is small, motion slow, no turbulence.)

10 JAMB-Style Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

1. An object of mass 80 kg is pulled on a horizontal rough ground by a force of 500 N just to start motion. Find the coefficient of static friction. (g = 10 m/s²)
A. 0.625 B. 0.5 C. 0.4 D. 0.8
Step 1: Normal force N = m g = 80 × 10 = 800 N.
Step 2: At limiting case, applied force = max static friction = μₛ N.
Step 3: 500 = μₛ × 800.
Step 4: μₛ = 500 / 800 = 0.625.
Step 5: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 0.625
2. A body of mass 36 kg falls through a viscous liquid which offers a drag force of 260 N when at terminal velocity. Find the upthrust (buoyancy). (g = 10 m/s²)
A. 360 N B. 100 N C. 260 N D. 0 N
Step 1: At terminal velocity, net force = 0.
Step 2: Weight = drag + buoyancy.
Step 3: Weight = m g = 36 × 10 = 360 N.
Step 4: 360 = 260 + U.
Step 5: U = 100 N.
Step 6: Matches option B.
Answer: B. 100 N
3. The coefficient of friction between a road and tire is 0.4. Find the maximum frictional force on a car of mass 1000 kg. (g = 10 m/s²)
A. 4000 N B. 1000 N C. 400 N D. 10000 N
Step 1: Normal force N = m g = 1000 × 10 = 10,000 N.
Step 2: Max friction = μ N = 0.4 × 10,000 = 4000 N.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 4000 N
4. A block on a horizontal surface requires 50 N to start moving and 40 N to keep moving at constant speed. Find μ_s and μ_k if mass = 20 kg. (g = 10 m/s²)
A. 0.25, 0.20 B. 0.20, 0.25 C. 0.5, 0.4 D. 2.5, 2.0
Step 1: N = m g = 20 × 10 = 200 N.
Step 2: μ_s = 50 / 200 = 0.25.
Step 3: μ_k = 40 / 200 = 0.20.
Step 4: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 0.25, 0.20
5. A sphere falls through a viscous fluid and reaches terminal velocity. If viscosity doubles, what happens to v_t? (other factors constant)
A. Doubles B. Halves C. Quadruples D. Remains same
Step 1: From v_t = 2 r² g (ρ - σ) / (9 η).
Step 2: v_t ∝ 1/η.
Step 3: Doubles viscosity → halves v_t.
Step 4: Matches option B.
Answer: B. Halves
6. According to Stoke’s law, drag force F ∝
A. r B. r² C. v D. v²
Step 1: F_d = 6π η r v.
Step 2: F ∝ v (and ∝ r).
Step 3: Matches option C.
Answer: C. v
7. A steel ball (density 7.8 g/cm³) falls in oil (density 0.9 g/cm³). If radius doubles, terminal velocity becomes?
A. Half B. Double C. 4 times D. 1/4 times
Step 1: v_t ∝ r².
Step 2: Radius double → v_t 4 times.
Step 3: Matches option C.
Answer: C. 4 times
8. Two spheres of same material fall in same fluid. Radius of one is twice the other. Ratio of terminal velocities (larger : smaller)?
A. 1:1 B. 2:1 C. 4:1 D. 1:4
Step 1: v_t ∝ r².
Step 2: Ratio = (2r)² / r² = 4/1.
Step 3: Matches option C.
Answer: C. 4:1
9. A raindrop reaches terminal velocity because
A. Weight = drag B. Acceleration increases C. Viscosity zero D. Buoyancy zero
Step 1: At terminal v, net force = 0.
Step 2: Weight = drag + buoyancy (approx weight = drag for small drops).
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. Weight = drag
10. A copper ball (radius 2 mm, density 8.9 × 10³ kg/m³) has terminal velocity 6.5 cm/s in oil (density 1.5 × 10³ kg/m³). Find viscosity of oil. (g = 10 m/s²)
A. 1.01 Pa·s B. 2.0 Pa·s C. 0.5 Pa·s D. 0.1 Pa·s
Step 1: v_t = 0.065 m/s, r = 0.002 m.
Step 2: v_t = 2 r² g (ρ - σ) / (9 η).
Step 3: 0.065 = 2 (0.002)² (10) (8900 - 1500) / (9 η).
Step 4: 0.065 = 2 × 4e-6 × 10 × 7400 / (9 η) = 0.592 / (9 η).
Step 5: η = 0.592 / (9 × 0.065) ≈ 1.01 Pa·s.
Step 6: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 1.01 Pa·s
