📚 Chapter 30: Electric Cells

🎯 Learning Objectives: Identify defects of simple voltaic cells, compare different cell types including solar cells, compare lead-acid and Nickel-iron accumulators, and solve problems involving series and parallel combinations of cells.

1️⃣ Simple Voltaic Cell & Its Defects 🔋

What is a Simple Voltaic Cell?

A simple voltaic cell is the earliest type of electric cell that converts chemical energy into electrical energy through redox reactions.

Components

  • Zinc rod: Negative electrode (anode)
  • Copper rod: Positive electrode (cathode)
  • Dilute sulfuric acid: Electrolyte

How It Works

  1. Zinc dissolves in acid, releasing electrons (oxidation): Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻
  2. Hydrogen ions from acid accept electrons at copper plate (reduction): 2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂ gas
  3. Electrons flow from Zn to Cu through external circuit = Electric Current

Two Major Defects

Defect Cause Effect Solution
Polarization Hydrogen gas bubbles accumulate on copper plate Reduces cell emf; stops current flow Use depolarizer (e.g., manganese dioxide) or brush off bubbles
Local Action Impurities (like carbon/iron) in zinc create tiny cells Zinc dissolves even when not connected; wastes zinc Amalgamate zinc with mercury (forms protective layer)

2️⃣ Types of Electric Cells 🔋

A. Daniel Cell (Primary Cell - Non-rechargeable)

  • Anode: Zinc rod in ZnSO₄ solution
  • Cathode: Copper rod in CuSO₄ solution
  • Separator: Porous pot prevents mixing but allows ion flow
  • Emf: 1.08V
  • Advantage: No polarization (no gas produced)
  • Disadvantage: Bulky; copper deposits on zinc over time

B. Leclanché Cell

Wet Type:

  • Anode: Zinc rod
  • Cathode: Carbon rod surrounded by MnO₂ + powdered carbon
  • Electrolyte: Ammonium chloride (NH₄Cl) solution
  • Depolarizer: MnO₂ (oxidizes hydrogen to water)
  • Emf: 1.5V

Dry Type (Modern batteries):

  • Same chemistry but electrolyte is a paste (not liquid)
  • Advantages: Portable; no spillage; maintenance-free
  • Uses: Torches, radios, toys

C. Lead-Acid Accumulator (Secondary Cell - Rechargeable)

  • Anode (−): Lead (Pb) plates
  • Cathode (+): Lead dioxide (PbO₂) plates
  • Electrolyte: Dilute sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄)
Discharging Reaction:
Pb + PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄ → 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O

Charging Reaction:
2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O → Pb + PbO₂ + 2H₂SO₄
  • Emf: 2.0V per cell (car battery = 6 cells = 12V)
  • Advantages: Rechargeable; high current output; low internal resistance
  • Disadvantages: Heavy; requires maintenance (topping up distilled water); corrosive acid

D. Nickel-Iron (NiFe) Accumulator (Edison Cell)

  • Anode: Iron (Fe)
  • Cathode: Nickel oxide (NiO₂)
  • Electrolyte: Potassium hydroxide (KOH) - alkaline

Comparison: Lead-Acid vs Nickel-Iron

Feature Lead-Acid Nickel-Iron
Weight Heavy Lighter
Durability Less durable More rugged/longer life
Maintenance Needs distilled water Minimal maintenance
Cost Cheaper More expensive
Efficiency Higher (80%) Lower (60%)
Emf 2.0V 1.2V

E. Modern Cells (JAMB 2026 includes these)

Lithium-Ion (Li-ion):

  • Advantages: High energy density; lightweight; rechargeable; no memory effect
  • Uses: Phones, laptops, electric vehicles

Mercury-Cadmium:

  • Advantages: Stable voltage; long shelf life
  • Disadvantage: Toxic (environmental hazard)

Solar Cells:

  • Convert sunlight directly to electricity using semiconductor materials (silicon)
  • Advantage: Renewable energy; no pollution

3️⃣ Maintenance of Cells & Batteries 🔧

For Lead-Acid Batteries

  1. Keep terminals clean - prevent corrosion with petroleum jelly
  2. Check electrolyte level regularly - top up with distilled water only (never tap water!)
  3. Keep battery charged - prevents sulfation (PbSO₄ crystallization)
  4. Avoid overcharging - causes excessive gassing and water loss
  5. Store in cool, dry place

For Dry Cells

  • Store in cool, dry place
  • Remove from devices when not in use for long periods
  • Don't mix old and new batteries

4️⃣ Arrangement of Cells 🔗

Series Arrangement

  • Connection: Positive of one to negative of next
  • Total Emf: Etotal = E₁ + E₂ + E₃ + ...
  • Total Internal Resistance: rtotal = r₁ + r₂ + r₃ + ...
  • Current: Same through all cells
  • Use: When higher voltage is needed

Parallel Arrangement

  • Connection: All positives together, all negatives together
  • Total Emf: Etotal = E (same as one cell - must be identical cells!)
  • Total Internal Resistance: 1/rtotal = 1/r₁ + 1/r₂ + 1/r₃ + ...
  • Current: Shared among cells
  • Use: When higher current capacity or longer life needed
JAMB Exam Tip: For identical cells:
• Series: Etotal = nE; rtotal = nr
• Parallel: Etotal = E; rtotal = r/n

5️⃣ Efficiency of a Cell ⚡

Types of Efficiency

  1. Commercial Efficiency: η = (Total electrical energy output / Total chemical energy consumed) × 100%
  2. Ampere-Hour Efficiency: ηah = (Ampere-hours during discharge / Ampere-hours during charge) × 100%

Factors Affecting Efficiency

  • Internal resistance (lower = better)
  • Current drawn (higher current = lower efficiency due to "lost volts")
  • Temperature
  • Age/condition of cell

📋 Key Formulas for JAMB

Formula Meaning
E = V + Ir Emf = Terminal p.d. + Lost volts
E = I(R + r) Emf = Current × (External resistance + Internal resistance)
V = IR Terminal potential difference
Lost volts = Ir Voltage drop across internal resistance
η = (Output/Input) × 100% Efficiency
Where:
• E = Electromotive force (emf) in volts
• V = Terminal potential difference in volts
• I = Current in amperes
• R = External resistance in ohms
• r = Internal resistance in ohms

🔢 Calculation 1: Basic Emf, Terminal P.D., and Lost Volts

Question: A cell has an emf of 1.5V and internal resistance of 0.5Ω. It is connected to a resistor of 2.5Ω. Calculate: (a) The current, (b) The terminal p.d., (c) The lost volts.

Given: E = 1.5V, r = 0.5Ω, R = 2.5Ω

(a) Current:
I = E/(R + r) = 1.5/(2.5 + 0.5) = 1.5/3.0 = 0.5A

(b) Terminal p.d.:
V = IR = 0.5 × 2.5 = 1.25V
Or: V = E - Ir = 1.5 - (0.5 × 0.5) = 1.25V

(c) Lost volts:
Lost volts = Ir = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25V
Or: Lost volts = E - V = 1.5 - 1.25 = 0.25V
Answer: (a) 0.5A, (b) 1.25V, (c) 0.25V

🔢 Calculation 2: Finding Internal Resistance

Question: A cell of emf 2.0V gives a terminal p.d. of 1.6V when a current of 0.8A is drawn from it. Calculate the internal resistance.

Given: E = 2.0V, V = 1.6V, I = 0.8A

Using E = V + Ir
Ir = E - V
r = (E - V)/I = (2.0 - 1.6)/0.8 = 0.4/0.8 = 0.5Ω
Answer: 0.5Ω

🔢 Calculation 3: Cells in Series

Question: Three identical cells, each of emf 1.5V and internal resistance 0.2Ω, are connected in series to an external resistor of 4.4Ω. Calculate: (a) Total emf, (b) Total internal resistance, (c) Current, (d) Terminal p.d.

Given: E = 1.5V, r = 0.2Ω per cell, n = 3, R = 4.4Ω

(a) Total emf: Etotal = nE = 3 × 1.5 = 4.5V

(b) Total internal resistance: rtotal = nr = 3 × 0.2 = 0.6Ω

(c) Current: I = Etotal/(R + rtotal) = 4.5/(4.4 + 0.6) = 4.5/5.0 = 0.9A

(d) Terminal p.d.: V = IR = 0.9 × 4.4 = 3.96V
Or: V = Etotal - Irtotal = 4.5 - (0.9 × 0.6) = 3.96V
Answer: (a) 4.5V, (b) 0.6Ω, (c) 0.9A, (d) 3.96V

🔢 Calculation 4: Cells in Parallel

Question: Four identical cells, each of emf 2.0V and internal resistance 0.4Ω, are connected in parallel to an external resistor of 1.5Ω. Calculate: (a) Total emf, (b) Total internal resistance, (c) Current, (d) Current per cell.

Given: E = 2.0V, r = 0.4Ω per cell, n = 4, R = 1.5Ω

(a) Total emf: Etotal = E = 2.0V

(b) Total internal resistance:
1/rtotal = 4/r = 4/0.4 = 10
rtotal = 0.1Ω (or rtotal = r/n = 0.4/4 = 0.1Ω)

(c) Current: I = Etotal/(R + rtotal) = 2.0/(1.5 + 0.1) = 2.0/1.6 = 1.25A

(d) Current per cell: Iper cell = 1.25/4 = 0.3125A ≈ 0.31A
Answer: (a) 2.0V, (b) 0.1Ω, (c) 1.25A, (d) 0.31A

🔢 Calculation 5: Mixed Series-Parallel

Question: Six identical cells (E = 1.5V, r = 0.3Ω) are arranged in two parallel groups of three cells in series. Connected to R = 2.1Ω. Find: (a) Total emf, (b) Total internal resistance, (c) Current.

Given: 3 cells in series per branch, 2 branches in parallel

(a) Emf per branch: 3 × 1.5 = 4.5V
Two branches in parallel: Etotal = 4.5V

(b) Internal resistance per branch: 3 × 0.3 = 0.9Ω
Two branches in parallel: 1/rtotal = 1/0.9 + 1/0.9 = 2/0.9
rtotal = 0.9/2 = 0.45Ω

(c) Current: I = 4.5/(2.1 + 0.45) = 4.5/2.55 = 1.76A
Answer: (a) 4.5V, (b) 0.45Ω, (c) 1.76A

🔢 Calculation 6: Maximum Power Transfer

Question: A cell of emf 3.0V and internal resistance 0.5Ω is connected to a variable resistor R. Calculate: (a) R for maximum power transfer, (b) The maximum power delivered.

Given: E = 3.0V, r = 0.5Ω

(a) For maximum power transfer: R = r = 0.5Ω

(b) Current at maximum power:
I = E/(R + r) = 3.0/(0.5 + 0.5) = 3.0A

Power: P = I²R = (3.0)² × 0.5 = 9 × 0.5 = 4.5W

Or: Pmax = E²/4r = (3.0)²/(4 × 0.5) = 9/2 = 4.5W
Answer: (a) 0.5Ω, (b) 4.5W

🔢 Calculation 7: Efficiency

Question: A battery of emf 12V and internal resistance 0.4Ω delivers a current of 2A to an external resistor. Calculate: (a) Total power, (b) Useful power, (c) Efficiency.

Given: E = 12V, r = 0.4Ω, I = 2A

(a) Total power: Ptotal = EI = 12 × 2 = 24W

(b) Find R: E = I(R + r) → 12 = 2(R + 0.4) → R = 5.6Ω
Useful power: P = I²R = (2)² × 5.6 = 22.4W
Or: V = E - Ir = 12 - 0.8 = 11.2V, P = VI = 22.4W

(c) Efficiency: η = (22.4/24) × 100% = 93.3%
Or: η = [R/(R + r)] × 100% = [5.6/6.0] × 100% = 93.3%
Answer: (a) 24W, (b) 22.4W, (c) 93.3%

🔢 Calculation 8: Comparing Arrangements

Question: Four identical cells (E = 1.5V, r = 0.2Ω) available to light a bulb rated 3.0V, 0.3A. Which arrangement works best?

Bulb resistance: R = V/I = 3.0/0.3 = 10Ω

Series (4 cells):
Etotal = 6.0V, rtotal = 0.8Ω
I = 6.0/(10 + 0.8) = 0.56A → TOO HIGH (blows bulb)

Parallel (4 cells):
Etotal = 1.5V, rtotal = 0.05Ω
I = 1.5/(10 + 0.05) = 0.15A → TOO LOW (dim bulb)

Best: 2 series, 2 parallel
Etotal = 3.0V, rtotal = 0.2Ω
I = 3.0/(10 + 0.2) = 0.29A ≈ 0.3A ✓
Answer: Use 2 cells in series, with 2 such branches in parallel

🎯 Quick Memory Tips

  1. "Zinc-Copper-ACID" - Remember simple cell components

  2. "Polarization = Gas; Local Action = Impurities" - Distinguish the two defects

  3. "Primary = Use once; Secondary = Recharge" - Cell classification

  4. "Series = Add voltage; Parallel = Add current capacity" - Arrangement rule

  5. E = V + Ir - "Emf equals Volts plus Internal resistance times current"

⚠️ Common JAMB Trick Questions

  • Q: Why does terminal p.d. decrease as current increases?
    A: Because lost volts (Ir) increase, leaving less voltage for the external circuit.

  • Q: When is maximum power transferred?
    A: When R = r (external resistance equals internal resistance)

  • Q: What happens to emf in parallel?
    A: Emf remains the same as single cell (if identical), but total internal resistance decreases.
