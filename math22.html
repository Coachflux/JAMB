🔄 Permutations and Combinations

This chapter covers factorials, arrangements, and selections - a high-yield topic that appears frequently in JAMB CBT exams.

1️⃣ FACTORIAL NOTATION (!)

Foundation Concept 🏗️

Definition: n! = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × ... × 3 × 2 × 1
Special Case: 0! = 1 (always remember!)

JAMB Shortcut: n! = n × (n−1)! — useful for simplifying

🔢 Examples: Factorial Calculations

• 4! = 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 24
• 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120
• 6! = 720
💡 0! = 1 (This is a definition, not a calculation!)
2️⃣ PERMUTATIONS (ORDER MATTERS)

Linear Permutations 📏

Formula: nPr = n!(n−r)!

Where:
• n = total number of items
• r = number of items being arranged
• Order matters (AB is different from BA)

When to use: Arranging people in a line, forming numbers, letter arrangements, race positions

🔢 Example 1: Linear Arrangement

In how many ways can 5 students be arranged in a row of 3 seats?

5P3 = 5!(5−3)! = 5!2! = 1202 = 60 ways
Answer: 60 ways

🔢 Example 2: Number Formation

How many 3-digit numbers can be formed from digits 2, 3, 4, 5, 6 without repetition?

5P3 = 5!2! = 60 numbers
Answer: 60 numbers

Circular Permutations 🔄

Formula: (n−1)!

Key Concept: In a circle, there's no "first" position — arrangements are the same if they can be rotated into each other

🔢 Example 3: Circular Table

In how many ways can 6 people sit around a circular table?

(6−1)! = 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120 ways
Answer: 120 ways

Permutations with Repeated Objects 🔁

Formula: n!n1! × n2! × n3! ...

Where n1, n2, n3 are frequencies of identical objects

🔢 Example 4: Repeated Letters

How many ways can the letters of "BANANA" be arranged?

• Total letters: 6
• A appears 3 times, N appears 2 times, B appears 1 time

Number of ways = 6!3! × 2! × 1! = 7206 × 2 × 1 = 72012 = 60 ways
Answer: 60 ways
3️⃣ COMBINATIONS (ORDER DOESN'T MATTER)

Selection Problems 🎯

Formula: nCr = n!r!(n−r)!

Key Difference: Order doesn't matter (AB is the SAME as BA)

When to use: Selecting teams, choosing committees, forming groups, handshakes

🔢 Example 5: Committee Selection

In how many ways can a committee of 3 be chosen from 10 people?

10C3 = 10!3! × 7! = 10 × 9 × 83 × 2 × 1 = 7206 = 120 ways
Answer: 120 ways

🔢 Example 6: Mixed Selection

A team of 4 players is selected from 7 boys and 5 girls. How many ways if 2 must be boys and 2 must be girls?

Ways to choose 2 boys from 7: 7C2 = 7 × 62 × 1 = 21
Ways to choose 2 girls from 5: 5C2 = 5 × 42 × 1 = 10

Total ways = 21 × 10 = 210 ways
Answer: 210 ways
4️⃣ PERMUTATION vs COMBINATION

Complete Comparison Table 📊

Aspect PERMUTATION COMBINATION
Key Question "In how many ways can we ARRANGE...?" "In how many ways can we SELECT...?"
Order Matters (AB ≠ BA) Does NOT matter (AB = BA)
Formula nPr = n!(n−r)! nCr = n!r!(n−r)!
Memory Trick "P for Position/Place" "C for Choose/Committee"
Real-life Race positions, passwords, queue Teams, committees, handshakes
Value Always ≥ Combination Always ≤ Permutation
💡 Permutation = Combination × r! or Combination = Permutation ÷ r!
5️⃣ JAMB EXAM TIPS & TRAPS

How to Identify Which to Use 🎯

Keywords in Question Use
Arrange, order, line up, rank, position, queue, different patterns PERMUTATION
Select, choose, pick, committee, team, group, handshake COMBINATION

🔢 Example 7: JAMB-Style Problem (Committee)

A committee of 5 from 6 men and 4 women. Find committees if:

(a) no restrictions (b) exactly 2 women (c) at least 3 men

(a) No restrictions:
10C5 = 10!5!5! = 252 committees

(b) Exactly 2 women (so 3 men):
6C3 × 4C2 = 20 × 6 = 120 committees

(c) At least 3 men:
• Case 1: 3 men, 2 women → 6C3 × 4C2 = 20 × 6 = 120
• Case 2: 4 men, 1 woman → 6C4 × 4C1 = 15 × 4 = 60
• Case 3: 5 men, 0 women → 6C5 × 4C0 = 6 × 1 = 6
Total: 120 + 60 + 6 = 186 committees
Answers: (a) 252 (b) 120 (c) 186
📋 QUICK REFERENCE FORMULAS

All Formulas at a Glance 📚

Formula When to Apply
n! Basic factorial calculations
nPn = n! Arranging all n items linearly
nPr = n!(n−r)! Arranging r items from n (linear)
(n−1)! Circular arrangements of n distinct items
n!n1!n2!... Arrangements with repeated identical items
nCr = n!r!(n−r)! Selecting r items from n (order irrelevant)
nCr = nCn−r Complementary combination property
nC0 = nCn = 1 Boundary cases
nC1 = n Selecting 1 from n

⚡ Common JAMB Patterns

"At least" or "At most": Use complementary counting or case-by-case addition

Restrictions: Treat restricted items as one unit, then arrange internally

Mixed selection: Multiply combinations for different categories

✏️ PRACTICE QUESTIONS (JAMB Standard):

Q1: How many ways can "MATHEMATICS" be arranged?

Q2: 8 people around a table, 2 particular people must sit together?

Q3: Principal and vice-principal from 12 teachers?

Q4: Selecting 4 books from 7 different books?

Answers: (1) 11!/(2!2!2!) (2) 2×6! (3) 12P2=132 (4) 7C4=35

🎯 JAMB EXAM TIPS

1. Master the distinction: Permutation (order matters) vs Combination (order doesn't)

2. Identify keywords quickly: "Arrange" = Permutation, "Select" = Combination

3. 0! = 1: Always remember this special case

4. Simplify factorials: Cancel common terms before calculating

5. Circular arrangements: Always (n−1)!, not n!

6. Repeated objects: Divide by factorials of identical items

7. Mixed problems: Multiply separate selections

⚠️ Common Traps:

• Using permutation when combination is needed (and vice versa)

• Forgetting that AB and BA are different in permutations

• Not dividing by factorials for repeated items

• Using n! instead of (n−1)! for circular arrangements

• Missing "at least" or "at most" conditions in questions

Pre-Exam Checklist:

  • Factorial notation (n!)
  • 0! = 1 (special case)
  • Linear permutations (nPr)
  • Circular permutations ((n−1)!)
  • Permutations with repeated objects
  • Combinations (nCr)
  • Relationship: Perm = Comb × r!
  • Keyword identification
  • Mixed selection problems
  • "At least/at most" strategies
  • Complementary counting
