Introduction to Probability for JAMB

Probability is a branch of mathematics that measures how likely an event is to happen. It ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain). For JAMB, focus on simple calculations using fractions, and understand key concepts like experimental probability and rules for combining probabilities.

Step 1: Understanding Basic Terms

Sample Space: All possible outcomes of an experiment. E.g., for tossing a coin: {Heads, Tails} (2 outcomes).
Event: A specific outcome or set of outcomes you're interested in. E.g., getting Heads.
Favorable Outcomes: The outcomes that match the event.
Probability Formula (Theoretical): P(Event) = Number of favorable outcomes / Total number of possible outcomes (assuming all outcomes are equally likely).
Example: Probability of rolling a 6 on a fair die = 1 (favorable) / 6 (total) = 1/6.

(a) Experimental Probability

Experimental probability is calculated from actual trials or experiments, not theory. It's an estimate that gets closer to the theoretical value with more trials.
Formula: P(Event) = Number of times the event occurs / Total number of trials.
Key Examples:
Tossing a Coin: Suppose you toss a fair coin 20 times and get Heads 12 times. Experimental P(Heads) = 12/20 = 3/5 = 0.6. (Theoretical is 1/2 = 0.5; more tosses would make it closer.)
Throwing a Die: Roll a die 30 times. Suppose 1 appears 4 times, 2 appears 6 times, etc. Experimental P(Even number) = (times 2,4,6 appear) / 30.
In JAMB, you might get a table of results from trials and calculate from it. Remember: It's based on observed data, not assuming fairness unless stated.

Example 1: Basic Experimental Probability (Die Rolls)
The result of rolling a fair die 150 times is summarized as follows (assume from a table: 1: 20 times, 2: 25, 3: 30, 4: 25, 5: 25, 6: 25). What is the experimental probability of obtaining a 5?
Step 1: Event = rolling a 5.
Step 2: Number of times 5 occurred = 25.
Step 3: Total trials = 150.
Step 4: P(5) = 25 / 150 = 1/6.

Step 3: Theoretical Probability

This assumes equally likely outcomes (e.g., fair coin or die). Most JAMB questions use this.
Example: P(Tails on coin toss) = 1/2.
Example: P(Rolling an odd number on die) = 3/6 = 1/2 (1,3,5 are favorable).

(b) Addition Rule for Probabilities – Mutually Exclusive Cases

Use this for "or" scenarios. Mutually exclusive events cannot happen at the same time (e.g., rolling a 1 or a 2 on one die).
Formula: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B).
If not mutually exclusive (e.g., drawing a red card or a king; king of hearts is both), use: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B).
Example: On a die, P(1 or 2) = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3 (mutually exclusive).
Example: P(Red card or King from deck) = 26/52 + 4/52 - 2/52 = 28/52 = 7/13 (subtract the 2 red kings).

(b) Multiplication Rule for Probabilities – Independent Cases

Use this for "and" scenarios. Independent events don't affect each other (e.g., coin toss and die roll).
Formula: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B).
For more events: Multiply all.
Example: P(Heads on coin and 3 on die) = (1/2) × (1/6) = 1/12.
Example: P(Passing two independent exams, each with P=0.7) = 0.7 × 0.7 = 0.49.
If dependent (e.g., drawing cards without replacement), adjust: P(First ace and second ace) = (4/52) × (3/51).

10 JAMB-style examples for (b) with full step-by-step solutions

Example 1: Theoretical Probability (Letter Selection)
The probability of picking a letter T from the word OBSTRUCTION is?
Step 1: Letters: O,B,S,T,R,U,C,T,I,O,N (11 total).
Step 2: T appears 2 times (favorable).
Step 3: P(T) = 2 / 11.
Answer: 2/11
Example 2: Multiplication (Independent Events - Coins)
If three unbiased coins are tossed, find the probability that they are all heads.
Step 1: P(Heads on one coin) = 1/2.
Step 2: Independent, so P(all heads) = (1/2) × (1/2) × (1/2) = 1/8.
Step 3: (Total outcomes 8, favorable HHH = 1).
Answer: 1/8
Example 3: Multiplication (Independent Events - Exams)
The probability of a student passing any examination is 2/3. If the student takes three examinations, what is the probability that he will not pass any of them?
Step 1: P(Pass) = 2/3, P(Fail) = 1/3.
Step 2: Independent, P(Fail all) = (1/3) × (1/3) × (1/3) = 1/27.
Answer: 1/27
Example 4: Basic Probability (Bottles)
A crate of soft drinks contains 10 bottles of Coca-Cola, 8 of Fanta, and 6 of Sprite. If one bottle is selected at random, what is the probability that it is NOT a Coca-Cola bottle?
Step 1: Total = 10+8+6=24.
Step 2: Non-Coca = 8+6=14.
Step 3: P = 14/24 = 7/12.
Answer: 7/12
Example 5: Addition and Multiplication (Combined Events)
The probability of outcome A is 1/6. The probability of outcome B is 1/4. If the probability of A or B or both is 1/12, what is the probability of both A and B?
Step 1: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B).
Step 2: 1/12 = 1/6 + 1/4 - P(A and B).
Step 3: 1/6 = 2/12, 1/4 = 3/12, sum = 5/12.
Step 4: 1/12 = 5/12 - P(A and B) → P(A and B) = 5/12 - 1/12 = 4/12 = 1/3.
Answer: 1/3
Example 6: Dice (Sum Probability)
A die is rolled twice. What is the probability of getting a sum equal to 9?
Step 1: Total outcomes = 6×6=36.
Step 2: Favorable: (3,6), (4,5), (5,4), (6,3) = 4.
Step 3: P = 4/36 = 1/9.
Answer: 1/9
Example 7: Without Replacement (Dependent - Balls)
A bag contains 2 yellow, 3 green, and 2 blue balls. Two balls are drawn at random without replacement. What is the probability that none of the balls drawn is blue?
Step 1: Total = 7, non-blue = 5.
Step 2: Use combinations: C(7,2) = 21 total ways.
Step 3: C(5,2) = 10 favorable.
Step 4: P = 10/21.
Answer: 10/21
Example 8: Multiplication (Independent - Subjects)
Amamath appears in an exam that has 4 subjects. The chance he passes an individual subject’s test is 0.8. What is the probability that he will pass in all the subjects?
Step 1: P(pass one) = 0.8.
Step 2: Independent, P(all) = 0.8^4.
Step 3: 0.8^2 = 0.64, 0.64^2 = 0.4096.
Answer: 0.4096
Example 9: Basic Prime Numbers
What is the probability of selecting a prime number from 1,2,3,...,10?
Step 1: Total = 10.
Step 2: Primes: 2,3,5,7 (4 primes; 1 not prime).
Step 3: P = 4/10 = 2/5.
Answer: 2/5
Example 10: Cards (Mutually Exclusive Addition)
Probability of drawing a spade or club from a deck of 52 cards?
Step 1: P(spade) = 13/52 = 1/4.
Step 2: P(club) = 13/52 = 1/4.
Step 3: Mutually exclusive (no overlap).
Step 4: P = 1/4 + 1/4 = 1/2.
Answer: 1/2

Step 6: Tips for Solving JAMB Problems

  • Identify if events are mutually exclusive (for addition) or independent (for multiplication).
  • Use combinations (C(n,r)) for selecting items without order, e.g., drawing balls.
  • Practice with coins, dice, cards, and bags of items.
  • Always simplify fractions.
  • For "at least one," use 1 - P(none).
