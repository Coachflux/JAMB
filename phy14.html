📚 Chapter 14: Change of State

🎯 Learning Objectives: Differentiate between latent heat and specific latent heats of fusion and vaporization, differentiate between melting/evaporation/boiling, examine effects of pressure and dissolved substances on boiling/melting points, solve numerical problems involving latent heat.

🔥 1. Understanding Change of State

Change of state (phase change) is the physical transformation of matter:

Solid ↔ Liquid ↔ Gas

Key Concept: During change of state, temperature remains constant even though heat is being added or removed. The heat energy breaks or forms molecular bonds, not to increase kinetic energy.

Remember: When ice melts at 0°C, it stays at 0°C until all ice turns to water. The heat supplied is "hidden" (latent) and doesn't raise temperature!

🧊 2. Types of Changes of State

Process Change Description Energy Example
Melting Solid → Liquid Ice turning to water Absorbed (Endothermic) Ice cubes melting
Freezing Liquid → Solid Water turning to ice Released (Exothermic) Water in freezer
Vaporization Liquid → Gas Water turning to steam Absorbed (Endothermic) Boiling water
Condensation Gas → Liquid Steam turning to water Released (Exothermic) Dew on grass
Sublimation Solid → Gas Dry ice (CO₂) to gas Absorbed (Endothermic) Mothballs, dry ice
Deposition Gas → Solid Frost forming on windows Released (Exothermic) Snowflakes forming
JAMB Tip: Sublimation skips the liquid phase (solid → gas directly). Common examples: Dry ice, iodine crystals, naphthalene balls!

⚡ 3. Latent Heat

Latent heat is heat energy absorbed or released during change of state at constant temperature.

The word "latent" means hidden because this heat doesn't cause temperature change—it only changes the state.

Types of Specific Latent Heat

Type Symbol Definition Value for Water Comparison
Specific Latent Heat of Fusion L_f Heat to change 1kg solid to liquid at melting point 334 kJ/kg or 336,000 J/kg Lower value
Specific Latent Heat of Vaporization L_v Heat to change 1kg liquid to gas at boiling point 2,260 kJ/kg or 2,260,000 J/kg Much higher (~7×)
MASTER FORMULAS:

Q = m × L_f (for melting/freezing)

Q = m × L_v (for boiling/condensation)

Where:
Q = Heat energy (Joules, J)
m = Mass (kg)
L_f or L_v = Specific latent heat (J/kg)

🌡️ 4. Melting, Evaporation & Boiling

Melting vs. Freezing

  • Melting Point: Temperature where solid changes to liquid
  • Freezing Point: Temperature where liquid changes to solid
  • For pure substances: Melting point = Freezing point

Evaporation vs. Boiling

Feature Evaporation Boiling
Temperature Occurs at any temperature Occurs at specific temperature (boiling point)
Location Only at the surface Throughout the entire liquid
Bubbles No bubbles formed Bubbles formed throughout
Rate Slow and gradual Rapid and vigorous
Energy source Any heat source Heat until vapor pressure = atmospheric pressure
Remember: Boiling is rapid evaporation when vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure!

🔬 5. Factors Affecting Boiling & Melting Points

A. Effect of Pressure

Pressure Change Boiling Point Melting Point Practical Example
Increased pressure Increases ⬆️ Decreases ⬇️ (most substances) Pressure cooker cooks faster
Decreased pressure Decreases ⬇️ Increases ⬆️ (most substances) High altitude cooking takes longer

Practical Examples:

  • Pressure cooker: Increases pressure → raises boiling point → food cooks faster
  • High altitudes: Lower atmospheric pressure → water boils at lower temperature → food takes longer to cook

B. Effect of Dissolved Substances (Impurities)

  • Adding salt or sugar to water raises the boiling point
  • Adding impurities lowers the melting point (freezing point depression)
  • This is why salt is spread on icy roads—it lowers the melting point of ice!

🏠 6. Applications in Appliances

Appliance Principle Key Process
Refrigerator Evaporation of refrigerant absorbs heat from inside Evaporation (endothermic)
Pressure Cooker Increased pressure raises boiling point Higher pressure = higher boiling point
Freezer Compression and expansion of refrigerant Compression releases heat, expansion absorbs
Electric Kettle Heating element provides latent heat for boiling Vaporization at 100°C
Air Conditioner Refrigerant evaporates indoors, condenses outdoors Evaporation cools, condensation releases heat

📝 7. Solved Examples (JAMB Style)

Example 1: Calculating Latent Heat of Fusion

Question: Calculate heat required to melt 0.5 kg ice at 0°C. (L_f = 336,000 J/kg)

Solution:
Q = m × L_f
Q = 0.5 × 336,000
Q = 168,000 J or 168 kJ
Answer: 168,000 J or 168 kJ

Example 2: Calculating Mass from Latent Heat

Question: How much steam at 100°C is produced when 11,300 J of heat is supplied to boiling water? (L_v = 2,260,000 J/kg)

Solution:
Q = m × L_v
m = Q/L_v = 11,300/2,260,000
m = 0.005 kg or 5 g
Answer: 0.005 kg or 5 g

Example 3: Combined Heating (Ice to Steam)

Question: Calculate total heat required to change 2 kg ice at 0°C to steam at 100°C.
[L_f = 336,000 J/kg, L_v = 2,260,000 J/kg, c_water = 4,200 J/kg·K]

Solution:
Stage 1: Melting ice at 0°C
Q₁ = 2 × 336,000 = 672,000 J

Stage 2: Heating water 0°C → 100°C
Q₂ = mcΔT = 2 × 4,200 × 100 = 840,000 J

Stage 3: Boiling water at 100°C
Q₃ = 2 × 2,260,000 = 4,520,000 J

Total heat: Q = 672,000 + 840,000 + 4,520,000
Q = 6,032,000 J
Answer: 6,032,000 J or 6.032 MJ

Example 4: Cooling Condensation

Question: Calculate heat released when 10 g of steam at 100°C condenses to water at 100°C. (L_v = 2,260,000 J/kg)

Solution:
m = 10 g = 0.01 kg
Q = m × L_v (condensation releases same amount)
Q = 0.01 × 2,260,000
Q = 22,600 J
Answer: 22,600 J or 22.6 kJ

🎯 8. Key Points to Remember for JAMB

  1. Temperature is constant during change of state
  2. L_v > L_f: Specific latent heat of vaporization always greater than fusion for same substance
  3. Water is special: Unusually high specific latent heats, excellent for cooling systems
  4. Sublimation: Skips the liquid phase (solid → gas)
  5. Pressure cookers: Increase pressure to raise boiling point
  6. Impurities: Raise boiling point but lower melting point
  7. Heat required: Vaporization needs ~7× more heat than melting for water
