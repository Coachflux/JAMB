Teaching the Topic: Simple Machines

This topic is all about tools that make our work easier. We'll go step by step, building from what a simple machine is to how we measure if it's good or not. I'll use simple words, everyday examples, and pictures to make it clear. Remember, machines don't create extra energy—they just help us use our force better.

Step 1: What is a Simple Machine? (Definition)

A simple machine is a basic device that helps us do work by changing the size, direction, or speed of a force. It doesn't create energy—it just makes tasks easier by multiplying the force we apply (called the effort) to move a heavier object (called the load).

Key idea: Work done = Force × Distance. A machine doesn't reduce the work; it spreads it out or redirects it.

Examples in everyday life: Using a ramp to push a heavy box uphill instead of lifting it straight up, or a crowbar to pry open a lid.

Important: All machines have some energy loss due to friction (rubbing parts), so they're never 100% perfect.

In New School Physics, simple machines are described as tools that transmit or modify force to perform work more conveniently.

Step 2: Types of Simple Machines

There are six main types of simple machines. Each one works on the principle of trading distance for force (or vice versa). I'll explain each with a simple example:

  • Lever: A rigid bar that pivots around a fixed point (fulcrum). It multiplies force or changes direction.
  • Classes:
  • First class (e.g., seesaw): Fulcrum in the middle (effort and load on opposite sides).
  • Second class (e.g., wheelbarrow): Load in the middle (fulcrum at one end, effort at the other).
  • Third class (e.g., tweezers): Effort in the middle (fulcrum at one end, load at the other).
  • Example: Using a plank and rock to lift a heavy stone.
  • Wheel and Axle: A wheel attached to a smaller axle (rod). Turning the wheel applies force to the axle.
  • Example: Steering wheel in a car or a doorknob—easy to turn the big wheel, which moves the small axle.
  • Pulley: A wheel with a rope or chain over it. It changes the direction of force.
  • Types: Fixed (changes direction only), movable (multiplies force), or block and tackle (combination for more advantage).
  • Example: Flagpole pulley to raise a flag effortlessly.
  • Inclined Plane: A slanted surface that reduces the force needed to lift something by increasing the distance.
  • Example: Ramp for loading trucks—longer path but less steep force.
  • Wedge: Two inclined planes back-to-back. It splits or cuts by concentrating force.
  • Example: Knife or axe—pushing it forward splits wood.
  • Screw: An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder. It converts rotational force to linear force.
  • Example: Screwdriver or bottle cap—twisting motion pushes it in with less effort.

To identify them: Look for how they reduce effort—levers pivot, pulleys pull, planes slope, etc.

Step 3: Mechanical Advantage (MA)

This measures how much a machine multiplies your force. It's the ratio of the load (output force) to the effort (input force).

Formula: MA = Load / Effort (no units, just a number).

What it means: If MA = 4, the machine makes your effort feel 4 times stronger (e.g., 100N effort lifts 400N load).

For ideal machines (no friction), MA is high. In reality, friction reduces it.

Examples:

  • Lever: MA = Effort arm length / Load arm length.
  • Inclined plane: MA = Length of plane / Height.

In problems, calculate MA directly from given load and effort.

Step 4: Velocity Ratio (VR)

This measures how much farther the effort moves compared to the load. It's about distances, not forces.

Formula: VR = Distance moved by effort / Distance moved by load (no units).

What it means: If VR = 5, you move your hand 5 times farther than the load moves.

It's fixed by the machine's design (geometry) and doesn't change with friction.

Examples:

  • Pulley system: VR = Number of ropes supporting the load.
  • Inclined plane: VR = Length of plane / Height.
  • Screw: VR = Circumference of effort path / Pitch (distance per turn).

VR is always greater than or equal to 1 for machines that multiply force.

Step 5: Efficiency of Machines

Efficiency tells us how well a machine converts input work to useful output work. There's always some loss (e.g., heat from friction).

Formula: Efficiency = (Useful work output / Work input) × 100%

Or, since work output = Load × Distance load, work input = Effort × Distance effort,

It simplifies to: Efficiency = (MA / VR) × 100%

What it means: If efficiency = 80%, 80% of your energy does useful work; 20% is wasted.

Why less than 100%? Friction, air resistance, or machine weight cause energy loss.

In ideal machines: Efficiency = 100%, so MA = VR.

In real machines: MA < VR, so efficiency < 100%.

To solve problems: Use the formulas step by step. Identify what's given (load, effort, distances, angles, etc.), plug in, and calculate.

10 JAMB-Style Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

1. (JAMB 2000): Which of the following is NOT a simple machine?
A. Lever B. Pulley C. Wedge D. Generator
Step 1: Recall types: lever, pulley, wedge, screw, inclined plane, wheel and axle.
Step 2: Generator is complex (uses electromagnetic induction).
Step 3: Matches option D.
Answer: D. Generator
2. (JAMB 1981): An inclined plane of angle θ, acting as a simple machine, has a velocity ratio of:
A. sin θ B. 1/sin θ C. cos θ D. 1/cos θ E. sin θ cos θ
Step 1: VR = length of plane / height.
Step 2: Length / height = hypotenuse / opposite = 1 / sin θ.
Step 3: Matches option B.
Answer: B. 1/sin θ
3. (JAMB 2001): The efficiency of a machine is always less than 100% because the:
A. Velocity ratio is always greater than the mechanical advantage B. Work output is always greater than the work input C. Effort applied is always greater than the load lifted D. Load lifted is always greater than the effort applied
Step 1: Efficiency = (MA / VR) × 100%.
Step 2: Friction makes MA < VR.
Step 3: VR > MA.
Step 4: Matches option A.
Answer: A. Velocity ratio is always greater than the mechanical advantage
4. (Similar to JAMB 2015): A lever of length 2 m lifts a load of 400 N with an effort of 100 N. Calculate its mechanical advantage.
A. 0.25 B. 4 C. 2 D. 0.5
Step 1: MA = Load / Effort.
Step 2: MA = 400 / 100 = 4.
Step 3: Matches option B.
Answer: B. 4
5. (Similar to JAMB 2017): A machine does 600 J of useful work when 800 J of work is supplied. What is its efficiency?
A. 75% B. 133% C. 60% D. 80%
Step 1: Efficiency = (useful work / input work) × 100%.
Step 2: (600 / 800) × 100% = 75%.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 75%
6. (Typical JAMB): If the effort moves 5 m while the load moves 1 m in a machine, find the velocity ratio.
A. 5 B. 0.2 C. 1 D. 6
Step 1: VR = Distance by effort / Distance by load.
Step 2: VR = 5 / 1 = 5.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 5
7. (JAMB-like): A machine has a mechanical advantage of 3 and a velocity ratio of 6. Find its efficiency.
A. 50% B. 200% C. 18% D. 100%
Step 1: Efficiency = (MA / VR) × 100%.
Step 2: (3 / 6) × 100% = 50%.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 50%
8. (Similar to JAMB 2002): A screw jack, 25% efficient, with pitch 0.4 cm is used to raise a load. The handle turns through a circle of radius 40 cm. Calculate: (i) VR, (ii) MA, (iii) Effort for 1000 N load.
A. VR ≈ 628, MA ≈ 157, Effort ≈ 6.37 N B. VR = 100, MA = 25, Effort = 40 N C. VR = 200, MA = 50, Effort = 20 N D. VR = 314, MA = 78.5, Effort = 12.74 N
Step 1: For screw, VR = 2π r / pitch = 2π × 40 / 0.4 = 251.3 / 0.4 ≈ 628.
Step 2: Efficiency = 25% = (MA / VR) × 100%, MA = 0.25 × 628 ≈ 157.
Step 3: Effort = Load / MA = 1000 / 157 ≈ 6.37 N.
Step 4: Matches option A.
Answer: A. VR ≈ 628, MA ≈ 157, Effort ≈ 6.37 N
9. (JAMB-like): A pulley system has VR = 4 and requires 800 N effort to lift 1600 N load. Calculate efficiency.
A. 50% B. 200% C. 25% D. 100%
Step 1: MA = Load / Effort = 1600 / 800 = 2.
Step 2: Efficiency = (2 / 4) × 100% = 50%.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 50%
10. (Typical JAMB): What type of simple machine is a seesaw?
A. Pulley B. Inclined plane C. Lever D. Wedge
Step 1: Seesaw pivots on fulcrum, effort/load opposite.
Step 2: First-class lever.
Step 3: Matches option C.
Answer: C. Lever
