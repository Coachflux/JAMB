Chemical Combination

Chemical combination refers to the process by which elements or compounds react to form new substances, governed by fundamental laws and principles. This note covers the key laws of chemical combination, chemical symbols, formulae, and equations, relative atomic mass (RAM), the mole concept, and stoichiometry of reactions, as outlined in JAMB chemistry syllabus. It includes deductions of laws from expressions/statements/data, simple calculations, and stoichiometry examples for clarity.

1. Laws of Chemical Combination

These laws explain how elements combine in predictable ways during chemical reactions.

Law of Conservation of Matter (Mass)

This law states that matter can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction; the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products. It was proposed by Antoine Lavoisier.

Deduction from data: If 4 g of hydrogen reacts with 32 g of oxygen to form 36 g of water, the mass is conserved (4 g + 32 g = 36 g). This implies atoms are rearranged, not lost or gained.

Law of Definite Proportions

A given chemical compound always contains the same elements in the same fixed proportion by mass, regardless of the source. It was proposed by Joseph Proust.

Deduction from statements/data: In water (H‚ÇÇO), hydrogen and oxygen always combine in a 1:8 mass ratio (2 g H with 16 g O, or multiples). If analysis of 18 g water yields 2 g H and 16 g O, the ratio is fixed, confirming the law.

Law of Multiple Proportions

When two elements combine to form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other element are in the ratio of small whole numbers. It was proposed by John Dalton.

Deduction from data: Carbon and oxygen form CO (12 g C with 16 g O) and CO‚ÇÇ (12 g C with 32 g O). For fixed 12 g C, oxygen masses are 16 g and 32 g, ratio 1:2 (whole numbers).

Law of Reciprocal Proportions

If two elements (A and B) combine separately with a third element (C), the ratio of masses in which A and B combine with a fixed mass of C is the same as (or a simple multiple of) the ratio in which A and B combine with each other. It was proposed by Richter.

Deduction from data: Hydrogen combines with oxygen (1 g H with 8 g O) and chlorine (1 g H with 35.5 g Cl) to form H‚ÇÇO and HCl. For fixed 1 g H, O:Cl = 8:35.5 ‚âà 1:4.4. Oxygen and chlorine combine in Cl‚ÇÇO‚Çá (35.5√ó2 g Cl with 8√ó7/2 g O, but simplified ratio matches multiples).

These laws form the basis of modern atomic theory, showing that atoms combine in fixed ratios.

2. Chemical Symbols, Formulae, Equations, and Their Uses

Chemical Symbols: These are shorthand notations for elements, usually one or two letters (e.g., H for hydrogen, Na for sodium). They represent one atom of the element and are used in writing formulae and equations.

Chemical Formulae: These represent compounds or molecules using symbols and subscripts (e.g., H‚ÇÇO for water: 2 H atoms, 1 O atom; CO‚ÇÇ for carbon dioxide). Empirical formulae show simplest ratios (e.g., CH for benzene), while molecular formulae show actual numbers (e.g., C‚ÇÜH‚ÇÜ).

  • They help in identifying reaction types (e.g., combination: A + B ‚Üí AB) and balancing for accuracy.

Stoichiometry is the quantitative study of reactants and products in balanced equations, using mole ratios.
