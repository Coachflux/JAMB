🔬 PART A: CONDUCTION THROUGH LIQUIDS

1️⃣ Electrolytes vs Non-Electrolytes 🧪

Electrolytes Non-Electrolytes
Conduct electricity when molten or dissolved Do not conduct electricity in any state
Undergo chemical decomposition during conduction No chemical change occurs
Examples: Acids (H₂SO₄, HCl), Bases (NaOH), Salts (CuSO₄, NaCl) Examples: Sugar, alcohol, distilled water, benzene
Contain mobile ions (charged particles) Contain only molecules—no free ions
Key Point: Solid ionic compounds (like NaCl crystals) don't conduct electricity because their ions are fixed in position. They only conduct when molten or dissolved, freeing up the ions to move.

2️⃣ The Concept of Electrolysis ⚡

Electrolysis is the process of decomposing a chemical compound (electrolyte) by passing an electric current through it.

🔧 The Electrolytic Cell Setup

  • Electrodes: Two conductors (metal or graphite) dipped in electrolyte
    • Anode (+): Positive electrode → Oxidation occurs here
    • Cathode (–): Negative electrode → Reduction occurs here
  • Electrolyte: The liquid containing ions
  • Battery/D.C. source: Provides the driving force

What Happens During Electrolysis:

  1. Positive ions (cations) move toward the cathode (negative)
  2. Negative ions (anions) move toward the anode (positive)
  3. At the electrodes, ions gain or lose electrons (discharge)
💡 Memory Aid: "CAT-ions go to the CAT-hode" (positive ions go to negative electrode)

3️⃣ Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis 📐

First Law

The mass of a substance liberated at an electrode is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity passed through the electrolyte.

m = ZIt = ZQ

Where:
• m = mass of substance deposited (grams)
• Z = electrochemical equivalent (E.C.E) (g/C)
• I = current (Amperes)
• t = time (seconds)
• Q = quantity of electricity = It (Coulombs)

Key Formula for Z:
Z = M/(n × 96,500)

Where:
• M = molar mass (g/mol)
• n = valency/number of electrons transferred
• 96,500 C = 1 Faraday (F) = 1 mole of electrons

Second Law

When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolytes, the masses of substances liberated are proportional to their chemical equivalent masses.

m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂

Where E = equivalent mass = Molar mass/Valency

Practical Meaning: If you pass the same current through CuSO₄ and AgNO₃ for the same time, the ratio of copper to silver deposited equals the ratio of their equivalent masses.

🔢 Calculation 1: Mass Deposited (First Law)

Question: Calculate the mass of copper deposited when 2A flows through CuSO₄ for 30 minutes. (Cu = 64, n = 2)

Step 1: Calculate Q
Q = It = 2 × (30 × 60) = 3,600 C

Step 2: Calculate Z
Z = 64/(2 × 96,500) = 64/193,000 g/C

Step 3: Calculate m
m = ZQ = (64 × 3,600)/193,000
= 230,400/193,000 = 1.19 g
Answer: 1.19 g

🔢 Calculation 2: Using Second Law (Ratio)

Question: If 0.54g of silver is deposited in the same time as 0.16g of copper, find the equivalent mass of copper. (Ag = 108, E = 108)

m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂
0.16/0.54 = Eᴄᵤ/108

Eᴄᵤ = (0.16 × 108)/0.54
= 17.28/0.54 = 32
Answer: 32

4️⃣ Applications of Electrolysis 🏭

Application How It Works
Electroplating Coating cheap metal with expensive one (e.g., iron with silver). Object to be plated = cathode; plating metal = anode.
Purification of Metals Impure metal = anode, pure metal = cathode. Electrolyte contains ions of pure metal.
Calibration of Ammeters Using copper voltameter (CuSO₄ with copper electrodes) to measure current by weighing copper deposited.
Extraction of Metals Reactive metals (Na, Al, Ca) extracted by electrolysis of molten ores.
Production of Compounds Manufacturing chlorine, hydrogen, and NaOH from brine (NaCl solution).

🥄 Electroplating Example: Silver-plating a spoon

  • Spoon = Cathode (negative)
  • Silver bar = Anode (positive)
  • Electrolyte = Silver nitrate (AgNO₃) solution
  • Result: Silver ions deposit on spoon; silver from anode dissolves to replace them
⚡ PART B: CONDUCTION THROUGH GASES

1️⃣ Discharge Through Gases 💨

Normally, gases are insulators because they have no free electrons or ions. However, under certain conditions, they can conduct:

How Gases Become Conductors:

  • High Voltage: Strong electric field ionizes gas atoms
  • Low Pressure: At reduced pressure, molecules are farther apart, allowing ions/accelerated electrons to travel without colliding
  • Ionization: Atoms lose electrons → create positive ions + free electrons

🔬 The Discharge Tube Experiment

  • Glass tube with electrodes at each end
  • Air pumped out to create partial vacuum
  • High voltage applied across electrodes

Observations at Different Pressures:

Pressure Observation
Atmospheric No discharge (insulator)
10 mmHg Thin reddish streamers
1 mmHg Crookes dark space - glow fills tube
0.01 mmHg Cathode rays (electron beams) stream from cathode; cause glass to fluoresce green

2️⃣ Applications of Conduction Through Gases 🌟

Device Application
Fluorescent Tubes Mercury vapor conducts; UV produced, converted to visible light by phosphor coating
Neon Signs Neon gas glows red-orange when ionized; different gases give different colors (argon = blue, helium = pink)
X-ray Tubes High-speed electrons (cathode rays) hit metal target, producing X-rays
Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO) Electron beams used to visualize electrical signals
Television/Monitor Tubes (CRTs) Electron beams scan screen to create images
Lightning Natural discharge through ionized air

    Answer: 4.02 g

  • Q2. How many Faradays are required to deposit 1 mole of Al from Al³⁺?
    Answer: 3 Faradays

  • Q3. Why does NaCl conduct when molten but not when solid?
    Answer: Ions are fixed in solid state but mobile when molten
