📚 Chapter 34: Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor in a Magnetic Field

🎯 Learning Objectives: Master force between parallel conductors, force on moving charges, DC motors, electromagnets, carbon microphones, moving-coil and moving-iron instruments, galvanometer conversions, and sensitivity calculations.

🔑 Section 1: Fundamental Concepts 🎯

1.1 Fleming's Left-Hand Rule (Motor Rule)

This rule determines the direction of force on a current-carrying conductor:

Finger Represents Direction
First finger Magnetic Field (B) Point in direction of magnetic field (N → S)
Second finger Current (I) Point in direction of conventional current (+ → -)
Thumb Force/Motion (F) Points in direction of force on conductor
Memory tip: "First Field, Second Current, Thumb Thrusts"

1.2 Force on a Current-Carrying Conductor

When a conductor of length L carrying current I is placed in a magnetic field B at angle θ:

F = BIL sin θ

Where:
• F = Force (Newtons, N)
• B = Magnetic flux density (Tesla, T) or (Wb/m²)
• I = Current (Amperes, A)
• L = Length of conductor in field (meters, m)
• θ = Angle between current direction and magnetic field

Special Cases:
• Maximum force: When θ = 90° (conductor perpendicular to field): F = BIL
• Zero force: When θ = 0° (conductor parallel to field): F = 0

🔢 Calculation 1: Force on a Conductor

Question: A 25 cm wire carrying a current of 4 A is placed perpendicular to a magnetic field of flux density 0.5 T. Calculate the force on the wire.

Given:
• L = 25 cm = 0.25 m
• I = 4 A
• B = 0.5 T
• θ = 90°, so sin 90° = 1

F = BIL sin θ = 0.5 × 4 × 0.25 × 1 = 0.5 N
Answer: 0.5 N

🔑 Section 2: Force Between Parallel Current-Carrying Conductors 🔗

2.1 The Principle

Two parallel wires carrying currents exert forces on each other through their magnetic fields.

2.2 Formulas

Force per unit length between two parallel conductors:

F/L = (μ₀ I₁ I₂)/(2πd)

Where:
• F/L = Force per unit length (N/m)
• μ₀ = Permeability of free space = 4π × 10⁻⁷ H/m or 4π × 10⁻⁷ T·m/A
• I₁, I₂ = Currents in the two conductors (A)
• d = Distance between conductors (m)

2.3 Direction of Forces

  • Currents in SAME direction → ATTRACTIVE force
  • Currents in OPPOSITE directions → REPULSIVE force
Definition of the Ampere: One ampere is the current which, flowing in two infinitely long parallel conductors 1 meter apart in vacuum, produces a force of 2 × 10⁻⁷ N/m on each conductor.

🔢 Calculation 2: Force Between Parallel Conductors

Question: Two long parallel wires 0.2 m apart carry currents of 5 A and 3 A in the same direction. Calculate the force per meter between them. Is it attractive or repulsive?

F/L = (μ₀ I₁ I₂)/(2πd)
= (4π × 10⁻⁷ × 5 × 3)/(2π × 0.2)
= (2 × 10⁻⁷ × 15)/0.2
= (30 × 10⁻⁷)/0.2
= 150 × 10⁻⁷ = 1.5 × 10⁻⁵ N/m

Since currents are in the same direction, the force is attractive.
Answer: 1.5 × 10⁻⁵ N/m, Attractive

🔑 Section 3: Force on a Moving Charge in Magnetic Field ⚡

3.1 The Lorentz Force

A charged particle q moving with velocity v at angle θ to magnetic field B experiences:

F = qvB sin θ

Where:
• F = Force (N)
• q = Charge (Coulombs, C)
• v = Velocity (m/s)
• B = Magnetic flux density (T)
• θ = Angle between velocity and magnetic field

3.2 Direction

Use Fleming's Left-Hand Rule adapted for moving charges:

  • First finger: Magnetic field (B)
  • Second finger: Direction of motion of positive charge (v)
  • Thumb: Direction of force (F)
  • For negative charges (electrons): Point second finger opposite to direction of motion.

3.3 Circular Motion in Magnetic Field

When a charged particle enters a magnetic field perpendicular to its velocity:

  • Force provides centripetal acceleration
  • Particle moves in a circular path
Radius of circular path:
r = mv/(qB)

Where:
• m = mass of particle (kg)
• r = radius of circular path (m)

🔢 Calculation 3: Force on Moving Charge

Question: An electron (charge = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C) moves at 2 × 10⁶ m/s perpendicular to a magnetic field of 0.1 T. Calculate the force on the electron.

F = qvB sin θ
= 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 2 × 10⁶ × 0.1 × 1
= 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁴ N
Answer: 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁴ N

🔢 Calculation 4: Radius of Circular Path

Question: A proton (mass = 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg, charge = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C) enters a magnetic field of 0.2 T perpendicular to its velocity of 10⁵ m/s. Calculate the radius of its path.

r = mv/(qB)
= (1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ × 10⁵)/(1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ × 0.2)
= (1.67 × 10⁻²²)/(3.2 × 10⁻²⁰)
= 5.22 × 10⁻³ m = 5.22 mm
Answer: 5.22 mm

🔑 Section 4: The DC Motor 🔄

4.1 Principle

A current-carrying coil in a magnetic field experiences a torque (turning force), causing rotation.

4.2 Key Components

  1. Armature/Coil - rotates in magnetic field
  2. Field magnets - provide magnetic field
  3. Commutator - reverses current direction every half-rotation
  4. Brushes - maintain contact with commutator
  5. Split rings - part of commutator system

4.3 Torque Equation

τ = BANI sin θ

Where:
• τ = Torque (Nm)
• B = Magnetic flux density (T)
• A = Area of coil (m²) = L × W
• N = Number of turns in coil
• I = Current (A)
• θ = Angle between plane of coil and magnetic field

Maximum torque occurs when coil is parallel to field (θ = 90°).

🔑 Section 5: Electromagnets 🔧

5.1 Principle

A soft iron core inside a current-carrying solenoid becomes strongly magnetized.

5.2 Factors Affecting Strength

  1. Number of turns in coil - more turns = stronger magnet
  2. Current magnitude - higher current = stronger field
  3. Presence of soft iron core - increases field strength dramatically
  4. Shape of pole pieces - can concentrate magnetic field

5.3 Applications

  • Electric bells, Relays, Magnetic cranes
  • MRI machines, Particle accelerators, Loudspeakers

5.4 Comparison: Soft Iron vs Steel

Property Soft Iron Steel
Magnetization Easy Hard
Demagnetization Easy Hard
Retentivity Low High
Use Temporary magnets (electromagnets) Permanent magnets

🔑 Section 6: Carbon Microphone 🎤

6.1 Principle

Sound waves cause a diaphragm to vibrate, pressing carbon granules together. This changes the resistance of the carbon, varying the current in the circuit.

6.2 Key Points

  • Works on principle of variable resistance
  • Sound waves → Diaphragm vibration → Pressure on carbon granules → Resistance change → Current variation
  • Requires external power source (unlike dynamic microphone)

🔑 Section 7: Moving-Coil and Moving-Iron Instruments 🧪

7.1 Moving-Coil (Permanent Magnet) Instruments

  • Principle: Current in a coil placed in a permanent magnetic field experiences torque
  • Characteristics: Measures DC only (rectifier needed for AC), Linear scale (uniform divisions), Sensitive and accurate, More expensive
  • Torque equation: τ = BANI

7.2 Moving-Iron Instruments

  • Principle: Soft iron pieces are magnetized by current-carrying coil and repel each other
  • Characteristics: Measures both AC and DC, Non-linear scale (crowded at beginning), Less sensitive than moving-coil, Cheaper and more robust

7.3 Comparison Table

Feature Moving-Coil Moving-Iron
Suitable for DC only AC and DC
Scale Linear/Uniform Non-linear
Sensitivity High Lower
Accuracy High Moderate
Cost Higher Lower
Damping Eddy current Air friction

🔑 Section 8: Converting Galvanometers ⚡

8.1 Galvanometer Basics

  • Detects small currents (microamperes)
  • Full-scale deflection current: I_g (typically 10μA - 1mA)
  • Internal resistance: R_g (typically 50-100Ω)

8.2 Converting to Ammeter

Purpose: Measure larger currents by providing parallel path (shunt) for excess current.

Shunt resistance formula:
R_s = (I_g R_g)/(I - I_g)

Where:
• R_s = Shunt resistance (Ω)
• I_g = Galvanometer full-scale current (A)
• R_g = Galvanometer resistance (Ω)
• I = Desired full-scale range (A)

Connection: Shunt in parallel with galvanometer.

8.3 Converting to Voltmeter

Purpose: Measure potential difference by adding series resistance (multiplier) to limit current.

Multiplier resistance formula:
R_m = V/I_g - R_g

Where:
• R_m = Multiplier resistance (Ω)
• V = Desired full-scale voltage (V)
• I_g = Galvanometer full-scale current (A)
• R_g = Galvanometer resistance (Ω)

Connection: Multiplier in series with galvanometer.

🔢 Calculation 5: Converting Galvanometer to Ammeter

Question: A galvanometer with resistance 50 Ω and full-scale deflection of 1 mA is to be converted to an ammeter reading 5 A. Calculate the shunt resistance required.

Given:
• R_g = 50 Ω
• I_g = 1 mA = 0.001 A
• I = 5 A

R_s = (I_g R_g)/(I - I_g)
= (0.001 × 50)/(5 - 0.001)
= 0.05/4.999
≈ 0.01 Ω (or 10 mΩ)
Answer: 0.01 Ω (or 10 mΩ)

🔢 Calculation 6: Converting Galvanometer to Voltmeter

Question: The same galvanometer (50 Ω, 1 mA f.s.d.) is to be converted to a voltmeter reading 10 V. Calculate the multiplier resistance.

R_m = V/I_g - R_g
= 10/0.001 - 50
= 10000 - 50
= 9950 Ω
Answer: 9950 Ω

🔑 Section 9: Galvanometer Sensitivity 📊

9.1 Current Sensitivity

Current Sensitivity = θ/I = BAN/K

Where:
• θ = Deflection angle
• I = Current
• K = Torsional constant of suspension wire (Nm/rad)

Unit: rad/A or div/A

9.2 Voltage Sensitivity

Voltage Sensitivity = θ/V = BAN/(KR_g)

Unit: rad/V or div/V

9.3 Factors Affecting Sensitivity

  1. Magnetic field strength (B) - stronger field = more sensitive
  2. Number of turns (N) - more turns = more sensitive
  3. Area of coil (A) - larger area = more sensitive
  4. Torsional constant (K) - weaker suspension = more sensitive
  5. Resistance (R_g) - affects voltage sensitivity only

💡 Exam Tips

  1. Always check units - convert cm to m, g to kg, etc.

  2. Watch the angle θ - maximum force at 90°, zero at 0°

  3. Remember μ₀ = 4π × 10⁻⁷ H/m - don't forget the 4π

  4. For galvanometer conversions:
    • Ammeter = Parallel shunt (low resistance)
    • Voltmeter = Series multiplier (high resistance)

  5. Current direction matters - conventional current (+ to -), not electron flow
