📚 Chapter 12: Temperature and Its Measurement

🎯 Learning Objectives: Identify thermometric properties, calibrate thermometers, differentiate between temperature scales (Celsius and Kelvin), compare thermometer types, and convert between scales.

1️⃣ Concept of Temperature 🌡️

Temperature is the degree of hotness or coldness of a body. It measures the average kinetic energy of molecules in a substance.

  • Heat flows from higher to lower temperature
  • Temperature determines direction of heat flow
  • SI unit: Kelvin (K)
  • Common unit: Degree Celsius (°C)

2️⃣ Thermometric Properties 🔬

A thermometric property is any physical property that changes measurably with temperature.

Property Examples Used In Range
Expansion of liquids Mercury, alcohol Laboratory thermometers -39°C to 357°C
Expansion of solids Bimetallic strips Thermostats, switches Limited range
Expansion of gases Constant volume gas Standard thermometer -270°C to 1500°C
Electrical resistance Platinum wire Precision measurements -200°C to 1000°C
Thermoelectric e.m.f Two dissimilar metals Thermocouples, industrial -250°C to 1500°C
Radiation Infrared emissions Pyrometers 1000°C and above

✅ Good Thermometric Properties Must:

  • Change uniformly with temperature
  • Be easily measurable
  • Have a wide range
  • Be reproducible

3️⃣ Temperature Scales 🌡️

Celsius Scale (°C)

  • 0°C = Ice point (melting ice)
  • 100°C = Steam point (boiling water)
  • Divided into 100 equal parts

Kelvin Scale (K) - Absolute Scale

  • 0 K = Absolute zero (-273.15°C)
  • 273.15 K = Ice point
  • 373.15 K = Steam point
  • No negative values!

Conversion Formulas

Master Formula:
T_C/100 = (T_K - 273)/100 = (T_F - 32)/180

Quick Conversions:
• °C to K: T_K = T_C + 273
• K to °C: T_C = T_K - 273
• °C to °F: T_F = (9/5)T_C + 32

4️⃣ Types of Thermometers 🌡️

Type Thermometric Property Range Advantages Disadvantages Primary Uses
Liquid-in-glass (mercury) Volume expansion -39°C to 357°C Simple, cheap, direct reading Fragile, limited range, slow response Laboratory, weather stations
Liquid-in-glass (alcohol) Volume expansion -115°C to 78°C Lower freezing point than mercury Less accurate, wets glass Very low temperatures
Gas thermometer (constant volume) Pressure of gas -270°C to 1500°C Most accurate, wide range, used as standard Bulky, slow, complex operation Calibrating other thermometers
Thermocouple Electromotive force (e.m.f) -250°C to 1500°C Wide range, fast response, remote reading Requires reference junction, less accurate Industrial, furnaces, engines
Resistance thermometer Electrical resistance -200°C to 1000°C Very precise, stable, remote reading Expensive, slow response, needs bridge circuit Scientific, industrial precision
Thermistor Resistance (semiconductor) -100°C to 150°C Very sensitive, fast response, small size Non-linear, limited range Electronic circuits, medical
Pyrometer (radiation) Thermal radiation 1000°C+ No contact needed, very high temperatures Expensive, affected by emissivity Metallurgy, kilns, stars

5️⃣ Calibration of Thermometers ⚙️

Calibration is marking a scale using fixed points.

Steps:

  1. Place in melting ice (0°C) → mark level
  2. Place in steam (100°C) → mark level
  3. Divide distance into 100 equal parts
Linear Scale Formula:
(T - T_lower)/(T_upper - T_lower) = (X - X_lower)/(X_upper - X_lower)

Where X = thermometric property (length, resistance, pressure, etc.)

🔢 7 Essential Calculations for JAMB

Calculation 1: Celsius to Kelvin

Question: Convert 27°C to Kelvin.

Solution:
T_K = T_C + 273
T_K = 27 + 273 = 300 K
Answer: 300 K

Calculation 2: Kelvin to Celsius

Question: Furnace temperature is 1273 K. What is this in Celsius?

Solution:
T_C = T_K - 273
T_C = 1273 - 273 = 1000°C
Answer: 1000°C

Calculation 3: Fahrenheit Conversion

Question: Convert 37°C (body temperature) to Fahrenheit.

Solution:
T_F = (9/5)T_C + 32
T_F = (9/5)(37) + 32
T_F = 66.6 + 32 = 98.6°F
Answer: 98.6°F

Calculation 4: Thermometer Calibration (Length)

Question: Mercury thread is 4.0 cm at 0°C and 24.0 cm at 100°C. Find temperature when length is 14.0 cm.

Solution:
(T - 0)/(100 - 0) = (14.0 - 4.0)/(24.0 - 4.0)
T/100 = 10/20 = 0.5
T = 0.5 × 100 = 50°C
Answer: 50°C

Calculation 5: Resistance Thermometer

Question: Platinum thermometer has resistance 5.0 Ω at ice point and 7.0 Ω at steam point. Find temperature at 6.4 Ω.

Solution:
T/100 = (R - R_0)/(R_100 - R_0)
T/100 = (6.4 - 5.0)/(7.0 - 5.0) = 1.4/2.0 = 0.7
T = 0.7 × 100 = 70°C
Answer: 70°C

Calculation 6: Gas Thermometer

Question: Constant volume gas thermometer records 80 cmHg at 0°C and 110 cmHg at 100°C. Find temperature at 95 cmHg.

Solution:
T/100 = (P - P_0)/(P_100 - P_0)
T/100 = (95 - 80)/(110 - 80) = 15/30 = 0.5
T = 0.5 × 100 = 50°C
Or in Kelvin: 50 + 273 = 323 K
Answer: 50°C (323 K)

Calculation 7: Thermocouple E.m.f

Question: Thermocouple produces 0.0 mV at 0°C and 5.0 mV at 100°C. Find temperature at 3.5 mV.

Solution:
T/100 = (E - E_0)/(E_100 - E_0)
T/100 = (3.5 - 0)/(5.0 - 0) = 3.5/5.0 = 0.7
T = 0.7 × 100 = 70°C
Answer: 70°C

📝 Quick Revision Tips for JAMB

  • Remember 273 — Always add/subtract for K ↔ °C
  • Linear relationship — All calibrations assume linearity
  • Absolute zero — 0 K = -273°C (theoretical limit)
  • Triple point — 273.16 K (modern precise standard)
  • Gas thermometers — Most accurate, used as standard

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Using 273.15 vs 273 — For JAMB, use 273 unless specified
  • ❌ Confusing temperature with heat — Temperature is intensity, heat is energy
  • ❌ Negative Celsius to Kelvin — e.g., -40°C = 233 K (not negative!)
  • ❌ Wrong calibration formula — Always use: T/100 = (X - X_0)/(X_100 - X_0)
