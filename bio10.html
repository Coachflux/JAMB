Reproduction

Definition: Reproduction is the biological process by which living organisms produce new individuals (offspring) of the same species. It ensures the continuation of the species and is one of the essential characteristics of life. Organisms reproduce either asexually (one parent) or sexually (two parents, fusion of gametes).

Key Differences: Asexual vs. Sexual Reproduction

Asexual: One parent, no gametes/fusion, offspring genetically identical (clones), fast, no variation (good in stable environments).

Sexual: Two parents (usually), gamete fusion, genetic variation, slower, promotes adaptation (good in changing environments).

a. Asexual Reproduction

Single parent produces offspring without gametes. Common in simple organisms and plants.

i. Fission

Parent cell divides into two or more equal parts.

Examples: Paramecium (binary fission), Amoeba (binary fission), E. coli bacteria (binary fission), Euglena (longitudinal fission), Plasmodium (multiple fission).

Advantage: Very rapid multiplication.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Binary Fission in Amoeba):

  • Parent cell: Original Amoeba before division
  • Nucleus division: Nucleus splits into two
  • Cytoplasm cleavage: Cell pinches in middle
  • Daughter cells: Two identical offspring

ii. Budding

Small outgrowth (bud) forms on parent, grows, and detaches.

Examples: Yeast, Hydra, Spongilla sponge, Obelia coral, Planaria flatworm.

Advantage: Useful for sessile or colonial organisms.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Budding in Hydra):

  • Parent body: Main Hydra
  • Bud: Outgrowth with tentacles forming
  • Detachment: Bud separates to become new individual

iii. Natural Vegetative Propagation

Plants use vegetative parts (no seeds).

Examples:

  • Rhizomes: Ginger, turmeric, grass, bamboo.
  • Tubers: Yam, Irish potato, sweet potato.
  • Bulbs: Onion, garlic, lily.
  • Runners/stolons: Strawberry, spider plant.
  • Suckers: Banana, pineapple, plantain.

Application: Rapid multiplication of desirable crop traits (e.g., cassava stems).

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Vegetative Propagation):

  • Rhizome: Underground stem (ginger)
  • Tuber: Swollen underground stem (potato)
  • Bulb: Underground bud (onion)
  • Runner: Horizontal stem (strawberry)
  • Sucker: New shoot from base (banana)

iv. Artificial Vegetative Propagation

Human methods for agriculture.

Examples: Cuttings (cassava, hibiscus), Grafting (mango, citrus), Budding (roses), Layering (guava).

Applications: Preserve high-yield/disease-resistant traits; common in orchards.

b. Sexual Reproduction in Flowering Plants

Involves fusion of male (pollen) and female (ovule) gametes ‚Üí genetic variation.

i. Floral Parts and Their Functions

  • Sepals (calyx): Protect bud (green).
  • Petals (corolla): Attract pollinators (coloured).
  • Stamens (male): Anther (pollen), filament (support).
  • Pistil/carpel (female): Stigma (pollen receipt), style (tube), ovary (ovules).

Essential: Stamens + pistil for reproduction.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Flower Structure):

  • Sepals: Green protective covering
  • Petals: Attract pollinators
  • Stamen: Male part (anther + filament)
  • Pistil: Female part (stigma, style, ovary)
  • Ovary: Contains ovules

ii. Pollination and Fertilization

Pollination: Pollen from anther to stigma.

  • Self-pollination: Same flower/plant (e.g., pea, tomato, rice).
  • Cross-pollination: Different plants (e.g., apple via bees). Advantages: Genetic diversity, hybrid vigour, disease resistance.

Double Fertilization (unique to angiosperms): Pollen tube grows to ovule. Two male gametes:

  • One fuses with egg ‚Üí zygote (2n) ‚Üí embryo.
  • One fuses with two polar nuclei ‚Üí triploid endosperm (3n) ‚Üí nutritive tissue.

Examples: Maize (endosperm = kernel), coconut (liquid endosperm).

Importance: Provides food for developing embryo; efficient seed formation.

Pollen Tube Growth

After pollination, pollen grain germinates on stigma ‚Üí pollen tube grows down style ‚Üí carries two male gametes to ovule in ovary. Tube nucleus guides growth; tube tip releases gametes near embryo sac.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Pollen Tube Growth):

  • Pollen grain: On stigma surface
  • Pollen tube: Grows through style to ovary
  • Style: Pathway for tube growth
  • Ovary: Contains ovule/embryo sac
  • Male gametes: Two sperm cells released into ovule

Embryo Sac (Female Gametophyte)

Inside ovule: 7-celled, 8-nucleate structure. Forms after megaspore mitosis. Contains egg cell (female gamete), two synergids, three antipodals, central cell with two polar nuclei.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Embryo Sac):

  • Egg cell: Female gamete (fuses with sperm ‚Üí zygote)
  • Synergids: Guide pollen tube, one degenerates
  • Central cell: Two polar nuclei (fuse with sperm ‚Üí endosperm)
  • Antipodals: Degenerate after fertilization
  • Micropyle: Entry point for pollen tube

iii. Products of Sexual Reproduction

Seed: From fertilized ovule (testa, embryo, endosperm/cotyledons).

Fruit: From ovary; protects seed, aids dispersal.

Placentation: Determines fruit type (marginal ‚Üí pea; axile ‚Üí tomato; parietal ‚Üí cucumber; basal ‚Üí sunflower; free central ‚Üí capsule).

Fruit types: Simple (mango), aggregate (raspberry), multiple (pineapple), succulent (orange).

c. Reproduction in Mammals (e.g., Humans)

i. Structures and Functions

Male: Testes (sperm + testosterone), epididymis (mature sperm), vas deferens (transport), glands (semen), penis (delivery). Millions of small, motile sperm continuously.

Female: Ovaries (ova + oestrogen/progesterone), fallopian tubes (fertilization), uterus (embryo growth), vagina (entry/birth). One large ovum monthly (cyclic).

Differentiate: Males continuous; females cyclic.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Mammalian Reproduction):

  • Testes: Produce sperm & testosterone
  • Ovaries: Produce ova & hormones
  • Fallopian tube: Site of fertilization
  • Uterus: Embryo implantation & growth

ii. Fertilization and Development

Sperm fuses with ovum in fallopian tube ‚Üí zygote. Zygote ‚Üí cleavage ‚Üí embryo implants in uterus. Placenta exchanges nutrients/oxygen/waste. Gestation ~9 months ‚Üí birth.

Mother's health/nutrition/drugs: Poor diet ‚Üí low birth weight; rubella ‚Üí defects; alcohol/smoking/drugs ‚Üí fetal alcohol syndrome, delays, abnormalities. Avoid teratogens.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Fetus in Womb):

  • Fetus: Developing offspring after 8 weeks
  • Placenta: Nutrient/oxygen/waste exchange
  • Umbilical cord: Connects fetus to placenta
  • Amniotic sac: Fluid-filled protection
  • Uterus: Site of fetal growth
  • Cervix: Lower uterus opening

Note on Twins (in Mammals/Humans)

Identical (Monozygotic): From one fertilized egg that splits early ‚Üí same DNA, same sex, look alike (genetically identical). Cause: Unknown (not hereditary).

Fraternal (Dizygotic): From two separate eggs fertilized by two different sperm ‚Üí two zygotes. Different DNA (like siblings), can be same/opposite sex, look different/similar. More common; hereditary (runs in families, influenced by mother's genes).

Other rare types: Conjoined, mirror-image. IVF increases dizygotic twins.

Short Notes on Labeled Parts (Twins Comparison):

  • Identical (Monozygotic): One zygote splits ‚Üí genetically identical
  • Fraternal (Dizygotic): Two separate zygotes ‚Üí genetically different
  • Placenta: May be shared (identical) or separate (fraternal)

Modern Methods of Regulating Reproduction

Birth control: Condoms, pills, IUDs, implants, sterilization.

In vitro fertilization (IVF): Eggs + sperm fertilized in lab ‚Üí embryo to uterus (for infertility).

Artificial insemination.
