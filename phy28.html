📚 Chapter 28: Electrostatics

🎯 Learning Objectives: Understand electric charges, charging methods, electroscopes, Coulomb's law, electric fields and potential, and lightning protection for JAMB success.

1️⃣ Fundamental Concepts of Charges ⚡

Existence of Positive and Negative Charges

All matter contains electric charges. There are two types:

  • Positive (+): Carried by protons (in nucleus)
  • Negative (−): Carried by electrons (orbiting nucleus)
Key Principle: Like charges repel, unlike charges attract.
Property Proton Electron
Charge +e = +1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C −e = −1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
Mass 1.67 × 10⁻²⁷ kg 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg
Location Nucleus Electron shells
SI Unit of Charge: Coulomb (C)

2️⃣ Methods of Charging a Body 🔋

A. Charging by Friction (Triboelectric Effect)

When two different materials rub against each other, electrons transfer from one to another.

  • Glass rod + Silk: Glass loses electrons → becomes positively charged
  • Ebonite rod + Fur: Ebonite gains electrons → becomes negatively charged
Memory Tip: "Glass is Posh (Positive), Ebonite is Negative"

B. Charging by Contact (Conduction)

A charged body touches a neutral body, transferring charge directly.

  1. Charged rod touches neutral metal sphere
  2. Charges flow until both share same charge
  3. Both end up with same type of charge

Example: Touching a negatively charged rod to a neutral electroscope → electroscope becomes negative.

C. Charging by Induction (No Contact!)

Charging a body without touching it, using a charged object nearby.

  1. Bring charged rod near (but not touching) neutral conductor
  2. Induced charges separate: opposite charge near rod, like charge far away
  3. Ground the far side (touch with finger) – charges escape to earth
  4. Remove ground first, then remove rod
  5. Conductor retains charge opposite to the rod
JAMB Shortcut: Induction always produces opposite charge to the inducing object.

3️⃣ The Electroscope ⚡

An instrument for detecting and testing electric charges.

Gold-Leaf Electroscope Components

  • Metal cap: Receives charge
  • Metal rod
  • Gold leaf: Moves when charged
  • Insulating support
  • Earthed metal case

Uses

  1. Detecting charge: Leaf diverges when charged
  2. Identifying charge type: Using a known charge
  3. Comparing charge magnitudes: Greater divergence = more charge
  4. Testing insulation vs. conduction

Charging an Electroscope by Induction

  • Bring negative rod near cap → leaf diverges (positive induced at top, negative at leaf)
  • Ground the cap → negative charges escape
  • Remove ground → remove rod → electroscope left with positive charge

4️⃣ Coulomb's Inverse Square Law 🧲

The Law

The force between two point charges is:

  • Directly proportional to the product of the charges
  • Inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them
F = k(q₁q₂)/r²

Where:
• F = Force (N)
• q₁, q₂ = Charges (C)
• r = Distance between charges (m)
• k = 1/(4πε₀) = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²C⁻²
• ε₀ = permittivity of free space = 8.85 × 10⁻¹² C²N⁻¹m⁻²
  • Force is attractive if charges are opposite
  • Force is repulsive if charges are alike
  • If distance doubles, force becomes 1/4 (inverse square!)

🔢 Calculation 1: Coulomb's Law (Basic)

Question: Two charges of +3μC and −5μC are placed 0.3m apart in air. Calculate the force between them.

Given:
• q₁ = +3μC = +3 × 10⁻⁶ C
• q₂ = −5μC = −5 × 10⁻⁶ C
• r = 0.3 m
• k = 9 × 10⁹ Nm²C⁻²

F = k(q₁q₂)/r²
F = (9 × 10⁹)(3 × 10⁻⁶)(5 × 10⁻⁶)/(0.3)²
F = (9 × 3 × 5 × 10⁹⁻⁶⁻⁶)/0.09
F = (135 × 10⁻³)/0.09
F = 1500 × 10⁻³ = 1.5 N
Answer: 1.5 N (Attractive - opposite charges)

🔢 Calculation 2: Finding Distance

Question: Two identical charges of +2μC repel each other with a force of 0.36N. Find their separation.

F = k(q²)/r² → r² = kq²/F

r² = (9 × 10⁹)(2 × 10⁻⁶)²/0.36
r² = (9 × 10⁹)(4 × 10⁻¹²)/0.36
r² = (36 × 10⁻³)/0.36 = 0.1
r = √0.1 = 0.316 m = 31.6 cm
Answer: 31.6 cm

5️⃣ Electric Field 🌐

Definition

A region of space where an electric charge experiences a force.

Electric Field Intensity (E)

Force per unit positive charge at a point.

E = F/q = kQ/r²

Unit: N/C or V/m

Electric Field Patterns

Charge Configuration Field Pattern Description
Single positive Radial lines pointing outward
Single negative Radial lines pointing inward
Two like charges Lines repel each other (no crossing)
Two opposite charges Lines go from + to − (attract)
Parallel plates Uniform, parallel lines (except at edges)

Rules for Drawing Field Lines

  1. Lines start from positive, end at negative
  2. Lines never cross each other
  3. Density of lines shows field strength
  4. Lines are perpendicular to conductor surfaces

🔢 Calculation 3: Electric Field Intensity

Question: Calculate the electric field intensity at a distance of 0.5m from a point charge of +4μC.

E = kQ/r²
E = (9 × 10⁹)(4 × 10⁻⁶)/(0.5)²
E = (36 × 10³)/0.25
E = 144 × 10³ = 1.44 × 10⁵ N/C
Answer: 1.44 × 10⁵ N/C (Radially outward)

6️⃣ Electric Potential ⚡

Electric Potential (V)

Work done per unit charge in bringing a positive test charge from infinity to that point.

V = kQ/r

Unit: Volt (V) = J/C

Potential Difference (p.d.)

Work done per unit charge in moving between two points.

ΔV = Vₐ − Vᵦ = W/q

Relationship between E and V

E = ΔV/d

Where d = distance between points (for uniform field)

🔢 Calculation 4: Electric Potential

Question: Find the electric potential at 0.2m from a charge of −6μC.

V = kQ/r
V = (9 × 10⁹)(−6 × 10⁻⁶)/0.2
V = (−54 × 10³)/0.2
V = −270 × 10³ = −2.7 × 10⁵ V
Answer: −2.7 × 10⁵ V

🔢 Calculation 5: Work Done

Question: How much work is required to move a charge of +5μC between two points with a potential difference of 200V?

W = qΔV
W = (5 × 10⁻⁶)(200)
W = 1000 × 10⁻⁶ = 1 × 10⁻³ J = 1 mJ
Answer: 1 mJ

🔢 Calculation 6: Uniform Electric Field

Question: Two parallel plates are 2cm apart with a p.d. of 400V. Find: (a) Electric field intensity, (b) Force on an electron.

(a) E = V/d = 400/0.02 = 20,000 V/m = 2 × 10⁴ V/m

(b) F = qE = (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹)(2 × 10⁴) = 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁵ N
Answer: (a) 2 × 10⁴ V/m, (b) 3.2 × 10⁻¹⁵ N

🔢 Calculation 7: Multiple Charges

Question: Two charges +4μC and +6μC are placed 0.5m apart. Where should a −2μC charge be placed between them to experience zero net force?

Let x = distance from +4μC
For equilibrium: F₁ = F₂

k(4μC)(2μC)/x² = k(6μC)(2μC)/(0.5−x)²
4/x² = 6/(0.5−x)²
√2/x = √3/(0.5−x)
x = 0.5√2/(√2 + √3) = 0.225 m
Answer: 22.5 cm from +4μC charge

7️⃣ Electric Discharge and Lightning ⚡🌩️

Electric Discharge

Sudden flow of charge when potential difference becomes large enough to ionize the surrounding medium (air, gas, etc.).

Lightning Formation

  1. Charge separation: Updrafts in clouds cause friction, separating charges
  2. Induction: Bottom of cloud becomes negative, induces positive charge on ground
  3. Breakdown: When E-field exceeds 3 × 10⁶ V/m, air ionizes
  4. Stroke: Rapid discharge (current up to 30,000 A!)

Lightning Conductor (Protection)

  • Sharp metal rod on building top
  • Connected to earth via thick copper strip
  • Principle: Sharp point has high charge density → ionizes air (corona discharge) → slowly neutralizes cloud charge → provides easy path to ground if struck

🎯 JAMB Exam Tips

  1. Charging methods: Identify from diagrams (friction = transfer, induction = opposite)

  2. Coulomb's Law: Inverse SQUARE (not cube!) - if distance doubles, force becomes 1/4

  3. Field direction: E-field lines go OUT of + and INTO −

  4. Test charge: Always assumed positive for field direction

  5. Memory aids: "FRICTION = Transfer", "INDUCTION = Opposite", "Glass is Posh (Positive)"

  6. Units: Charge (C), Field (N/C or V/m), Potential (V), Force (N)

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ❌ Forgetting to convert μC to C (×10⁻⁶)
  • ❌ Using r instead of r² in Coulomb's law
  • ❌ Confusing electric field (vector) with potential (scalar)
  • ❌ Getting field direction wrong (test charge is positive!)
  • ❌ Forgetting that potential is scalar but field is vector
  • ❌ Mixing up charging by contact (same charge) vs induction (opposite charge)
