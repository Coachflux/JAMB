1️⃣ Definition of Electrolysis

Electrolysis is the process by which an electric current is passed through an electrolyte (molten or aqueous) causing chemical decomposition of the electrolyte into its constituents at the electrodes.

Basic electrolysis setup: anode (+), cathode (−), electrolyte, DC supply

Keyword check (definition)

  • Electric current + electrolyte + decomposition + electrodes

2️⃣ Electrolytes vs Non-Electrolytes

Electrolyte: conducts electricity when molten or in aqueous solution and is decomposed by the current.

Non-electrolyte: does not conduct electricity and is not decomposed.

Type Meaning Examples
Strong electrolytes Complete ionisation NaCl, HCl, H₂SO₄, NaOH
Weak electrolytes Partial ionisation CH₃COOH, NH₃(aq)
Non-electrolytes No ions formed Sugar, ethanol, urea, kerosene

3️⃣ Faraday’s Laws of Electrolysis

Faraday’s First Law

The mass (m) of a substance liberated is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (Q) passed.

m ∝ Q → m = ZQ = ZIt

Q = It

Faraday’s Second Law

For the same quantity of electricity passing through different electrolytes, masses liberated are proportional to their chemical equivalents.

m₁/Eq₁ = m₂/Eq₂

Equivalent mass: Eq = M/n

F constant

  • F = 96500 C mol⁻¹ (Faraday constant)

5️⃣ Factors Affecting Discharge of Ions

  • Position in electrochemical series (easier ions discharge first)
  • Concentration of ions (e.g., brine gives Cl₂, dilute NaCl gives O₂)
  • Nature of electrode (inert vs reactive)

Series + concentration + electrode type.

6️⃣ Uses of Electrolysis

  • Purification of copper (electrolytic refining)
  • Extraction of aluminium (Hall–Héroult process)
  • Production of NaOH, Cl₂, H₂ (chlor-alkali process)
  • Electroplating (silver, gold, chromium)
Electroplating: object to be plated is usually the cathode

7️⃣ Electrochemical Series (Order)

Learn this order: K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Sn > Pb > H > Cu > Ag > Au

Electrochemical series helps predict discharge and reactivity

How JAMB asks it

  • “Which metal displaces another from salt solution?”
  • “Which ion discharges first at cathode/anode?”

8️⃣ Corrosion of Iron & Prevention

Corrosion of iron is an electrolytic process (anodic oxidation + cathodic reduction) in the presence of water and oxygen.

Rusting is an electrochemical process on iron surface

Prevention methods

  • Painting
  • Galvanising (coat with Zn)
  • Cathodic protection (Mg/Zn sacrificial anode)
  • Grease/oil coating

🧮 6 JAMB Calculations (Worked)

Example 1: Mass deposited (Faraday 1st law)

0.5 A for 30 min deposits Cu (M=63.5, n=2). Find mass.

t = 30×60 = 1800 s

Q = It = 0.5×1800 = 900 C

m = (MIt)/(nF) = (63.5×900)/(2×96500) ≈ 0.296 g

Example 2: Time required

2 A deposits 0.635 g Cu. Find time. (M=63.5, n=2)

t = (m n F)/(M I) = (0.635×2×96500)/(63.5×2) ≈ 965 s ≈ 16.1 min

Example 3: Volume of gas at STP

Find volume of H₂ at STP liberated by 96500 C (n=2 for H₂).

mol e⁻ = Q/F = 1 mol

mol H₂ = 1/2 = 0.5 mol

V = 0.5×22.4 = 11.2 dm³

Example 4: E°cell calculation

Zn|Zn²⁺||Cu²⁺|Cu, E°Cu=+0.34 V, E°Zn=−0.76 V

E°cell = E°cathode − E°anode = 0.34 − (−0.76) = 1.10 V

Example 5: Equivalent mass

Equivalent mass of Al (M=27, n=3)

Eq = M/n = 27/3 = 9 g

Example 6: Faraday’s 2nd law (mass ratio)

Same Q deposits 1.08 g Ag (Eq=108) and x g Cu (Eq=31.75). Find x.

m₁/Eq₁ = m₂/Eq₂ → 1.08/108 = x/31.75

x = (1.08×31.75)/108 ≈ 0.3175 g

Tip

  • Always write n correctly (Cu²⁺ needs 2e⁻; Al³⁺ needs 3e⁻; H₂ needs 2e⁻).
