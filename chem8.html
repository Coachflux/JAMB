Acids, Bases and Salts 🚀

JAMB 2026 – New School Chemistry

(a) General Characteristics, Properties and Uses

Definition of Acids

An acid is a substance whose aqueous solution furnishes H₃O⁺ (hydronium) ions OR a proton (H⁺) donor.

Properties of Acids (learn these 7 points)

  • Sour taste (e.g. lemon)
  • Turn blue litmus paper red
  • Corrosive (burn skin)
  • Conduct electricity (because they produce ions)
  • React with metals to give H₂ gas
  • React with bases to form salt + water (neutralisation)
  • React with trioxocarbonate(IV) salts to give CO₂

Examples

  • Inorganic: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃
  • Organic: CH₃COOH (vinegar), citric acid (oranges), tartaric acid (grapes)

Basicity of an acid

Basicity = number of replaceable H⁺ ions per molecule

Definition of Bases

A base is a substance that furnishes OH⁻ ions in water OR a proton acceptor.

Properties of Bases

  • Bitter taste
  • Soapy/slippery feel
  • Turn red litmus paper blue
  • Corrosive (strong ones like NaOH)
  • Conduct electricity

Alkalis = soluble bases (NaOH, KOH, NH₃(aq), Ca(OH)₂)

Salts

A salt is an ionic compound formed when the H⁺ of an acid is replaced by a metal ion or ammonium ion.

Types of Salts:

  • Normal salts: NaCl, K₂SO₄
  • Acid salts: NaHSO₄, NaHCO₃
  • Basic salts: Mg(OH)Cl, Pb(OH)NO₃
  • Double salts: potash alum K₂SO₄·Al₂(SO₄)₃·24H₂O

Methods of Preparing Salts

  • Neutralisation: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
  • Acid + metal: Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂
  • Acid + trioxocarbonate: 2HCl + Na₂CO₃ → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂
  • Acid + basic oxide: 2HCl + CuO → CuCl₂ + H₂O
  • Precipitation: AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃

(b) Qualitative Comparison of Conductance

Strong acids/bases (HCl, H₂SO₄, NaOH, KOH) → completely ionise → high conductance.

Weak acids/bases (CH₃COOH, NH₃(aq)) → partially ionise → low conductance.

At same concentration, conductance of strong >> weak.

(c) pH and pOH Scale

pH = –log₁₀[H₃O⁺]  pOH = –log₁₀[OH⁻]  At 25 °C: pH + pOH = 14

6 JAMB Calculation Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

Example 1: pH of Strong Acid

JAMB Question: Calculate the pH of 0.01 M HCl solution.

Solution:
HCl is strong acid → [H⁺] = 0.01 mol/dm³ = 10⁻² mol/dm³
pH = –log(10⁻²) = 2
Answer: pH = 2 (acidic)

Example 2: pH of Strong Base

JAMB Question: Calculate the pH of 0.001 M NaOH solution.

Solution:
NaOH is strong base → [OH⁻] = 0.001 = 10⁻³ mol/dm³
pOH = –log(10⁻³) = 3
pH = 14 – 3 = 11
Answer: pH = 11 (alkaline)

Example 3: pH of Weak Acid

JAMB Question: Calculate the pH of 0.1 M CH₃COOH (Ka = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵).

Solution:
For weak acid: [H⁺] = √(Ka × C) = √(1.8×10⁻⁵ × 0.1) = √(1.8×10⁻⁶) ≈ 1.34 × 10⁻³
pH = –log(1.34×10⁻³) ≈ 2.87
Answer: pH ≈ 2.87

Example 4: Titration (Find Concentration)

JAMB Question: 25 cm³ of 0.1 M HCl neutralises 20 cm³ of NaOH. Find concentration of NaOH.

Solution:
HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O (1:1 ratio)
Moles HCl = 0.1 × 25/1000 = 0.0025 mol
Moles NaOH = 0.0025 mol
Conc. NaOH = 0.0025 × 1000/20 = 0.125 M
Answer: 0.125 M

Example 5: Titration (Volume Calculation)

JAMB Question: What volume of 0.2 M NaOH will neutralise 30 cm³ of 0.15 M H₂SO₄?

Solution:
H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O (1:2 ratio)
Moles H₂SO₄ = 0.15 × 30/1000 = 0.0045 mol
Moles NaOH = 2 × 0.0045 = 0.009 mol
Volume NaOH = 0.009 × 1000/0.2 = 45 cm³
Answer: 45 cm³

Example 6: Hydrolysis pH Prediction

JAMB Question: Predict whether 0.1 M NH₄Cl solution is acidic, basic or neutral.

Solution:
NH₄Cl is salt of strong acid (HCl) + weak base (NH₃)
NH₄⁺ hydrolyses: NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺
Produces H₃O⁺ → solution is acidic (pH < 7)
Answer: Acidic

(d) Acid/Base Titrations

Appropriate indicators:

  • Strong acid + strong base → phenolphthalein or methyl orange
  • Strong acid + weak base → methyl orange
  • Weak acid + strong base → phenolphthalein

(e) Hydrolysis of Salts

Rules:

  • Strong acid + strong base → neutral (pH = 7)
  • Strong acid + weak base → acidic
  • Weak acid + strong base → basic

Examples:

  • NH₄Cl → acidic (NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺)
  • Na₂CO₃ → basic (CO₃²⁻ + H₂O ⇌ HCO₃⁻ + OH⁻)
