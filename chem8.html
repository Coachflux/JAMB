Acids, Bases and Salts 🚀

JAMB 2026 – New School Chemistry

(a) General Characteristics, Properties and Uses

Definition of Acids

An acid is a substance whose aqueous solution furnishes H₃O⁺ (hydronium) ions OR a proton (H⁺) donor.

Properties of Acids (learn these 7 points)

  • Sour taste (e.g. lemon)
  • Turn blue litmus paper red
  • Corrosive (burn skin)
  • Conduct electricity (because they produce ions)
  • React with metals to give H₂ gas
  • React with bases to form salt + water (neutralisation)
  • React with trioxocarbonate(IV) salts to give CO₂

Examples

  • Inorganic: HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃
  • Organic: CH₃COOH (vinegar), citric acid (oranges), tartaric acid (grapes)

Basicity of an acid

Basicity = number of replaceable H⁺ ions per molecule

TypeExamplesBasicity
MonobasicHCl, HNO₃, CH₃COOH1
DibasicH₂SO₄, H₂CO₃2

Definition of Bases

A base is a substance that furnishes OH⁻ ions in water OR a proton acceptor.

Properties of Bases

  • Bitter taste
  • Soapy/slippery feel
  • Turn red litmus paper blue
  • Corrosive (strong ones like NaOH)
  • Conduct electricity

Alkalis = soluble bases (NaOH, KOH, NH₃(aq), Ca(OH)₂)

Strong Acid vs Weak Acid • Strong Base vs Weak Base

Strong acid: completely ionises in water (100% H⁺ ions), e.g. HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃ → very low pH, high conductance.

Weak acid: partially ionises (few H⁺ ions), e.g. CH₃COOH → higher pH than strong acid of same concentration, low conductance.

Strong base: completely ionises (100% OH⁻ ions), e.g. NaOH, KOH → very high pH, high conductance.

Weak base: partially ionises, e.g. NH₃(aq) → lower pH than strong base of same concentration, low conductance.

Methods of Preparing Salts

  • Neutralisation
  • Acid + metal
  • Acid + trioxocarbonate(IV)
  • Acid + basic oxide
  • Precipitation (double decomposition)

(b) Qualitative Comparison of Conductance

Strong acids/bases → completely ionise → high conductance.
Weak acids/bases → partially ionise → low conductance.
At same concentration, strong >> weak.

(c) pH and pOH Scale

pH = –log₁₀[H₃O⁺]  pOH = –log₁₀[OH⁻]  pH + pOH = 14 (25 °C)

6 JAMB Calculation Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions & Formulas

Example 1: pH of Strong Acid

Formula: pH = –log₁₀[H⁺]

Question: Calculate pH of 0.01 M HCl.

Step 1: HCl is strong → [H⁺] = 0.01 = 10⁻² mol/dm³
Step 2: pH = –log(10⁻²) = 2
Answer: pH = 2

Example 2: pH of Strong Base

Formula: pOH = –log₁₀[OH⁻]  pH = 14 – pOH

Question: Calculate pH of 0.001 M NaOH.

Step 1: NaOH strong → [OH⁻] = 0.001 = 10⁻³
Step 2: pOH = –log(10⁻³) = 3
Step 3: pH = 14 – 3 = 11
Answer: pH = 11

Example 3: pH of Weak Acid

Formula: [H⁺] = √(Kₐ × C)  pH = –log[H⁺]

Question: pH of 0.1 M CH₃COOH (Kₐ = 1.8 × 10⁻⁵).

Step 1: [H⁺] = √(1.8×10⁻⁵ × 0.1) = √1.8×10⁻⁶ ≈ 1.34×10⁻³
Step 2: pH = –log(1.34×10⁻³) ≈ 2.87
Answer: pH ≈ 2.87

Example 4: Titration (Find Concentration)

Formula: M₁V₁ = M₂V₂ (1:1 ratio)

Question: 25 cm³ 0.1 M HCl neutralises 20 cm³ NaOH. Find [NaOH].

Step 1: Moles HCl = 0.1 × 25/1000 = 0.0025 mol
Step 2: Moles NaOH = 0.0025 mol
Step 3: [NaOH] = 0.0025 × 1000/20 = 0.125 M
Answer: 0.125 M

Example 5: Titration (Volume Calculation)

Formula: M₁V₁n₁ = M₂V₂n₂ (n = basicity/acidity)

Question: Volume of 0.2 M NaOH to neutralise 30 cm³ 0.15 M H₂SO₄.

Step 1: Moles H₂SO₄ = 0.15 × 30/1000 = 0.0045 mol
Step 2: Moles NaOH = 2 × 0.0045 = 0.009 mol (n=2 for H₂SO₄)
Step 3: Volume NaOH = 0.009 × 1000/0.2 = 45 cm³
Answer: 45 cm³

Example 6: Hydrolysis pH Prediction

Formula: Salt of strong acid + weak base → acidic (produces H₃O⁺)

Question: Nature of 0.1 M NH₄Cl solution.

Step 1: NH₄Cl = strong acid (HCl) + weak base (NH₃)
Step 2: NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺
Step 3: Produces H₃O⁺ → acidic (pH < 7)
Answer: Acidic

(d) Acid/Base Titrations

Appropriate indicators:

  • Strong acid + strong base → phenolphthalein or methyl orange
  • Strong acid + weak base → methyl orange
  • Weak acid + strong base → phenolphthalein

(e) Hydrolysis of Salts

Rules (memorise):

  • Strong acid + strong base → neutral (pH=7)
  • Strong acid + weak base → acidic
  • Weak acid + strong base → basic
