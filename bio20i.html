🧬 Chapter 20: Variation in Population

Morphological vs physiological variation + continuous vs discontinuous + blood and blood groups

🌟 Meaning of Variation

Variation refers to the differences that exist among individuals of the same species (population). No two organisms are exactly alike (even identical twins can differ due to environment).

Variation exists in size, colour, and many body features

Main Causes of Variation

  • Genetic constitution (inherited genes from parents)
  • Environmental conditions (nutrition, climate, habitat, light, etc.)
  • Often both (especially for continuous traits)

1️⃣ Morphological Variations

Definition: Differences in physical appearance (outward structure/looks).

Morphological variation example: height and body size

Examples (JAMB)

  • Size: height, weight
  • Colour: skin, eye, hair; coat colour in animals; feathers/scales
  • Fingerprints: unique patterns (arches, loops, whorls)

🔑 Key Point

  • Most morphological variations are continuous.

Fingerprint types: arch, loop, whorl (used for identification)

2️⃣ Physiological Variations

Definition: Differences in how the body functions or reacts (not always visible externally).

Physiological variation example: tongue rolling (yes/no)

Examples (JAMB)

  • Ability to roll the tongue
  • Ability to taste PTC (taster vs non-taster)
  • Blood groups (ABO and Rhesus factor)

🔑 Key Point

  • Most physiological variations in humans are discontinuous (clear groups).

PTC tasting: taster vs non-taster (genetic)

📊 Continuous vs Discontinuous Variation

Feature Continuous Variation Discontinuous Variation
Definition Smooth range between extremes; no clear groups Distinct categories; no intermediates
Data type Quantitative (measured) Qualitative (counted)
Graph Histogram (often bell-shaped) Bar chart (separate bars)
Cause Many genes + environment (polygenic) One/few genes; little environment effect
Examples Height, weight, skin colour shade ABO blood groups, tongue rolling, PTC tasting

🩸 Blood (Extra Detail for JAMB)

Definition (Blood): Blood is a liquid connective tissue that circulates in blood vessels and transports substances around the body.

Blood is made of plasma + red cells + white cells + platelets

Components of Blood

Component Main Features Functions
Plasma Straw-coloured fluid (mostly water) Transports nutrients, hormones, CO₂, urea; distributes heat
Red blood cells (RBC) Contain haemoglobin; biconcave; no nucleus (in mammals) Transport oxygen (and some CO₂)
White blood cells (WBC) Have nucleus; different types Defend body (immunity): destroy pathogens, make antibodies
Platelets Cell fragments Blood clotting (prevents bleeding)

Functions of Blood (Quick List)

  • Transport: O₂, CO₂, digested food, hormones, wastes
  • Protection: WBC fight infection; antibodies
  • Clotting: platelets + clotting factors stop bleeding
  • Temperature regulation: distributes heat around the body

⚠️ Common JAMB Trap

  • RBC carry oxygen using haemoglobin (not WBC).
  • Platelets are for clotting.

🧪 Blood Groups (ABO & Rhesus)

Definition (Blood group): A classification of blood based on the presence or absence of specific antigens on the surface of red blood cells.

ABO groups depend on A and B antigens on red blood cells

ABO System: Antigens and Antibodies

Blood Group Antigen on RBC Antibody in Plasma Notes
A A antigen Anti-B Cannot receive B/AB (risk of agglutination)
B B antigen Anti-A Cannot receive A/AB
AB A and B antigens No anti-A/anti-B Universal recipient (ABO only)
O No A/B antigens Anti-A and Anti-B Universal donor (ABO only)

Rhesus (Rh) Factor

Definition: Rh factor depends on whether the D antigen is present on RBC.

  • Rh positive (Rh+): D antigen present
  • Rh negative (Rh-): D antigen absent

Rh incompatibility in pregnancy: Rh- mother and Rh+ baby can cause problems

⚠️ Rh in Pregnancy (JAMB loves this)

  • If an Rh- mother carries an Rh+ fetus, she may form anti-Rh antibodies after exposure to fetal blood.
  • These antibodies can affect a future Rh+ baby (haemolytic disease of newborn).
  • Prevention: anti-D injection (Rh immunoglobulin) after birth/miscarriage.

Agglutination (Why mismatching is dangerous)

Definition: Agglutination is the clumping of red blood cells when antibodies react with incompatible antigens (can block blood vessels and cause serious reactions).

Agglutination happens when blood groups are mismatched

🎯 Applications of Discontinuous Variation (JAMB)

1) Crime detection (Forensics)

Fingerprints are unique and used to identify suspects/victims

  • Fingerprints: identify individuals
  • Blood group/DNA: can link a suspect to a crime scene

2) Blood transfusion

Rule: Match ABO and Rh to prevent agglutination.

ABO shortcuts: O = universal donor; AB = universal recipient (ABO only).

3) Paternity determination (Blood group logic)

Key idea: Blood groups can exclude a person (not prove 100%). DNA is more accurate, but JAMB tests ABO reasoning.

  • If child is AB, parents cannot both be O.
  • AB requires A allele and B allele.

⚠️ JAMB Tip

  • Know ABO compatibility and simple inheritance logic — common MCQs.
