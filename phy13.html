📚 Chapter 13: Quantity of Heat

🎯 Learning Objectives: Differentiate between heat capacity and specific heat capacity, determine both using simple methods, and solve numerical problems involving heat calculations.

🔑 Core Concepts 🔥

1. Heat as a Form of Energy

  • Heat is energy transferred due to temperature difference
  • Measured in Joules (J) or calories (cal)
  • 1 calorie = 4.2 J (approximately)

2. Heat Capacity (C)

Quantity of heat required to raise the temperature of a whole body by 1 Kelvin.

C = Q/ΔT

Where:
C = Heat capacity (J/K or J/°C)
Q = Quantity of heat energy (J)
ΔT = Temperature change (K or °C)

3. Specific Heat Capacity (c)

Quantity of heat required to raise unit mass (1 kg) of a substance by 1 Kelvin.

MASTER FORMULA:
c = Q/(mΔT)

OR

Q = mcΔT

Where:
c = Specific heat capacity (J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹)
m = Mass (kg)
Q = Heat energy (J)
ΔT = Temperature change (K or °C)

4. Relationship Between C and c

Property Heat Capacity (C) Specific Heat Capacity (c)
Definition Heat to raise whole object by 1K Heat to raise 1kg by 1K
Property of The whole object The material itself
Mass dependence Depends on mass AND material Independent of mass
Unit J K⁻¹ or J °C⁻¹ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
Formula link C = mc or c = C/m

🧪 Methods of Determination 🔬

Method Principle Formula/Approach Best For
Method of Mixtures Heat lost by hot body = Heat gained by cold body Q_lost = Q_gained Solids and liquids
Electrical Method Electrical energy converts to heat Q = IVt = I²Rt = V²t/R Liquids, precise measurements
Newton's Law of Cooling Rate of heat loss ∝ temperature difference dQ/dt ∝ (θ - θ₀) High temperature measurements

🔢 7 Essential Calculations for JAMB

Example 1: Basic Specific Heat Capacity

Question: Calculate temperature change when 500 J heat is supplied to 100 g water. [c_water = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹]

Solution:
Given: Q = 500 J, m = 100 g = 0.1 kg, c = 4200 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹

Using Q = mcΔT:
ΔT = Q/(mc) = 500/(0.1 × 4200)
ΔT = 500/420 = 1.19 ≈ 1.2°C
Answer: 1.2°C (or 1.2 K, same interval)
JAMB Tip: Always convert mass to kg first!

Example 2: Heat Capacity of a Calorimeter

Question: Aluminium block heated by 30 W heater. Temperature rises 100°C in 5 minutes. Find heat capacity.

Solution:
Given: P = 30 W, t = 5 min = 300 s, ΔT = 100 K

Heat supplied: Q = Pt = 30 × 300 = 9000 J

Using Q = CΔT:
C = Q/ΔT = 9000/100 = 90 J K⁻¹
Answer: 90 J K⁻¹

Example 3: Method of Mixtures (Equilibrium Temperature)

Question: Metal mass 0.5 kg at 100°C transferred to calorimeter (C = 80 J K⁻¹) containing water (C = 420 J K⁻¹) at 20°C. Find equilibrium temperature. [c_metal = 400 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹]

Solution:
Let final temperature = θ

Heat lost by metal:
Q_lost = mc(100 - θ) = 0.5 × 400 × (100 - θ) = 200(100 - θ)

Heat gained by calorimeter + water:
Q_gained = 80(θ - 20) + 420(θ - 20) = 500(θ - 20)

Setting equal: 200(100 - θ) = 500(θ - 20)
20000 - 200θ = 500θ - 10000
30000 = 700θ
θ = 30000/700 = 42.9°C ≈ 43°C
Answer: Approximately 43°C

Example 4: Finding Specific Heat Capacity

Question: Metal mass 0.5 kg at 100°C transferred to calorimeter (C = 80 J K⁻¹) with water (C = 420 J K⁻¹) at 20°C. Equilibrium temperature is 44°C. Find specific heat capacity of metal.

Solution:
Given: m_metal = 0.5 kg, T_metal = 100°C, T_initial = 20°C, T_final = 44°C
C_cal = 80 J K⁻¹, C_water = 420 J K⁻¹

Heat lost by metal: Q_lost = 0.5 × c × (100 - 44) = 28c

Heat gained: Q_gained = 80(44-20) + 420(44-20) = 80(24) + 420(24)
Q_gained = 1920 + 10080 = 12000 J

Equating: 28c = 12000
c = 12000/28 = 428.6 ≈ 429 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
Answer: Approximately 430 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹

Example 5: Electrical Heating Method

Question: Electric heater rated 50 W immersed in liquid (m = 0.5 kg). Temperature rises from 25°C to 75°C in 210 s. Calculate specific heat capacity. Neglect heat losses.

Solution:
Given: P = 50 W, m = 0.5 kg, ΔT = 50°C, t = 210 s

Heat supplied: Q = Pt = 50 × 210 = 10500 J

Using Q = mcΔT:
c = Q/(mΔT) = 10500/(0.5 × 50) = 10500/25
c = 420 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
Answer: 420 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹

Example 6: Comparing Two Substances

Question: Two liquids X and Y with same mass receive same quantity of heat. Temperature rise in X is twice that of Y. Find ratio c_X : c_Y.

Solution:
Given: m_X = m_Y = m, Q_X = Q_Y = Q, ΔT_X = 2ΔT_Y

For liquid X: Q = m c_X ΔT_X = m c_X (2ΔT_Y)
For liquid Y: Q = m c_Y ΔT_Y

Equating: m c_X (2ΔT_Y) = m c_Y ΔT_Y
2c_X = c_Y
c_X/c_Y = 1/2
Answer: 1:2 (or c_X : c_Y = 1:2)

Example 7: Heat with Change of State (Combined)

Question: Calculate heat required to melt completely 1 kg ice at -30°C. [L_fusion = 3.5 × 10⁵ J kg⁻¹, c_ice = 2.1 × 10³ J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹]

Solution:
Stage 1: Heat ice from -30°C to 0°C
Q₁ = mcΔT = 1 × 2100 × 30 = 63000 J

Stage 2: Melt ice at 0°C
Q₂ = mL = 1 × 3.5 × 10⁵ = 350000 J

Total heat: Q_total = 63000 + 350000
Q_total = 413000 J = 4.13 × 10⁵ J
Answer: 4.13 × 10⁵ J (or 413 kJ)

📝 Quick Reference: Common Specific Heat Capacities

Substance Specific Heat Capacity (J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹) Notes
Water 4200 Highest of common substances
Ice 2100 Half of liquid water
Aluminium 900 Relatively high for metal
Copper 390 Good conductor, lower c
Iron/Steel 450 Common structural metal
Lead 130 Very low, heats quickly
Mercury 140 Liquid metal, low c

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Unit conversion errors: Always convert mass to kg, time to seconds
  • Temperature vs Heat: Temperature is hotness, heat is energy transferred
  • C vs c: Remember which depends on mass
  • Sign conventions: Heat lost = negative, heat gained = positive
  • Equilibrium: Identify which bodies lose heat and which gain

🎓 JAMB Exam Tips

  • Convert units first before plugging into formulas
  • Check magnitude: Water = 4200, metals = 400-900 J kg⁻¹ K⁻¹
  • Method of mixtures: Q_lost = Q_gained
  • Electrical method: Q = IVt (power × time)
  • Latent heat: Break into stages (heating + change of state)
