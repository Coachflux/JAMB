📘 Chapter 10: Pressure

🎯 Learning Objectives: By the end of this chapter, you should be able to recognize the SI unit of pressure (Pascal), identify pressure-measuring instruments, relate pressure variation to height, use a barometer as an altimeter, determine the relationship between pressure, depth, and density (P = ρgh), apply Pascal's principle, and solve problems involving pressure in liquids.

Part A: Atmospheric Pressure 🌤️

1. Definition

Atmospheric pressure is the pressure exerted by the weight of air in the atmosphere acting upon any surface in contact with it.

2. SI Unit

  • Pascal (Pa) — the standard unit of pressure
  • Other common units: mmHg, atm, bar

3. Measuring Instruments

Instrument How It Works
Simple Mercury Barometer Glass tube filled with mercury inverted in a dish. Measures atmospheric pressure by the height of mercury column
Aneroid Barometer Sealed, evacuated metal box with flexible lid. Uses mechanical expansion/contraction to measure pressure changes
Manometer U-shaped tube containing liquid. Measures pressure difference between two points

4. Variation with Height

  • Atmospheric pressure decreases with increasing altitude
  • The higher you go, the less air above you → lower pressure

5. Barometer as Altimeter

Since pressure decreases predictably with height, barometers can measure altitude. Used in aircraft and mountaineering to determine height above sea level.

Part B: Pressure in Liquids 💧

1. Fundamental Relationship

P = ρgh

Where:
P = Pressure (Pa)
ρ (rho) = Density of liquid (kg/m³)
g = Acceleration due to gravity (9.8 or 10 m/s²)
h = Depth/height of liquid column (m)

2. Key Properties of Liquid Pressure

  • Increases with depth: The deeper you go, the greater the pressure
  • Independent of container shape: Same depth = same pressure in any container
  • Acts equally in all directions: Pressure is transmitted uniformly
  • Depends on liquid density: Denser liquids exert more pressure at same depth

3. Pascal's Principle

"Pressure applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every portion of the fluid and the walls of the containing vessel."

Applications:

  • Hydraulic presses — used in car jacks, braking systems
  • Hydraulic lifts — lifting heavy vehicles in garages
  • Brake systems — force multiplication in vehicle brakes

🧮 7 Essential Calculations for JAMB

Calculation 1: Absolute Pressure at Depth

Question: Calculate the absolute pressure at the bottom of a lake at a depth of 32.8 m. Assume density of water = 1000 kg/m³, g = 10 m/s², Atmospheric pressure = 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa.

Solution:
Absolute Pressure = Atmospheric Pressure + Liquid Pressure
P_absolute = P_atm + ρgh

P_atm = 1.01 × 10⁵ Pa
ρgh = 1000 × 10 × 32.8 = 3.28 × 10⁵ Pa

P_absolute = 1.01 × 10⁵ + 3.28 × 10⁵
= 4.29 × 10⁵ Pa
Answer: 4.29 × 10⁵ Pa

Calculation 2: Total Pressure in Ocean

Question: The atmospheric pressure due to water is 1.3 × 10⁶ N/m². What is the total pressure at the bottom of an ocean 10m deep? [Density of water = 1000 kg/m³, g = 10 m/s²]

Solution:
P_total = P_atm + ρgh

P_atm = 1.3 × 10⁶ N/m²
ρgh = 1000 × 10 × 10 = 1.0 × 10⁵ N/m²

P_total = 1.3 × 10⁶ + 0.1 × 10⁶
= 1.4 × 10⁶ N/m²
Answer: 1.4 × 10⁶ N/m²

Calculation 3: Depth for One Atmosphere Change

Question: At what depth below sea-level would one experience a change of pressure equal to one atmosphere? [Density of sea water = 1013 kg/m³, 1 atm = 1.01 × 10⁵ N/m², g = 10 m/s²]

Solution:
P = ρgh
h = P / (ρg)

h = (1.01 × 10⁵) / (1013 × 10)
h = 101000 / 10130
h ≈ 9.97 m ≈ 10.0 m
Answer: 10.0 m

Calculation 4: Hydraulic Press (Pascal's Principle)

Question: In a hydraulic press, the pump piston exerts a pressure of 100 Pa on the liquid. What force is exerted on the second piston of cross-sectional area 3 m²?

Solution:
According to Pascal's Principle: P₁ = P₂

P₁ = 100 Pa
A₂ = 3 m²

P₂ = F₂ / A₂
F₂ = P₂ × A₂ = 100 × 3 = 300 N
Answer: 300 N

Calculation 5: Hydraulic Car Lift

Question: A hydraulic lift has a small piston with radius 0.05 m and a large piston with radius 0.25 m. If a force of 100 N is applied to the small piston, what load can be lifted by the large piston?

Solution:
Pascal's Principle: P₁ = P₂
F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂

A₁ = πr₁² = π × (0.05)² = 0.007854 m²
A₂ = πr₂² = π × (0.25)² = 0.19635 m²

F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁)
F₂ = 100 × (0.19635 / 0.007854)
F₂ = 100 × 25 = 2500 N
Answer: 2500 N (or 2.5 kN)

Calculation 6: Pressure in a Mercury Column

Question: Calculate the pressure at the bottom of a mercury column 760 mm high. [Density of mercury = 13600 kg/m³, g = 9.8 m/s²]

Solution:
P = ρgh
h = 760 mm = 0.760 m

P = 13600 × 9.8 × 0.760
P = 101292.8 Pa
P ≈ 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa
Answer: 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa (or 101.3 kPa, equivalent to 1 atm)

Calculation 7: Comparing Pressures in Different Liquids

Question: Two liquids X and Y have densities of 800 kg/m³ and 1200 kg/m³ respectively. At what depth in liquid X will the pressure be equal to the pressure at a depth of 0.6 m in liquid Y?

Solution:
For equal pressures: P_X = P_Y
ρ_X × g × h_X = ρ_Y × g × h_Y

800 × h_X = 1200 × 0.6
h_X = (1200 × 0.6) / 800
h_X = 720 / 800
h_X = 0.9 m
Answer: 0.9 m (or 90 cm)

🔑 Key Formulas Summary

Formula Use
P = ρgh Calculate pressure in a liquid
P = F/A Basic pressure definition
P₁ = P₂ Hydraulic systems (Pascal's)
P_absolute = P_atm + ρgh Total pressure at depth
h = P/(ρg) Finding depth from pressure

💡 Quick Study Tips

  • Remember the units: Always use SI units (Pa, kg/m³, m, m/s²)
  • Draw diagrams: Sketch containers with liquid levels to visualize depth (h)
  • Practice conversions: Know how to convert between mmHg, cmH₂O, and Pascals
  • Real-world connections: Think about hydraulic systems, diving, and weather
  • Solve past questions: JAMB frequently tests pressure calculations

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing atmospheric pressure (air) with liquid pressure (water/fluids)
  • Forgetting that h is the vertical depth, not slant height
  • Mixing up density values — water = 1000 kg/m³, mercury = 13600 kg/m³
  • Not recognizing pressure acts equally in all directions
  • Forgetting to add atmospheric pressure when calculating absolute pressure
