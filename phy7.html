(a) Work, Energy, and Power

This section introduces the core ideas. New School Physics emphasizes practical applications and units.

(i) Definition of Work, Energy, and Power

Work (W): Work is done when a force causes an object to move in the direction of the force. It's the product of force and displacement (distance moved in the force's direction). Formula: W = F × s × cosθ, where F is force (in Newtons, N), s is displacement (in meters, m), and θ is the angle between force and displacement. If force is parallel to motion (θ = 0°), cos 0 = 1, so W = F s. Unit: Joule (J), where 1 J = 1 N m.

Example: Pushing a box 5 m with 10 N force straight ahead: W = 10 × 5 = 50 J.

Against gravity: W = m g h (lifting mass m height h, g = 10 m/s²).

Energy (E): The ability to do work. Energy is stored work or the capacity to perform work. Same unit as work: Joule (J).

Example: A raised object has energy because it can do work when falling.

Power (P): The rate of doing work or transferring energy. Formula: P = W / t or P = F v (force times velocity). Unit: Watt (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s.

Example: Doing 100 J of work in 10 s: P = 100 / 10 = 10 W.

Differentiate: Work is total energy transferred, energy is stored, power is how fast it's done.

(ii) Forms of Energy

Energy exists in various forms (New School Physics lists these with examples):

  • Kinetic Energy (KE): Energy due to motion. KE = ½ m v² (m = mass, v = velocity).
  • Example: A moving car.
  • Potential Energy (PE): Stored energy due to position. Gravitational PE = m g h.
  • Example: Water at the top of a hill.
  • Mechanical Energy: Sum of KE + PE.
  • Other forms: Heat (thermal), Light (radiant), Sound, Electrical, Chemical (e.g., in batteries), Nuclear (from atoms).

Compare: KE depends on speed, PE on height; heat is random molecular motion, chemical is from bonds.

(iii) Conservation of Energy

The law states: Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it can only be transformed from one form to another. Total energy in a closed system remains constant.

Example: A ball dropped from height h: Initial PE = m g h, converts to KE as it falls. At bottom, all PE becomes KE (ignoring air resistance). Total energy same.

(iv) Qualitative Treatment Between Different Forms of Energy

This means describing (not calculating) how energy changes forms without numbers.

  • Potential to Kinetic: Object falling (e.g., pendulum swing).
  • Chemical to Electrical: Battery powering a bulb.
  • Electrical to Heat/Light: Electric stove or bulb.
  • Nuclear to Heat/Electrical: In power plants.

Example: In a hydroelectric dam, water's PE → KE (flowing) → Mechanical (turbine) → Electrical.

(v) Interpretation of Area Under the Force-Distance Curve

The area under a force vs. displacement graph represents work done. If force is constant, area = rectangle (F × s). If variable, integrate or approximate.

Example: Straight line graph from 0 to s with constant F: Area = W = F s.

Why? Work is the integral of F ds, which is the area.

Solve problems: Use formulas like W = F s cosθ, P = W/t, conservation for transformations.

(b) Energy and Society

This connects physics to real-world issues, emphasizing sustainable use.

(i) Sources of Energy

Primary sources: Fossil fuels (coal, oil, gas), nuclear, renewables (solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, biomass).

Examples: Nigeria relies on oil (non-renewable) and hydro (renewable).

(ii) Renewable and Non-Renewable Energy

Renewable: Can be replenished naturally, e.g., sun (solar), wind, water (hydro), biomass (plants). Infinite supply, less pollution.

Non-Renewable: Finite, e.g., coal, crude oil, natural gas, uranium. Cause pollution when burned.

Distinguish: Renewables don't deplete, non-renewables do; examples: Solar vs. oil.

(iii) Uses of Energy

Domestic: Cooking, lighting.

Industrial: Manufacturing, transportation.

Agricultural: Irrigation, machinery.

Examples: Electricity for homes, petrol for cars.

(iv) Energy and Development

Energy drives economic growth: Powers industries, improves living standards, enables technology. Lack of energy hinders development (e.g., blackouts in developing countries).

Importance: For education (lights), health (hospitals), economy (factories).

(v) Energy Diversification

Using multiple sources to reduce risk (e.g., not relying only on oil). Nigeria aims to diversify to solar/wind.

Methods: Invest in renewables alongside fossils.

(vi) Environmental Impact of Energy

Global warming: CO₂ from burning fossils traps heat.

Greenhouse effect: Gases like CO₂, methane cause temperature rise.

Spillage: Oil spills pollute water/land (e.g., Niger Delta).

Analyze effects: Climate change, health issues, biodiversity loss.

Friendly sources: Solar/wind (low impact). Hazardous: Nuclear (radiation risk), fossils (pollution).

(vii) Energy Crises

Shortages due to depletion, high demand, wars, poor infrastructure. Results: High prices, blackouts.

Impact: Slows development.

(viii) Conversion of Energy

Transforming one form to another, e.g., solar panels (light to electrical), generators (mechanical to electrical).

Forms: Chemical → Heat (burning fuel), Heat → Mechanical (steam turbine).

(ix) Devices Used in Energy Production

Turbines (hydro/wind), solar panels, generators, nuclear reactors, batteries.

Identify uses in environment: Solar for homes, hydro for electricity. Safe use: Efficient appliances, conservation.

(c) Dams and Energy Production

(i) Location of Dams

Built on rivers with high flow and elevation drop (for PE). Examples: Kainji Dam (Nigeria) on Niger River.

(ii) Energy Production

Water stored at height (PE) → Released, flows (KE) → Turns turbine → Generator produces electricity. Efficiency depends on height/flow.

(d) Nuclear Energy

From splitting atoms (fission) in reactors. Uranium fuel → Heat → Steam → Turbine → Electricity.

Pros: High energy, low CO₂. Cons: Radioactive waste, accidents (e.g., Chernobyl).

Nigeria exploring nuclear for diversification.

(e) Solar Energy

(i) Photoelectric Effect

Light (photons) hits metal, ejects electrons if energy > work function. Einstein explained: Energy of photon = h f (h = Planck's constant, 6.6 × 10^{-34} Js; f = frequency).

Threshold frequency f₀: Minimum f for ejection. Work function φ = h f₀.

Example: If φ = 2 eV, light below f₀ won't eject electrons.

(ii) Solar Collector

Device absorbs sunlight for heat (e.g., flat plate with black surface, tubes for water heating).

(iii) Solar Panel for Energy Supply

Photovoltaic (PV) cells: Semiconductors convert light directly to electricity via photoelectric effect. Panels for homes/grids.

Photovoltaic cells: Use silicon; efficiency \~20%.

Work function: φ = h f (minimum energy to free electron; 1 eV = 1.6 × 10^{-19} J).

10 JAMB-Style Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

1. If the force and the velocity on a system are each reduced simultaneously by half, the power of the system is
A. doubled B. reduced to a quarter C. reduced by half D. constant
Step 1: Recall P = F × v.
Step 2: New F = F/2, new v = v/2.
Step 3: New P = (F/2) × (v/2) = (F v)/4 = P/4.
Step 4: Reduced to a quarter.
Step 5: Matches option B.
Answer: B. reduced to a quarter
2. A car of mass 800 kg attains a speed of 25 m/s in 20 s. The power developed in the engine is
A. 1.25 × 10⁴ W B. 2.50 × 10⁴ W C. 1.25 × 10⁶ W D. 2.50 × 10⁶ W
Step 1: Power = rate of change of kinetic energy.
Step 2: KE = ½ m v² = ½ × 800 × 25² = 400 × 625 = 250,000 J.
Step 3: t = 20 s.
Step 4: P = 250,000 / 20 = 12,500 W = 1.25 × 10⁴ W.
Step 5: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 1.25 × 10⁴ W
3. The work done by a force in moving an object over a distance of 4 m is 48 J. If the force is doubled, the work done over the same distance would be
A. 24 J B. 48 J C. 96 J D. 192 J
Step 1: Original W = F × s = 48 J, s = 4 m, F = 48 / 4 = 12 N.
Step 2: New F = 2 × 12 = 24 N.
Step 3: New W = 24 × 4 = 96 J.
Step 4: Matches option C.
Answer: C. 96 J
4. A machine whose efficiency is 75% is used to lift a load of 1000 N. If the velocity ratio of the machine is 4, find the effort required.
A. 187.5 N B. 333.3 N C. 533.3 N D. 1333.3 N
Step 1: Efficiency = (MA / VR) × 100%.
Step 2: 75 = (MA / 4) × 100 → MA / 4 = 0.75 → MA = 3.
Step 3: MA = load / effort → effort = load / MA = 1000 / 3 ≈ 333.3 N.
Step 4: Matches option B.
Answer: B. 333.3 N
5. A body falls from a height of 20 m. What is its kinetic energy just before hitting the ground? (g = 10 m/s², mass = 2 kg, ignore air resistance)
A. 200 J B. 400 J C. 600 J D. 800 J
Step 1: Conservation of energy: Initial PE = final KE.
Step 2: PE = m g h = 2 × 10 × 20 = 400 J.
Step 3: KE = 400 J.
Step 4: Matches option B.
Answer: B. 400 J
6. Which of the following is a renewable source of energy?
A. Coal B. Crude oil C. Wind D. Nuclear
Step 1: Renewable: Naturally replenished.
Step 2: Wind is renewable.
Step 3: Coal/oil/nuclear finite.
Step 4: Matches option C.
Answer: C. Wind
7. The environmental impact of burning fossil fuels includes
A. Reduction in global warming B. Greenhouse effect C. Energy diversification D. Safe energy use
Step 1: Burning releases CO₂.
Step 2: CO₂ causes greenhouse effect.
Step 3: Matches option B.
Answer: B. Greenhouse effect
8. In a hydroelectric dam, the energy conversion is mainly
A. Kinetic to potential B. Potential to kinetic to electrical C. Chemical to electrical D. Nuclear to heat
Step 1: Water height: Potential.
Step 2: Flows: Kinetic.
Step 3: Turbine: Mechanical to electrical.
Step 4: Matches option B.
Answer: B. Potential to kinetic to electrical
9. The work function of a metal is 3.2 eV. What is the threshold frequency? (h = 4.14 × 10^{-15} eV s)
A. 7.7 × 10^{14} Hz B. 5.5 × 10^{14} Hz C. 9.9 × 10^{14} Hz D. 4.4 × 10^{14} Hz
Step 1: φ = h f₀.
Step 2: f₀ = φ / h = 3.2 / 4.14 × 10^{-15} ≈ 7.7 × 10^{14} Hz.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 7.7 × 10^{14} Hz
10. A solar panel uses the photoelectric effect to convert
A. Heat to electrical energy B. Light to electrical energy C. Kinetic to potential energy D. Chemical to light energy
Step 1: Photoelectric effect: Photons eject electrons.
Step 2: In PV cells: Light → Electrical.
Step 3: Matches option B.
Answer: B. Light to electrical energy
