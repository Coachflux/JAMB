Introduction to Measurements

This topic is about how we measure things in physics accurately and consistently. Measurements help us describe the world using numbers and units, avoiding confusion (e.g., saying "5" without units could mean 5 m or 5 kg!).

Step 1: Fundamental Physical Quantities and Units

What are Fundamental Quantities?
These are basic building blocks of measurement that can't be broken down further. They're like the "alphabet" of physics. In New School Physics, they're listed as independent quantities we measure directly.

The seven fundamental quantities (and their SI units) are:

  • Length: Measures distance or size. Unit: meter (m). Example: Height of a person.
  • Mass: Measures amount of matter. Unit: kilogram (kg). Example: Mass of a book (not weightвҖ”more later).
  • Time: Measures duration. Unit: second (s). Example: Time to run a race.
  • Electric Current: Measures flow of charge. Unit: ampere (A). Example: Current in a wire.
  • Temperature: Measures hotness/coldness. Unit: kelvin (K). Example: Room temperature (often Celsius in daily life, Kelvin in physics).
  • Luminous Intensity: Measures brightness of light. Unit: candela (cd). Example: Light from a bulb.
  • Amount of Substance: Measures number of particles (atoms/molecules). Unit: mole (mol). Example: Moles of salt in water.

Why Fundamental? They don't depend on each other. You measure length with a ruler, mass with a balance, etc.

Tip for Easy Understanding: Think of a recipeвҖ”flour (kg), bowl size (volume, derived), cooking time (s). Fundamentals are the raw ingredients.

Step 2: Derived Physical Quantities and Units

What are Derived Quantities?
These are made by combining fundamental ones (like words from alphabet letters). We "derive" them using formulas.

Common Derived Quantities (from New School Physics):

  • Area: Length Г— Length. Unit: mВІ. Example: Floor space = length Г— width.
  • Volume: Length Г— Length Г— Length. Unit: mВі. Example: Water in a tank = length Г— width Г— height.
  • Speed: Length Г· Time. Unit: m/s. Example: Car speed = distance Г· time.
  • Force: Mass Г— Acceleration. Unit: newton (N) = kgВ·m/sВІ. Example: Push on an object.
  • Weight: Force due to gravity = Mass Г— g (g вүҲ 10 m/sВІ). Unit: N (not kgвҖ”kg is mass!). Example: Your weight on Earth.
  • Density: Mass Г· Volume. Unit: kg/mВі. Example: Density of water вүҲ 1000 kg/mВі.
  • Pressure: Force Г· Area. Unit: N/mВІ = pascal (Pa). Example: Air pressure.

How to Determine Units: Combine fundamental units.
Example: Speed = distance (m) Г· time (s) = m/s.

For irregular bodies: Use displacement method (submerge in water for volume) or formulas (sphere volume = 4/3ПҖrВі).

Step 3: Measuring Instruments

These tools are explained with diagrams in New School Physics. Here's how to use them simply:

  • Metre Rule: For lengths up to 1 m. Least count: 0.1 cm (1 mm). Use: Place along object, read from zero. For curves, use thread then measure.
  • Vernier Caliper: For precise lengths/diameters (e.g., pipe). Least count: 0.01 cm (0.1 mm). How: Slide jaws to object, read main scale + vernier scale (where lines match). Good for regular/irregular bodies.
  • Micrometer Screw Gauge: For tiny thicknesses (e.g., wire). Least count: 0.01 mm. How: Rotate screw till it grips, read sleeve + thimble.
  • Measuring Cylinder: For volume of liquids/irregular solids. Unit: cmВі or ml. How: Pour liquid, read at meniscus (curved surfaceвҖ”eye level). For solids: Displacement method (final volume - initial).
  • Beam Balance: For mass (e.g., Buchart's or chemical balance). Unit: kg or g. How: Place object on one pan, weights on other till balanced.
  • Stop Watch: For time. Unit: seconds (s). How: Start/stop for events. Digital ones precise to 0.01 s.

For Areas/Volumes: Regular shapes use formulas. Irregular: Divide into shapes or count squares on graph paper (area), or displacement (volume).

Tip: Always note least count (smallest measurement) for accuracy.

Step 4: Dimensions

Definition: Dimensions show how a quantity depends on fundamentals. Like a "fingerprint." We use [M] for mass, [L] for length, [T] for time, etc. Ignores numbers, focuses on powers.

Step 5: Limitations of Experimental Measurements

Measurements aren't perfect due to tools and humans. From New School Physics:

  • Accuracy of Instruments: Depends on least count. Metre rule: Вұ0.1 cm; micrometer: Вұ0.01 mm. Parallax error (wrong eye angle) reduces accuracy.
  • Random Errors: Vary (e.g., hand shake). Fix: Average multiple readings.
  • Systematic Errors: Consistent (e.g., zero error on caliper). Fix: Calibrate (subtract error).
  • Estimate Error: Error = Вұ half least count (e.g., ruler: Вұ0.05 cm).
  • Significant Figures: Digits that matter. 5.20 cm has 3 sig figs (precise to 0.01 cm). In multiplication, use least sig figs (2.1 Г— 3 = 6.3).
  • Standard Form: Scientific notation. 0.000045 = 4.5 Г— 10вҒ»вҒө. Big: 123000 = 1.23 Г— 10вҒө. Helps with large/small numbers.

Tip: For errors, think "how wrong could I be?"вҖ”estimate max/min possible.
