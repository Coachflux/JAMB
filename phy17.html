📚 Chapter 17: Vapours

🎯 Learning Objectives: Distinguish saturated/unsaturated vapors, relate S.V.P to boiling point, determine S.V.P using barometer method, explain dew/mist/fog/rain formation, calculate dew point and relative humidity, estimate humidity using hygrometers.

1️⃣ Types of Vapours ☁️

Unsaturated Vapour

A vapour NOT in contact with its own liquid.

  • Can hold more vapour (not at maximum capacity)
  • Obeys gas laws (Boyle's, Charles')
  • Pressure increases when compressed or cooled
  • Can be made saturated by adding more vapour or reducing temperature

Saturated Vapour

A vapour IN contact with its own liquid in a closed space.

  • Dynamic equilibrium (evaporation rate = condensation rate)
  • Does NOT obey gas laws
  • Pressure remains constant at constant temperature
  • Maximum amount the space can hold at that temperature
Feature Unsaturated Saturated
Contact with liquid No Yes
Obeys gas laws Yes No
Can add more vapour Yes No
Pressure on compression Increases Stays constant

2️⃣ Saturated Vapour Pressure (S.V.P) 💧

Definition: The pressure exerted by a vapour when in dynamic equilibrium with its liquid in a closed container at a given temperature.

S.V.P and Boiling Point

  • Boiling occurs when S.V.P = External (Atmospheric) Pressure
  • Water boils at 100°C at sea level (S.V.P = 760 mmHg = 1 atm)
  • Lower external pressure → Lower boiling point
  • Higher external pressure → Higher boiling point

✅ Real-Life Applications

  • Pressure cookers: Increase pressure → higher boiling point → faster cooking
  • High altitudes: Lower atmospheric pressure → water boils at <100°C

Factors Affecting S.V.P

  • Temperature: S.V.P increases with temperature (exponentially)
  • Nature of liquid: Volatile liquids have higher S.V.P
  • NOT affected by: Volume of container, amount of liquid, presence of other gases

3️⃣ Determination of S.V.P (Barometer Method) 🧪

Experimental Setup:

  1. Fill long glass tube completely with mercury
  2. Invert into mercury trough (Torricelli setup)
  3. Introduce small amount of liquid (water) using bent wire
  4. Liquid rises to top and evaporates, creating vapour
Measurement:
S.V.P = h₁ - h₂ (in mmHg)

Where:
• h₁ = Height of mercury column without vapour
• h₂ = Height of mercury column with vapour present

The vapour pushes down the mercury column. The difference gives vapour pressure.

4️⃣ Formation of Dew, Mist, Fog & Rain 🌧️

Phenomenon Formation Process
Dew Water vapour condenses on cool surfaces when air cools to dew point
Mist Tiny water droplets suspended in air near ground (visibility <1 km)
Fog Thicker mist with very low visibility
Rain Water droplets in clouds become too heavy and fall when air is saturated
Common factor: All occur when air is cooled to or below the dew point temperature, causing water vapour to condense.

5️⃣ Humidity and Dew Point 🌡️

Absolute Humidity

Mass of water vapour present in a unit volume of air (g/m³).

Relative Humidity (R.H)

The ratio of mass of water vapour present to mass required to saturate that volume at the same temperature, expressed as percentage.

Formula 1 (Using mass):
R.H = (Mass of water vapour present / Mass of water vapour at saturation) × 100%

Formula 2 (Using vapour pressures):
R.H = (S.V.P at dew point / S.V.P at air temperature) × 100%

Dew Point

The temperature at which air becomes saturated with water vapour (100% relative humidity). Below this temperature, condensation begins.

6️⃣ Hygrometry 🌫️

Wet and Dry Bulb Hygrometer

  • Dry bulb thermometer: Measures actual air temperature
  • Wet bulb thermometer: Bulb wrapped in wet cloth; measures cooling effect of evaporation

How It Works

  • Low humidity = High evaporation = Large temperature difference
  • High humidity = Low evaporation = Small temperature difference
  • 100% humidity = No evaporation = Both thermometers read same

✅ Using the Hygrometer Table

  1. Record dry bulb temperature (T₁)
  2. Record wet bulb temperature (T₂)
  3. Calculate difference: ΔT = T₁ - T₂
  4. Look up relative humidity in standard hygrometric tables

🔢 7 Essential Calculations for JAMB

Calculation 1: S.V.P from Barometer Readings

Question: Mercury column stands at 760 mm when dry and 710 mm when water is introduced. Find S.V.P.

Given:
h₁ (without vapour) = 760 mmHg
h₂ (with vapour) = 710 mmHg

Solution:
S.V.P = h₁ - h₂
S.V.P = 760 - 710
Answer: 50 mmHg

Calculation 2: Relative Humidity

Question: Air at 25°C contains 9.2 g/m³ water vapour. Saturation vapour density at 25°C is 23.0 g/m³. Calculate R.H.

Solution:
R.H = (9.2 / 23.0) × 100%
R.H = 0.4 × 100%
Answer: 40%

Calculation 3: Finding Dew Point

Question: Temperature is 30°C, R.H = 60%. Saturation densities: 30.4 g/m³ at 30°C, 19.8 g/m³ at 20°C. Estimate dew point.

Solution:
Actual vapour density = 60% of 30.4
= 0.60 × 30.4 = 18.24 g/m³

This ≈ saturation vapour density at 20°C
Therefore, dew point ≈ 20°C
Answer: ≈ 20°C

Calculation 4: Boiling Point at High Altitude

Question: At what temperature will water boil where atmospheric pressure is 600 mmHg? (S.V.P of water = 600 mmHg at 94°C)

Solution:
Boiling occurs when S.V.P = External pressure
External pressure = 600 mmHg
S.V.P = 600 mmHg at 94°C
Therefore, boiling point = 94°C
Answer: 94°C

Calculation 5: Relative Humidity Using S.V.P

Question: S.V.P at 20°C = 17.5 mmHg, at 10°C = 9.2 mmHg. If dew point is 10°C on a 20°C day, find R.H.

Solution:
R.H = (S.V.P at dew point / S.V.P at air temp) × 100%
R.H = (9.2 / 17.5) × 100%
R.H = 52.6%
Answer: ≈ 53%

Calculation 6: Pressure Cooker

Question: A pressure cooker cooks at 120°C. S.V.P of water at 120°C is 1489 mmHg. What is the pressure inside? (Atmospheric = 760 mmHg)

Solution:
At boiling: Pressure inside = S.V.P at that temperature
Absolute pressure = 1489 mmHg
Gauge pressure = 1489 - 760
Answer: 729 mmHg (gauge) or 1489 mmHg (absolute)

Calculation 7: Hygrometer Reading

Question: Wet and dry bulb hygrometer shows 20°C (dry) and 16°C (wet). Find R.H from the table.

Given Table:
Dry Bulb 20°C: Difference 2°C → 83%, 4°C → 66%, 6°C → 51%

Solution:
Temperature difference = 20 - 16 = 4°C
From table at 20°C dry bulb and 4°C difference
Answer: 66%

📝 Quick Revision Notes

  • Saturated vapour = In equilibrium with liquid, max pressure at that temp
  • Boiling point = Temperature where S.V.P = Atmospheric pressure
  • Relative humidity = (Actual/Saturation) × 100%
  • Dew point = Temperature where air becomes saturated
  • S.V.P depends only on temperature, not volume or amount of liquid
  • Evaporation occurs at all temperatures; boiling at fixed temperature

⚠️ Common Exam Tricks

  • ❌ Saturated vapour does NOT obey Boyle's law (unsaturated does)
  • ❌ S.V.P increases with temperature exponentially, not linearly
  • ❌ Boiling point decreases with altitude (lower pressure)
  • ❌ R.H of 100% means dew point = air temperature
  • ❌ Adding salt raises boiling point (colligative property)
