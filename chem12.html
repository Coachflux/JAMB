Rates of Chemical Reaction 🚀

JAMB 2026 – New School Chemistry

1. What is Reaction Rate?

Rate of reaction = change in concentration (or amount) of reactant or product per unit time.

Formula:

Rate = Δ[product] / Δt  or  Rate = –Δ[reactant] / Δt

The faster the reaction, the steeper the slope on a graph.

2. Factors that Change the Rate

JAMB wants you to identify the four main factors and explain how each works using collision theory.

(i) Temperature

  • Higher temperature → particles move faster → more frequent and energetic collisions → more particles have energy ≥ Activation Energy (Ea).
  • Rule of thumb: 10°C rise roughly doubles the rate.

JAMB examples:

  • HCl(aq) + Na₂S₂O₃(aq) → sulphur precipitate (hotter HCl = shorter time for cross to disappear)
  • Mg ribbon + HCl(aq) (hot acid = very fast effervescence)

(ii) Concentration / Pressure

  • More particles per unit volume → more collisions per second → faster rate.
  • For gases, higher pressure = higher concentration.

Examples:

  • Concentrated HCl + Na₂S₂O₃ = faster cloudiness
  • Iodine clock reaction: doubling concentration halves time to colour change

(iii) Surface Area

  • Smaller particles = larger total surface area → more collisions.

Example: Powdered marble + HCl = very fast CO₂; lumps = slow.

(iv) Catalyst

  • Provides alternative pathway with lower Ea; not used up.

Examples:

  • MnO₂ for H₂O₂ decomposition (fast bubbling)
  • MnO₂ for KClO₃ decomposition

3. Reaction Rate Curves

Typical graphs:

  • Concentration of reactant vs time (starts steep, flattens)
  • Volume of gas produced vs time (steep initial slope)

Higher temperature / concentration / surface area / catalyst → steeper initial slope and reaches end sooner.

4. Activation Energy & Collision Theory

Activation Energy (Ea): Minimum energy colliding particles must have before they react.

Collision Theory:

  • Particles must collide.
  • Collision must have energy ≥ Ea.
  • Particles must have correct orientation.

Higher temperature → more molecules have energy ≥ Ea.

5. 6 JAMB Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

Example 1: Calculate Rate from Concentration Change

Question: In a reaction, concentration of reactant falls from 0.8 mol/dm³ to 0.2 mol/dm³ in 120 s. Calculate average rate.

Solution:
Step 1: Δ[reactant] = 0.8 – 0.2 = 0.6 mol/dm³
Step 2: Rate = Δ[reactant] / Δt = 0.6 / 120 = 0.005 mol/dm³ s⁻¹
Answer: 0.005 mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹

Example 2: Effect of Temperature on Rate

Question: A reaction takes 40 s at 25 °C. At 35 °C it takes 20 s. By what factor does the rate increase?

Solution:
Step 1: Rate ∝ 1/time
Step 2: Rate at 25 °C = 1/40; at 35 °C = 1/20
Step 3: Factor = (1/20) / (1/40) = 2
Answer: Rate doubles (10 °C rise rule)

Example 3: Surface Area Comparison

Question: Powdered marble reacts with HCl in 10 s; same mass lumps take 80 s. By what factor is the rate faster for powder?

Solution:
Step 1: Rate ∝ 1/time
Step 2: Powder rate = 1/10; lumps = 1/80
Step 3: Factor = (1/10) / (1/80) = 8
Answer: Powder is 8 times faster

Example 4: Catalyst Effect

Question: H₂O₂ decomposes in 120 s without MnO₂; with MnO₂ it takes 15 s. By what factor does the catalyst increase the rate?

Solution:
Step 1: Rate ∝ 1/time
Step 2: Without = 1/120; with = 1/15
Step 3: Factor = 120/15 = 8
Answer: Rate increases by 8 times

Example 5: Initial Rate from Graph

Question: On a volume of gas vs time graph, the tangent at t=0 gives slope 0.04 cm³/s. What is initial rate?

Solution:
Step 1: Initial rate = slope of tangent at t=0
Step 2: Slope = 0.04 cm³/s
Answer: Initial rate = 0.04 cm³ s⁻¹

Example 6: Collision Theory & Temperature

Question: Explain why a 10 °C rise doubles the rate using collision theory.

Solution:
Step 1: Higher T → particles move faster
Step 2: More frequent collisions
Step 3: More particles have energy ≥ Ea (exponential increase)
Step 4: More successful collisions → rate doubles
Answer: More energetic & frequent collisions
