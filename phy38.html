📚 Chapter 38: Elementary Modern Physics

🎯 Learning Objectives: Master atomic models, photoelectric effect, radioactivity, nuclear energy, and quantum mechanics concepts for JAMB success.

1️⃣ Models of the Atom & Their Limitations ⚛️

A. Thomson's "Plum Pudding" Model (1897)

🍮 Concept

Atom is a sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded like plums in a pudding.

Limitation: Could not explain Rutherford's gold foil experiment (alpha particle scattering).

B. Rutherford's Nuclear Model (1911)

☀️ Concept

Atom has a small, dense, positively charged nucleus with electrons orbiting around it (like planets around the sun).

Limitations:

  • Predicted continuous spectrum (but atoms produce line spectra)
  • Predicted atoms should be unstable (electrons would spiral into nucleus)
  • Could not explain why electrons don't radiate energy while orbiting

C. Bohr's Model (1913) ⭐ Most Important for JAMB

🎯 Key Postulates

  1. Electrons move in specific stationary orbits (energy levels) without radiating energy
  2. Each orbit has a definite amount of energy
  3. Energy is emitted/absorbed only when electrons jump between orbits
  4. Angular momentum is quantized: mvr = nh/2π where n = 1, 2, 3...

Limitations:

  • Only works perfectly for hydrogen atom
  • Cannot explain spectra of multi-electron atoms
  • Doesn't account for fine structure of spectral lines
  • Ignores wave nature of electrons

3️⃣ Energy Levels & Spectra 🌈

Energy Level Diagram (Hydrogen Atom)

n=∞ ———— 0 eV (Ionization level - electron free)
n=4 ———— -0.85 eV
n=3 ———— -1.51 eV
n=2 ———— -3.4 eV ← Balmer series (Visible light)
n=1 ———— -13.6 eV ← Ground state (Lyman series - UV)
Key Formulas:
• Energy of nth level: Eₙ = -13.6/n² eV
• Frequency of emitted photon: hf = E₂ - E₁ or f = (E₂ - E₁)/h
• Wavelength: 1/λ = Rₕ(1/n₁² - 1/n₂²) where Rₕ = Rydberg constant
Type How Produced Example
Line/Emission Hot gas emitting light Neon signs, sodium lamp
Band Molecules Flames, molecular gases
Continuous Hot solids/liquids Light bulb, glowing iron

🔢 Calculation 1: Energy Levels

Question: Calculate the energy of a photon emitted when an electron in a hydrogen atom transitions from n=3 to n=2.

Using Eₙ = -13.6/n² eV:

Energy at n=3: E₃ = -13.6/3² = -13.6/9 = -1.51 eV
Energy at n=2: E₂ = -13.6/2² = -13.6/4 = -3.40 eV

Energy of photon: ΔE = E₃ - E₂ = -1.51 - (-3.40) = 1.89 eV
Answer: 1.89 eV (Red light in Balmer series)

4️⃣ Thermionic & Photoelectric Emissions ⚡

Thermionic Emission

  • Definition: Emission of electrons from a heated metal surface
  • Condition: Metal must be heated to high temperature
  • Application: Cathode ray tubes, X-ray tubes, electron guns

Photoelectric Effect ⭐ Very Important

🔑 Key Observations

  • No emission occurs below a threshold frequency (f₀) regardless of intensity
  • Maximum kinetic energy of electrons depends on frequency, not intensity
  • Number of electrons emitted depends on intensity of light

5️⃣ Einstein's Equation & Stopping Potential 🧮

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation:
hf = W₀ + ½mv²ₘₐₓ

Where:
• hf = Energy of incident photon
• W₀ = Work function (minimum energy to remove electron) = hf₀
• ½mv²ₘₐₓ = Maximum kinetic energy of emitted electron (Eₖ)

Stopping Potential (Vₛ):
eVₛ = ½mv²ₘₐₓ = hf - W₀
Vₛ = (hf - W₀)/e = h(f - f₀)/e
Key Values to Memorize:
• Planck's constant, h = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
• Electron charge, e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C
• Speed of light, c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s

🔢 Calculation 2: Photoelectric Effect

Question: Light of wavelength 400 nm falls on a metal surface with work function 2.0 eV. Calculate: (a) Photon energy in eV, (b) Maximum kinetic energy, (c) Threshold frequency.

(a) Energy of photon:
E = hc/λ = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴)(3 × 10⁸)/(400 × 10⁻⁹)
= 4.97 × 10⁻¹⁹ J
= 4.97 × 10⁻¹⁹/1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 3.11 eV

(b) Maximum kinetic energy:
Eₖ = hf - W₀ = 3.11 - 2.0 = 1.11 eV

(c) Threshold frequency:
W₀ = hf₀ → f₀ = W₀/h = (2.0 × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹)/(6.63 × 10⁻³⁴)
= 4.83 × 10¹⁴ Hz
Answers: (a) 3.11 eV (b) 1.11 eV (c) 4.83 × 10¹⁴ Hz

🔢 Calculation 3: Stopping Potential

Question: For the metal in Example 2, calculate the stopping potential.

eVₛ = Eₖ = 1.11 eV
Vₛ = 1.11 eV/e = 1.11 V
Answer: 1.11 V

7️⃣ Production of X-Rays 🩻

🔧 Simple Method: Coolidge Tube

  1. High voltage accelerates electrons from heated cathode (thermionic emission) toward metal target (anode)
  2. When high-speed electrons hit the target, X-rays are produced

Two Types of X-rays:

  • Characteristic X-rays: Produced when electrons knock out inner-shell electrons, and outer electrons fill the vacancy
  • Bremsstrahlung (Braking Radiation): Produced when electrons are decelerated by the nucleus

Properties of X-rays:

  • High penetrating power (pass through flesh, stopped by bones/lead)
  • Travel in straight lines
  • Not deflected by electric/magnetic fields (uncharged)
  • Cause fluorescence in certain materials
  • Can ionize gases and affect photographic plates

Property Alpha (α) Beta (β) Gamma (γ)
Nature Helium nucleus (⁴₂He) Fast electrons (⁰₋₁e) Electromagnetic waves
Charge +2e -e 0
Mass 4 amu 1/1840 amu 0

Ionizing Power Very high Moderate Very low
Penetrating Power Low (paper) Moderate (aluminum) High (thick lead)
Effect of E/B Fields Deflected slightly Deflected strongly (opposite to α) Not deflected
Decay Equations:
• Alpha decay: ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ⁻⁴_Z₋₂ Y + ⁴₂α
• Beta decay: ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_Z₊₁ Y + ⁰₋₁β + ν̄ (neutrino)
• Gamma decay: ᴬ_Z X* → ᴬ_Z X + γ (excited to ground state)

🔢 Calculation 4: Radioactive Decay

Question: A radioactive isotope has a half-life of 10 days. If the initial mass is 80g, how much remains after 30 days?

Number of half-lives: n = t/T½ = 30/10 = 3

Using: N = N₀(½)ⁿ
N = 80 × (½)³ = 80 × 1/8 = 10 g
Answer: 10 g

9️⃣ Half-Life & Decay Constant ⏱️

Key Definitions:
• Half-life (T½): Time taken for half of radioactive nuclei to decay
• Decay constant (λ): Probability of decay per unit time

Formulas:
• Number remaining: N = N₀e^(-λt) or N = N₀(½)^(t/T½)
• Activity: A = λN (decays per second, Becquerels, Bq)
• Relationship: T½ = ln(2)/λ = 0.693/λ

🔢 Calculation 5: Decay Constant

Question: Calculate the decay constant of a substance with half-life of 5 years.

T½ = 0.693/λ → λ = 0.693/T½
λ = 0.693/5 = 0.139 year⁻¹

Or in s⁻¹: λ = 0.693/(5 × 365 × 24 × 3600) = 4.39 × 10⁻⁹ s⁻¹
Answer: 4.39 × 10⁻⁹ s⁻¹ (or 0.139 year⁻¹)

🔢 Calculation 6: Activity

Question: A sample contains 10²⁰ radioactive nuclei with decay constant 2 × 10⁻⁶ s⁻¹. Calculate the activity.

A = λN = (2 × 10⁻⁶)(10²⁰) = 2 × 10¹⁴ Bq
Answer: 2 × 10¹⁴ Bq (or 200 TBq)

🔟 Nuclear Energy: Fission & Fusion 💥

Nuclear Fission

  • Definition: Splitting of a heavy nucleus into two lighter nuclei
  • Example: ²³⁵₉₂U + ¹₀n → ¹⁴¹₅₆Ba + ⁹²₃₆Kr + 3¹₀n + Energy
  • Application: Nuclear power plants, atomic bombs
  • Condition: Requires critical mass and neutron bombardment

Nuclear Fusion

  • Definition: Combining of two light nuclei to form a heavier nucleus
  • Example: ²₁H + ³₁H → ⁴₂He + ¹₀n + Energy
  • Application: Hydrogen bombs, stellar energy production (sun)
  • Condition: Requires extremely high temperature (10⁷ K) and pressure

🔢 Calculation 7: Energy from Fission

Question: In a fission reaction, the mass defect is 0.2 amu. Calculate the energy released.

Using 1 amu = 931 MeV:
E = Δmc² = 0.2 × 931 = 186.2 MeV

Convert to joules: 186.2 × 10⁶ × 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ = 2.98 × 10⁻¹¹ J
Answer: 186.2 MeV (or 2.98 × 10⁻¹¹ J)

1️⃣1️⃣ Binding Energy & Mass Defect ⚖️

Mass Defect (Δm):
Δm = [Zmₚ + Nmₙ] - mₙᵤcₗₑᵤₛ
Where Z = atomic number, N = neutron number

Binding Energy:
ΔE = Δmc²
1 amu = 931 MeV

Key Concept: Higher binding energy per nucleon = more stable nucleus (Iron-56 is most stable)

🔢 Calculation 8: Binding Energy

Question: Calculate binding energy per nucleon for helium-4 (⁴₂He) given: mₚ = 1.0073 amu, mₙ = 1.0087 amu, mₙᵤcₗₑᵤₛ = 4.0015 amu.

Helium-4 has 2 protons and 2 neutrons.

Mass of separated nucleons: 2(1.0073) + 2(1.0087) = 4.0320 amu
Mass defect: Δm = 4.0320 - 4.0015 = 0.0305 amu

Total binding energy: E = 0.0305 × 931 = 28.4 MeV
Binding energy per nucleon: 28.4/4 = 7.1 MeV/nucleon
Answer: 7.1 MeV/nucleon

1️⃣2️⃣ Wave-Particle Duality 🌊⚛️

De Broglie Hypothesis (1924):
Matter has both wave and particle properties
De Broglie wavelength: λ = h/p = h/(mv)

Electron Diffraction: Electron beams produce diffraction patterns (like X-rays) when passed through crystals, confirming wave nature of electrons.

🔢 Calculation 9: De Broglie Wavelength

Question: Calculate the de Broglie wavelength of an electron moving at 2 × 10⁶ m/s. (mₑ = 9.11 × 10⁻³¹ kg)

λ = h/(mv) = (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴)/((9.11 × 10⁻³¹)(2 × 10⁶))
= (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴)/(1.822 × 10⁻²⁴)
= 3.64 × 10⁻¹⁰ m = 0.364 nm
Answer: 3.64 × 10⁻¹⁰ m (or 0.364 nm)

1️⃣3️⃣ Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle 📏

Statement: It is impossible to simultaneously know both the exact position and exact momentum of a particle.

Mathematical Form:
Δx · Δp ≥ h/(4π)

Where:
• Δx = uncertainty in position
• Δp = uncertainty in momentum (mΔv)

Implication: The more precisely we know position, the less we know momentum, and vice versa.

🔢 Calculation 10: Heisenberg Uncertainty

Question: An electron has speed of 5 × 10⁵ m/s with uncertainty of 1%. Calculate the minimum uncertainty in its position.

Uncertainty in velocity: Δv = 0.01 × 5 × 10⁵ = 5 × 10³ m/s

Uncertainty in momentum: Δp = mΔv = (9.11 × 10⁻³¹)(5 × 10³) = 4.56 × 10⁻²⁷ kg·m/s

Using Δx · Δp ≥ h/(4π):
Δx ≥ (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴)/(4π × 4.56 × 10⁻²⁷)
≥ (6.63 × 10⁻³⁴)/(5.73 × 10⁻²⁶) = 1.16 × 10⁻⁸ m
Answer: 1.16 × 10⁻⁸ m (or 11.6 nm)
