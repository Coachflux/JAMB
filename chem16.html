1️⃣ What Are Organic Compounds?

Organic compounds are compounds of carbon (except a few like CO₂, CO, and carbonates).

Examples

  • Methane
  • Ethanol
  • Benzene
  • Glucose
  • Proteins

2️⃣ Why Carbon Forms Many Compounds

  • Tetravalency: carbon forms 4 covalent bonds (e.g., CH₄)
  • Catenation: carbon atoms form chains/rings (straight, branched, ring structures)
Carbon tetravalency: CH₄ has four covalent bonds

6️⃣ Empirical & Molecular Formula

Empirical formula = simplest whole-number ratio of atoms.

Example empirical formula: CH₂O

Molecular formula = (empirical formula) × n

(CH₂O) × 6 = C₆H₁₂O₆

7️⃣ Aliphatic Hydrocarbons (Open-Chain)

Open-chain hydrocarbons include alkanes, alkenes, alkynes.

Alkanes

  • Saturated hydrocarbons
  • General formula: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂
  • Examples: CH₄, C₂H₆, C₃H₈, C₄H₁₀

Properties & reactions

  • Low members: gases; medium: liquids; high: solids
  • Insoluble in water; low melting/boiling points
  • Substitution: CH₄ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl (in sunlight)
  • Isomerism (structural) up to 6 carbons

Petroleum & Petrochemicals

  • Fractional distillation: gas, petrol, kerosene, diesel, lubricating oil, bitumen
  • Cracking: heavy → light hydrocarbons
  • Reforming: straight → branched chains
  • Petrochemicals: plastics, fertilizers, detergents, synthetic fibres
  • Octane number: fuel quality indicator

Alkenes

  • Unsaturated (double bond), formula: CₙH₂ₙ
  • Addition reactions + polymerization
  • Test: decolourises bromine water

Alkynes

  • Triple bond, formula: CₙH₂ₙ₋₂
  • Ethyne preparation: CaC₂ + H₂O → C₂H₂ + Ca(OH)₂
  • Burns with luminous flame; used in oxy-acetylene welding

8️⃣ Aromatic Hydrocarbons (Benzene)

  • Benzene: C₆H₆ (stable, aromatic smell)
  • Undergoes substitution reactions
  • Uses: plastics, dyes, drugs, detergents
Benzene ring (aromatic): stable structure, mainly substitution reactions

9️⃣ Alkanols (Alcohols)

Alcohols contain the –OH functional group (example: ethanol).

Types

  • Primary, secondary, tertiary

Production

  • Fermentation
  • Hydration of ethene

Uses

  • Fuel, solvent, antiseptic, drinks
🧠 Lucas test

Lucas test distinguishes primary/secondary/tertiary alcohols (know the idea for theory questions).

🔟 Alkanals & Alkanones (Aldehydes vs Ketones)

  • Alkanals (aldehydes): –CHO (e.g., ethanal)
  • Alkanones (ketones): –CO– (e.g., propanone)

Tollens test (key difference)

Observation

  • Aldehyde → silver mirror
  • Ketone → no reaction

1️⃣1️⃣ Alkanoic Acids & Esters

  • Carboxylic acids contain –COOH (e.g., ethanoic acid)
  • Neutralization: acid + base → salt + water
  • Esterification: acid + alcohol → ester + water (e.g., ethyl ethanoate)
  • Natural esters → fats/oils; saponification → soap + glycerol
  • Margarine: hydrogenated vegetable oils

1️⃣2️⃣ Amines

Amines contain the –NH₂ group.

  • Primary: CH₃NH₂
  • Secondary: (CH₃)₂NH
  • Tertiary: (CH₃)₃N
  • Uses: drugs, dyes, plastics

1️⃣3️⃣ Carbohydrates

General formula: Cₙ(H₂O)ₙ

Types

  • Monosaccharides: glucose, fructose
  • Disaccharides: sucrose, maltose
  • Polysaccharides: starch, cellulose

Tests (very common)

Observations

  • Benedict’s → red precipitate
  • Fehling’s → brick red

Hydrolysis

  • Starch → glucose

1️⃣4️⃣ Proteins

  • Proteins are made from amino acids
  • Hydrolysis → amino acids
  • Enzymes are protein catalysts (amylase, pepsin)

1️⃣5️⃣ Polymers

Natural polymers

  • Rubber, starch, cellulose, proteins

Synthetic polymers

  • Nylon, polythene, PVC

Polymerization

  • Addition: ethene → polythene
  • Condensation: nylon formation

Plastics

  • Thermoplastics: remouldable (PVC)
  • Thermosetting: cannot remould (Bakelite)
🧠 Plastic memory

Thermoplastic = soften again • Thermoset = set permanently.
