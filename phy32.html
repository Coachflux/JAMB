📚 Chapter 32: Electrical Energy and Power

🎯 Learning Objectives: Apply expressions for electrical energy and power, understand commercial units (kWh), analyze electric power transmission, identify heating effects, understand domestic wiring, and determine fuse ratings.

1️⃣ Core Concepts: Electrical Energy & Power ⚡

Electrical Energy (E)

Energy is the capacity to do work. In electrical terms, it's the work done by electric current.

E = I²Rt = (V²/R)t = VIt = Pt

Where:
• E = Electrical energy (Joules, J)
• I = Current (Amperes, A)
• V = Potential difference (Volts, V)
• R = Resistance (Ohms, Ω)
• t = Time (seconds, s)

Electrical Power (P)

Power is the rate of energy consumption or work done per unit time.

P = E/t = I²R = V²/R = VI

Where:
• P = Power (Watts, W)
• 1 Watt = 1 Joule/second
🧠 Quick Memory Aid:
"Power is NOW, Energy is TOTAL"
• Power = how fast energy is used (instantaneous)
• Energy = total work done over time

🔢 Calculation 1: Calculating Power

Question: A 240V electric kettle draws a current of 5A. Calculate its power rating.

P = VI = 240V × 5A = 1200W = 1.2kW
Answer: 1.2 kW

🔢 Calculation 2: Calculating Energy in Joules

Question: A 60W bulb is switched on for 8 hours. Calculate the energy consumed in joules.

Convert time: t = 8 × 3600 = 28,800 seconds

E = Pt = 60W × 28,800s = 1,728,000J = 1.728 × 10⁶ J
Answer: 1.728 × 10⁶ J

🔢 Calculation 3: Finding Resistance from Power

Question: An electric heater is rated 2000W, 240V. Calculate its resistance.

P = V²/R → R = V²/P
R = 240²/2000 = 57,600/2000 = 28.8Ω
Answer: 28.8 Ω

🔢 Calculation 4: Using Alternative Power Formula

Question: A current of 4A flows through a 10Ω resistor. Calculate the power dissipated.

P = I²R = (4)² × 10 = 16 × 10 = 160W
Answer: 160 W

2️⃣ Commercial Units 💰

Unit Definition Conversion
Kilowatt-hour (kWh) Energy consumed by 1 kW appliance in 1 hour 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
Watt-hour (Wh) Smaller unit for small devices 1 Wh = 3600 J
Horsepower (hp) Mechanical power unit 1 hp = 746 W
  • Why kWh? Electricity companies bill in kWh (not Joules) because Joules are too small for practical billing, and kWh is convenient for household consumption

🔢 Calculation 5: Basic kWh Calculation

Question: A 100W bulb is used for 10 hours. Calculate energy consumed in kWh.

E = (100W × 10h)/1000 = 1000Wh/1000 = 1kWh
Answer: 1 kWh

🔢 Calculation 6: Electricity Bill Calculation

Question: A household uses daily: 5 bulbs (60W each) for 6h, TV (150W) for 4h, Refrigerator (200W) for 24h, Electric iron (1000W) for 30 min. Calculate total kWh in 30 days and bill at ₦50 per kWh.

Daily consumption:
• Bulbs: 5 × 60W × 6h = 1800Wh = 1.8kWh
• TV: 150W × 4h = 600Wh = 0.6kWh
• Refrigerator: 200W × 24h = 4800Wh = 4.8kWh
• Iron: 1000W × 0.5h = 500Wh = 0.5kWh

Total daily = 1.8 + 0.6 + 4.8 + 0.5 = 7.7kWh

Monthly = 7.7 × 30 = 231kWh
Bill = 231 × ₦50 = ₦11,550
Answer: 231 kWh, Bill = ₦11,550

🔢 Calculation 7: Converting kWh to Joules

Question: Calculate the energy in joules for 5 kWh.

E = 5 × 3.6 × 10⁶ = 18 × 10⁶ J = 1.8 × 10⁷ J
Answer: 1.8 × 10⁷ J

🔢 Calculation 8: Finding Operating Hours

Question: How long will a 2kW heater take to consume 10 kWh?

t = E/P = 10kWh/2kW = 5 hours
Answer: 5 hours

3️⃣ Electric Power Transmission 🏭

The Journey: Power Station → Consumer

Power Station → Step-Up Transformer → Transmission Lines →
Step-Down Transformer → Distribution → Consumer

Why High Voltage Transmission?

To minimize power loss in transmission lines!

Power Loss in Cables: Ploss = I²Rcable

Strategy:
1. Step-up transformer increases voltage to 132kV - 330kV
2. Current decreases (since P = VI, if V↑ then I↓)
3. Since Ploss = I²R, lower current = much lower heat loss
4. Step-down transformer reduces voltage for safe home use (240V)
  • Key Points: Transmission at high voltage, low current; Transformers work on AC only; DC cannot be easily transformed

🔢 Calculation 9: Calculating Transmission Current

Question: A power station generates 100MW at 25kV. Calculate the current.

P = VI → I = P/V = (100 × 10⁶)/(25 × 10³) = 4000A
Answer: 4000 A

🔢 Calculation 10: Power Loss in Transmission

Question: This 100MW power is stepped up to 330kV. The transmission line has resistance of 5Ω. Calculate: (a) The new current, (b) Power loss, (c) Efficiency.

(a) New current: I = (100 × 10⁶)/(330 × 10³) = 303.03A

(b) Power loss: Ploss = I²R = (303.03)² × 5 = 459,136W ≈ 459kW

(c) Efficiency: η = [(100,000 - 459)/100,000] × 100 = 99.54%
Answer: (a) 303.03 A, (b) 459 kW, (c) 99.54%

🔢 Calculation 11: Comparing Transmission Voltages

Question: Compare power loss when transmitting 50MW through a cable with 2Ω resistance at: (a) 50kV, (b) 200kV.

(a) At 50kV: I = (50 × 10⁶)/(50 × 10³) = 1000A
Ploss = (1000)² × 2 = 2,000,000W = 2MW (4% loss)

(b) At 200kV: I = (50 × 10⁶)/(200 × 10³) = 250A
Ploss = (250)² × 2 = 125,000W = 125kW (0.25% loss)

Conclusion: Higher voltage reduces power loss significantly!
Answer: (a) 2 MW loss (4%), (b) 125 kW loss (0.25%)

4️⃣ Heating Effects of Electric Current (Joule's Law) 🔥

Joule's Law of Heating

When current flows through a conductor, heat is produced:

H = I²Rt

Principle Behind Appliances

Appliance Heating Element Application
Electric iron Nichrome wire Pressing clothes
Electric kettle Heating coil Boiling water
Electric heater Resistance wire Room heating
Electric bulb Tungsten filament Light (and heat)
Fuse wire Thin lead/tin wire Circuit protection
  • Why Nichrome? High resistivity (produces more heat), high melting point (doesn't burn out), oxidizes slowly (long-lasting)

🔢 Calculation 12: Basic Joule's Law

Question: Calculate the heat produced when 3A flows through a 20Ω resistor for 5 minutes.

Convert time: t = 5 × 60 = 300s

H = I²Rt = (3)² × 20 × 300 = 9 × 20 × 300 = 54,000J
Answer: 54,000 J

🔢 Calculation 13: Calculating Water Heating Time

Question: An electric kettle (2000W, 240V) heats 2kg of water from 20°C to 100°C. Calculate time required. (Specific heat capacity of water = 4200 J/kg·K)

Heat required: Q = mcΔT = 2 × 4200 × (100-20) = 672,000J

Assuming all electrical energy converts to heat: E = Q = 672,000J

Time: t = E/P = 672,000/2000 = 336s = 5.6 minutes
Answer: 5.6 minutes

🔢 Calculation 14: Finding Resistance of Heating Element

Question: An electric iron draws 4A from 240V mains. Calculate: (a) Its resistance, (b) Heat produced in 10 minutes.

(a) R = V/I = 240/4 = 60Ω

(b) t = 10 × 60 = 600s
H = I²Rt = (4)² × 60 × 600 = 16 × 60 × 600 = 576,000J
Answer: (a) 60 Ω, (b) 576,000 J

5️⃣ Electrical Wiring of Houses 🏠

Domestic Circuit Layout

Main Supply (240V) → Fuse/Consumer Unit →
    ├── Lighting Circuit (5A)
    ├── Power Circuit (15A/30A)
    └── Heavy Appliances (Cooker, AC)

Key Features

Feature Purpose
Parallel connection Each device gets full voltage; independent operation
Live wire (Brown/Red) Carries current to appliance (240V potential)
Neutral wire (Blue/Black) Completes circuit (0V, grounded)
Earth wire (Green/Yellow) Safety - directs leakage current to ground
Switches Connected to live wire (not neutral)
Fuses/Circuit breakers Overcurrent protection

Why Parallel > Series for Homes?

Parallel Series
Same voltage across all devices Voltage divides
Devices work independently One fails = all fail
Can add more devices easily Adding devices reduces brightness
Safer and more practical Impractical for homes

🔢 Calculation 15: Parallel Circuit Analysis

Question: Three bulbs (60W, 100W, and 40W) are connected in parallel to 240V. Calculate: (a) Current through each bulb, (b) Total current, (c) Total resistance.

(a) Using P = VI → I = P/V:
• I₁ = 60/240 = 0.25A
• I₂ = 100/240 = 0.417A
• I₃ = 40/240 = 0.167A

(b) Total current: Itotal = 0.25 + 0.417 + 0.167 = 0.834A

(c) Total resistance: Rtotal = V/Itotal = 240/0.834 = 287.8Ω
Answer: (a) 0.25A, 0.417A, 0.167A (b) 0.834A (c) 287.8 Ω

🔢 Calculation 16: Circuit Load Calculation

Question: A 240V lighting circuit has a 5A fuse. Calculate the maximum power it can safely handle.

Pmax = VI = 240 × 5 = 1200W

Therefore, you can safely connect up to 1200W of lighting (e.g., twenty 60W bulbs).
Answer: 1200 W

6️⃣ Use of Fuses 🔒

What is a Fuse?

A safety device containing a thin metal wire that melts when current exceeds a safe limit, breaking the circuit.

How to Choose Fuse Rating

Fuse Rating = Total Power/Voltage

Or slightly higher than operating current.

Types of Fuses

Type Rating Use
Cartridge fuse 3A, 5A, 13A Plugs, small appliances
Circuit breaker (MCB) 5A, 15A, 30A, 45A Modern consumer units
Rewirable fuse Various Older installations
Important:
• Always connect fuse to the live wire
• Never use a higher-rated fuse than recommended (fire risk!)
• Never bypass a fuse with wire/coin

🔢 Calculation 17: Basic Fuse Rating

Question: An electric kettle is rated 2000W, 240V. Determine the appropriate fuse rating.

I = P/V = 2000/240 = 8.33A

Standard fuse ratings: 3A, 5A, 10A, 13A

Select 10A or 13A fuse (next standard rating above 8.33A)
Answer: 10A or 13A fuse

🔢 Calculation 18: Multiple Appliances on One Circuit

Question: A 240V power circuit has four appliances: TV (200W), Fridge (150W), Fan (80W), Computer (300W). Calculate the minimum fuse rating.

Total power = 200 + 150 + 80 + 300 = 730W

Current: I = 730/240 = 3.04A

Use 5A fuse (next standard rating)
Answer: 5A fuse

🔢 Calculation 19: Checking Circuit Safety

Question: A 240V, 15A circuit already has a water heater (3000W). Can a 2000W electric iron be added safely?

Existing current: I = 3000/240 = 12.5A

Additional current: Iiron = 2000/240 = 8.33A

Total current = 12.5 + 8.33 = 20.83A

This exceeds 15A! The iron cannot be safely added without upgrading the fuse or creating a separate circuit.
Answer: No, cannot be added safely (20.83A > 15A)

🔢 Calculation 20: Fuse Wire Properties

Question: Explain why fuse wire should have: (a) Low melting point, (b) High resistance.

(a) Low melting point: So it melts quickly when current exceeds safe limits, breaking the circuit before damage occurs.

(b) High resistance: To ensure significant heat is generated (H = I²Rt) when overcurrent flows, causing the wire to melt.
Answer: See explanation above

🎯 JAMB Exam Tips

  1. Remember conversions: 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J (common calculation!)

  2. Power transmission: Always mention Ploss = I²R and why high voltage is used

  3. Parallel circuits: Emphasize independent operation and same voltage

  4. Fuse calculations: Show working: I = P/V, then round up to standard rating

  5. Heating effects: Know practical applications (iron, kettle, bulb, fuse)

  6. Unit consistency: Always convert time to seconds for energy in joules, keep hours for kWh

  7. Standard fuse ratings: 3A, 5A, 10A, 13A, 15A, 30A (always choose the next higher standard rating)
