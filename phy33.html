📚 Chapter 33: Magnets and Magnetic Fields

🎯 Learning Objectives: Identify natural and artificial magnets, differentiate magnetic properties of soft iron and steel, understand methods of making and demagnetizing magnets, determine flux patterns for magnets and current-carrying conductors, identify Earth's magnetic elements, and examine applications of Earth's magnetic field.

1️⃣ Natural and Artificial Magnets 🧲

Type Description Examples
Natural Magnets Found in nature; magnetized by Earth's magnetic field over time Magnetite (lodestone) - iron oxide mineral
Artificial Magnets Man-made through industrial processes Bar magnets, horseshoe magnets, electromagnets
  • Key Points: Natural magnets are weak compared to artificial ones; All magnets have two poles: North (N) and South (S); Like poles repel, unlike poles attract; Magnetic poles always exist in pairs - no magnetic monopoles exist

2️⃣ Magnetic Properties of Soft Iron and Steel ⚙️

Property Soft Iron Steel
Magnetization Easy to magnetize Harder to magnetize
Demagnetization Easy to demagnetize Hard to demagnetize
Retentivity Low (loses magnetism quickly) High (retains magnetism)
Coercivity Low High
Use Temporary magnets, electromagnets Permanent magnets
Applications Transformers, relays, electric bells Compasses, loudspeakers, generators
Memory Tip: "Soft iron is soft on keeping magnetism - it lets it go easily. Steel is steely determined to stay magnetized!"

3️⃣ Methods of Making Magnets and Demagnetization 🔧

Methods of Making Magnets

  1. Stroking Method (Single Touch): Stroke steel/iron with one pole of a strong magnet repeatedly in one direction. The material becomes magnetized with polarity opposite to the stroking pole at the starting end.
  2. Divided Touch Method: Use two magnets with like poles stroking from the center outward.
  3. Electrical Method (Most Important for JAMB): Place material inside a solenoid (coil of wire), pass direct current (DC) through the coil. Strength depends on: current magnitude, number of turns, and duration.
  4. Induction: Place unmagnetized iron near a strong magnet without touching. Temporary magnetism is induced.

Methods of Demagnetization

Method How It Works
Heating Heat magnet to red hot, then cool while lying East-West
Hammering Hammer magnet vigorously while lying East-West
AC Solenoid Method Place in solenoid with alternating current, then slowly withdraw
Rough Handling Dropping or rough treatment disrupts domain alignment
  • Important: To preserve a magnet, store it with keepers (soft iron bars across poles) in a dry place, lying in North-South direction.

4️⃣ Concept of Magnetic Field 🌐

A magnetic field is a region of space around a magnet or current-carrying conductor where magnetic forces can be detected.

  • It is a vector quantity (has magnitude and direction)
  • Represented by magnetic field lines (lines of force)
  • Direction: North to South externally, South to North internally (closed loops)

Properties of Magnetic Field Lines

  1. Never cross each other
  2. Crowded near poles (stronger field)
  3. Form continuous closed loops
  4. Tangent to line gives direction of field

5️⃣ Magnetic Field of a Permanent Magnet 🧭

Bar Magnet

  • Lines emerge from North pole, enter South pole
  • Strongest at the poles
  • Neutral point exists where Earth's field cancels magnet's field

Two Magnets - Different Configurations

Arrangement Pattern Description
N-N or S-S (Like poles facing) Repulsion - lines bend away from each other; neutral point between them
N-S (Unlike poles facing) Attraction - lines go from N to S directly; no neutral point between
Practical Tip: Use iron filings or plotting compass to visualize field patterns experimentally.

6️⃣ Magnetic Field Around Current-Carrying Conductors ⚡

A. Straight Current-Carrying Conductor

  • Pattern: Concentric circles around the wire
  • Direction: Determined by Right-Hand Grip Rule
    • Thumb points in current direction
    • Fingers curl in field direction

B. Circular Coil (Loop)

  • Pattern: Similar to short bar magnet
  • One face acts as North pole, opposite face as South pole
  • Field is stronger at the center

C. Solenoid (Long Coil)

  • Pattern: Similar to bar magnet
  • Polarity Rule: Current clockwise at one end = South pole; Current anticlockwise = North pole
  • Right-Hand Grip Rule for Solenoids: Fingers follow current, thumb points to North pole

Field Strength Factors

  • Number of turns per unit length
  • Current magnitude
  • Presence of iron core (increases field dramatically)

7️⃣ Earth's Magnetic Field 🌍

The Earth behaves like a giant magnet with:

  • Magnetic North Pole near geographic South Pole
  • Magnetic South Pole near geographic North Pole

Key Elements

Element Definition Symbol
Magnetic Meridian Vertical plane containing Earth's magnetic axis -
Geographic Meridian Vertical plane containing geographic axis -
Angle of Declination Angle between magnetic meridian and geographic meridian α
Angle of Dip (Inclination) Angle Earth's field makes with horizontal δ
Horizontal Component Component of Earth's field parallel to surface Bₕ = B cos δ
Vertical Component Component perpendicular to surface Bᵥ = B sin δ
Relationship: B² = Bₕ² + Bᵥ² and tan δ = Bᵥ/Bₕ

8️⃣ Flux and Flux Density 📊

Quantity Symbol Definition Unit Formula
Magnetic Flux Φ Total magnetic field passing through an area Weber (Wb) Φ = BA (when B⊥A)
Magnetic Flux Density B Force per unit length on conductor carrying 1A current Tesla (T) B = Φ/A or B = F/IL
Important: 1 T = 1 Wb/m² = 1 N/(A·m)
Flux density is what we commonly call "magnetic field strength" or "magnetic induction."

🔢 Calculation 1: Magnetic Flux Calculation

Question: A rectangular coil of dimensions 20 cm × 15 cm is placed perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field of flux density 0.4 T. Calculate the magnetic flux through the coil.

Convert dimensions: 20 cm = 0.20 m, 15 cm = 0.15 m
Area A = 0.20 × 0.15 = 0.03 m²

Using Φ = BA (since perpendicular, θ = 0°)
Φ = 0.4 T × 0.03 m² = 0.012 Wb = 12 mWb
Answer: 0.012 Wb (or 12 mWb)

🔢 Calculation 2: Force on Current-Carrying Conductor

Question: A wire of length 50 cm carries a current of 4.0 A. It is placed perpendicular to a magnetic field of flux density 0.60 T. Calculate the force on the wire.

Convert length: L = 50 cm = 0.50 m
Since perpendicular, θ = 90°, sin 90° = 1

Using F = BIL sin θ
F = 0.60 T × 4.0 A × 0.50 m × 1 = 1.2 N
Answer: 1.2 N

🔢 Calculation 3: Force at an Angle

Question: A conductor of length 1.4 m carrying a current of 0.87 A is placed at 30° to a magnetic field of flux density 80 mT. Calculate the force on the conductor.

Convert units: B = 80 mT = 80 × 10⁻³ T = 0.080 T

Using F = BIL sin θ
F = 0.080 × 0.87 × 1.4 × sin 30°
F = 0.080 × 0.87 × 1.4 × 0.5
F = 0.04872 N ≈ 0.049 N or 4.9 × 10⁻² N
Answer: 0.049 N (or 4.9 × 10⁻² N)

🔢 Calculation 4: Earth's Magnetic Field Components

Question: At a certain location, the Earth's total magnetic flux density is 5.0 × 10⁻⁵ T and the angle of dip is 60°. Calculate: (a) The horizontal component, (b) The vertical component.

(a) Horizontal Component:
Bₕ = B cos δ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁵ × cos 60°
Bₕ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁵ × 0.5 = 2.5 × 10⁻⁵ T

(b) Vertical Component:
Bᵥ = B sin δ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁵ × sin 60°
Bᵥ = 5.0 × 10⁻⁵ × 0.866 = 4.33 × 10⁻⁵ T

Check: B² = Bₕ² + Bᵥ²
(5.0 × 10⁻⁵)² = (2.5 × 10⁻⁵)² + (4.33 × 10⁻⁵)²
25 × 10⁻¹⁰ = 6.25 × 10⁻¹⁰ + 18.75 × 10⁻¹⁰ ✓
Answer: (a) 2.5 × 10⁻⁵ T, (b) 4.33 × 10⁻⁵ T

🔢 Calculation 5: Flux Linkage in a Solenoid

Question: A solenoid has 300 turns, circular cross-section of radius 0.40 m, and is placed perpendicular to a magnetic field of flux density 5.1 mT. Calculate the magnetic flux linkage.

Convert B: 5.1 mT = 5.1 × 10⁻³ T
Area A = πr² = π × (0.40)² = 0.503 m²

Flux per turn Φ = BA = 5.1 × 10⁻³ × 0.503 = 2.565 × 10⁻³ Wb

Flux linkage = NΦ = 300 × 2.565 × 10⁻³ = 0.77 Wb turns
(or 0.8 Wb turns to 2 s.f.)
Answer: 0.77 Wb turns (or 0.8 Wb turns)

🔢 Calculation 6: Finding Flux Density from Force

Question: A 25 cm wire carrying a current of 8.0 A experiences a force of 0.40 N when placed perpendicular to a uniform magnetic field. Calculate the flux density.

Convert length: L = 25 cm = 0.25 m

Rearrange F = BIL to get B = F/(IL)
B = 0.40 / (8.0 × 0.25) = 0.40 / 2.0 = 0.20 T
Answer: 0.20 T

📋 Complete Formula Summary Table

Concept Formula Units When to Use
Magnetic Flux Φ = BA cos θ Wb, T, m² Calculating total magnetic field through a surface
Magnetic Flux (perpendicular) Φ = BA Wb, T, m² When field is perpendicular to surface (θ = 0°)
Flux Density B = Φ/A T, Wb, m² Finding field strength from flux
Flux Density (from force) B = F/(IL sin θ) T, N, A, m When force on conductor is known
Force on Conductor F = BIL sin θ N, T, A, m Calculating force on current-carrying wire
Maximum Force F = BIL N, T, A, m When conductor is perpendicular to field (θ = 90°)
Horizontal Component (Earth) Bₕ = B cos δ T, T, degrees Resolving Earth's field horizontally
Vertical Component (Earth) Bᵥ = B sin δ T, T, degrees Resolving Earth's field vertically
Resultant Field B = √(Bₕ² + Bᵥ²) T, T, T Finding total field from components
Tangent of Dip tan δ = Bᵥ/Bₕ degrees, T, T Relating dip angle to field components
Flux Linkage NΦ = BAN Wb-turns, T, m² For coils with multiple turns

9️⃣ Variation of Magnetic Field Intensity 📈

Earth's magnetic field varies:

  • With Location: Stronger near poles, weaker near equator
  • With Time: Slow secular changes over years
  • With Altitude: Decreases with height above surface
  • Local Anomalies: Deposits of magnetic minerals cause local variations

At Magnetic Poles

  • Dip angle = 90° (field is vertical)
  • Horizontal component = 0

At Magnetic Equator

  • Dip angle = 0° (field is horizontal)
  • Vertical component = 0

🔟 Applications 🧭

Application How Earth's Field is Used
Navigation Compass needles align with magnetic meridian; mariners use magnetic compasses corrected for declination
Mineral Exploration Magnetic surveys detect iron ore deposits and other magnetic minerals by measuring local field variations
Geological Surveying Mapping subsurface geology through magnetic anomalies
Bird/Animal Migration Some species use Earth's field for orientation

🎯 JAMB Exam Tips

  1. Diagrams are crucial - Practice drawing field patterns for all configurations

  2. Remember the rules - Right-hand grip rule, polarity rules

  3. Distinguish carefully - Between geographic and magnetic poles (they're opposite!)

  4. Units matter - Know Weber, Tesla, and their relationships; convert cm to m, mT to T

  5. Trigonometry - Remember sin 90° = 1 (maximum force), sin 0° = 0 (no force)

  6. Applications - Always link theory to practical uses in navigation and exploration

  7. Formula selection - Identify what is given and what is required before choosing a formula
