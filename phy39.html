🎯 Part 1: Classification of Materials

1️⃣ Metals, Semiconductors & Insulators ⚡

The Band Theory Concept

Imagine electrons in an atom live in "energy bands." The two important bands are:

  • Valence Band (VB): Where electrons are normally stuck to atoms
  • Conduction Band (CB): Where electrons are free to move and conduct electricity

The gap between them is called the Band Gap (Forbidden Energy Gap).

Material Band Gap Size Conductivity Example
Conductors (Metals) No gap (overlap) Very High Copper, Aluminum, Silver
Semiconductors Small gap (~1 eV) Moderate Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge)
Insulators Large gap (>5 eV) Negligible Rubber, Glass, Wood
Key Points for JAMB:
• Metals: Valence and conduction bands overlap → electrons move freely
• Semiconductors: Small gap → some electrons can jump to conduction band when heated or doped
• Insulators: Large gap → electrons cannot jump → no conduction
🧪 Part 2: Semiconductors

2️⃣ Intrinsic Semiconductors 🔬

📋 Definition & Properties

  • Definition: Pure semiconductor materials (Si or Ge) with NO impurities
  • Properties:
    • Equal number of electrons and holes (charge carriers)
    • Conductivity increases with temperature
    • At 0K (absolute zero), behaves like an insulator
💡 Memory Aid: Holes = When an electron leaves the valence band, it creates a vacancy called a "hole." Holes act as positive charge carriers.

3️⃣ Extrinsic Semiconductors (Doped) ⚙️

To make semiconductors useful, we add impurities through doping:

Type Dopant Added Majority Carrier Minority Carrier Charge
n-type Pentavalent (5 valence e⁻) e.g., Phosphorus, Arsenic Electrons Holes Neutral overall
p-type Trivalent (3 valence e⁻) e.g., Boron, Aluminum Holes Electrons Neutral overall
🧠 Memory Trick:
• n-type = negative carriers (electrons) are majority
• p-type = positive carriers (holes) are majority
Important: Both n-type and p-type remain electrically neutral overall because the dopant atoms are neutral—they just provide extra electrons or holes.
⚡ Part 3: The p-n Junction Diode

4️⃣ Formation & Biasing 🔌

Formation of p-n Junction

When you join p-type and n-type semiconductors:

  1. Electrons from n-side diffuse to p-side
  2. Holes from p-side diffuse to n-side
  3. This creates a depletion region (no free carriers) at the junction
  4. A barrier potential develops (0.7V for Si, 0.3V for Ge)

Biasing (How to Use It)

Bias Type Connection Behavior Current Flow
Forward Bias +ve to p-side, -ve to n-side Depletion region narrows Conducts (High current)
Reverse Bias +ve to n-side, -ve to p-side Depletion region widens Blocks (Minimal current)
Key JAMB Point: Diode acts as a one-way valve for current—allows flow in forward bias, blocks in reverse bias.
🔄 Part 4: Rectification

5️⃣ AC to DC Conversion ⚡

Since diodes conduct only in one direction, they're perfect for converting AC to DC.

Half-Wave Rectification

  • Uses one diode
  • Allows only one half of AC cycle to pass
  • Output: Pulsating DC (half the input wave is missing)
  • Efficiency: Low (40.6%)

Full-Wave Rectification

Two common methods:

1. Center-Tap Transformer (Two Diodes)

  • Uses two diodes and a center-tapped transformer
  • Both halves of AC cycle are used
  • Output: Pulsating DC with double the frequency of input

2. Bridge Rectifier (Four Diodes)

  • Uses four diodes in a bridge configuration
  • No center-tapped transformer needed
  • More efficient use of transformer
  • Output: Full-wave rectified DC
Feature Half-Wave Full-Wave (Center-Tap) Full-Wave (Bridge)
Diodes needed 1 2 4
Transformer Simple Center-tapped required Simple
Output frequency Same as input 2 × input 2 × input
Efficiency Lower Higher Higher
Peak inverse voltage 2Vm 2Vm Vm
Smoothing: To get steady DC from pulsating DC, use a capacitor in parallel with the load—it charges during peaks and discharges during troughs.
🔊 Part 5: Transistors

6️⃣ Transistors - Amplification 📢

What is a Transistor?

A transistor is a semiconductor device with three layers used for:

  • Amplification (making weak signals stronger)
  • Switching (controlling current flow)

Types

  • npn transistor: p-type sandwiched between two n-types
  • pnp transistor: n-type sandwiched between two p-types

Structure and Terminals

Every transistor has three terminals:

  • Emitter: Heavily doped, emits charge carriers
  • Base: Thin, lightly doped, controls carrier flow
  • Collector: Moderately doped, collects carriers

Basic Operation (Common Emitter)

Key Relationships:
• Small current into the Base controls large current from Collector to Emitter
• Current gain (β) = Ic/Ib (typically 20-200)
• Key Point: Small base current changes → Large collector current changes = Amplification

🔊 Transistor as an Amplifier

  • Weak AC signal is fed into the base circuit
  • Small variations in base current cause large variations in collector current
  • The output is an amplified version of the input signal
