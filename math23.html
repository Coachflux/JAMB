🎲 Chapter 23: Probability

This chapter covers likelihood of events, types of events, and calculation methods - essential for JAMB 2026. Probability ranges from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

1️⃣ BASIC CONCEPTS

Definition & Key Terms 📝

Basic Probability Formula:
P(E) = Number of favourable outcomesTotal number of possible outcomes

Key Terms Table

Term Definition Example
Experiment Action with uncertain outcomes Tossing a coin
Outcome Single result of experiment Getting "Head"
Sample Space (S) Set of all possible outcomes S = {H, T}
Event (E) Subset of sample space E = {H}
2️⃣ TYPES OF EVENTS

A. Mutually Exclusive Events 🚫

Definition: Events that cannot occur together
Formula: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)

🔢 Example 1: Mutually Exclusive

Rolling a die, getting 2 OR 5.

P(2 or 5) = 16 + 16 = 26 = 13
Answer: 1/3

B. Independent Events 🔗

Definition: Events where one does not affect the other
Formula: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)

🔢 Example 2: Independent Events

Tossing a coin twice. Probability of two heads:

P(HH) = 12 × 12 = 14
Answer: 1/4

C. Complementary Events 🔄

Definition: The event NOT occurring
Formula: P(A') = 1 − P(A)

🔢 Example 3: Complementary

Probability of NOT getting 6 on a die:

P(not 6) = 1 − 16 = 56
Answer: 5/6
💡 "At least one" = 1 − P(none) — most useful shortcut in probability!
3️⃣ CALCULATION METHODS

A. Simple Probability 🎯

🔢 Example 4: Ball Selection

A bag contains 5 red, 3 blue, and 2 green balls (10 total). Find probability of:

(a) Red ball: P(R) = 510 = 12

(b) Blue ball: P(B) = 310

(c) Not green: P(G') = 1 − 210 = 810 = 45

B. Addition Rule (OR) ➕

For non-mutually exclusive events:
P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)

🔢 Example 5: Addition Rule

In a class of 40 students: 20 study Maths (M), 15 study Physics (P), 8 study both. Find P(Maths OR Physics).

P(M ∪ P) = 2040 + 1540 − 840
= 2740
Answer: 27/40

C. Conditional Probability 🎲

Definition: Probability of A given that B has occurred
Formula: P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B)P(B)

🔢 Example 6: Conditional Probability

Using Example 5 data, find P(Physics | Maths).

P(P|M) = P(P ∩ M)P(M) = 8/4020/40 = 820 = 25
Answer: 2/5
4️⃣ PERMUTATIONS & COMBINATIONS IN PROBABILITY

Factorial Notation ❗

n! = n × (n−1) × (n−2) × ... × 1
Example: 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120

Permutations (Order Matters) 📏

nPr = n!(n−r)!

🔢 Example 7: Permutation

Arrange 3 people from 5 in a row:

5P3 = 5!(5−3)! = 5!2! = 1202 = 60 ways
Answer: 60 ways

Combinations (Order Doesn't Matter) 🎯

nCr = n!r!(n−r)!

🔢 Example 8: Combination

Choose 3 students from 10 for a committee:

10C3 = 10!3! × 7! = 10 × 9 × 83 × 2 × 1 = 7206 = 120 ways
Answer: 120 ways
5️⃣ ADVANCED EXAMPLES

🔢 Example 9: With Replacement

A bag has 4 white and 6 black balls. Two balls drawn with replacement. Find P(both white).

P(WW) = 410 × 410 = 16100 = 425
Answer: 4/25

🔢 Example 10: Without Replacement

Same bag, but without replacement:

P(WW) = 410 × 39 = 1290 = 215
Answer: 2/15

🔢 Example 11: At Least One

Find probability of getting at least one 6 when rolling two dice.

Method: 1 − P(no sixes)

= 1 − (56 × 56)
= 1 − 2536
= 1136
Answer: 11/36
📋 COMPLETE FORMULA SUMMARY

All Probability Formulas 📚

Concept Formula When to Use
Basic Probability P(E) = n(E)n(S) Simple events
Complementary P(E') = 1 − P(E) "Not" happening
Mutually Exclusive P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) A OR B (can't both)
Addition Rule P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B) A OR B (can both)
Independent P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) A AND B (independent)
Conditional P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B)P(B) A given B occurred
Permutations nPr = n!(n−r)! Arrangements (order)
Combinations nCr = n!r!(n−r)! Selections (no order)
Expected Value E(X) = ∑x · P(x) Average outcome

⚡ Quick Decision Guide

Mutually exclusive? P(A) + P(B)

Independent? P(A) × P(B)

With replacement? Probabilities stay same

Without replacement? Denominator decreases

At least one? 1 − P(none)

✏️ PRACTICE QUESTION:

Three coins are tossed simultaneously. Find probability of:

(a) Exactly two heads

(b) At least one tail

(c) No heads

Answers: (a) 3/8 (b) 7/8 (c) 1/8

Sample space: {HHH, HHT, HTH, THH, HTT, THT, TTH, TTT} → 8 outcomes

🎯 JAMB EXAM TIPS

1. Read carefully: Note "with" or "without" replacement

2. Draw tree diagrams: For complex problems

3. "At least one": Use 1 − P(none) — fastest method

4. Check independence: Before multiplying probabilities

5. Use combinations: For "choosing" probability questions

6. Sample space: Always identify all possible outcomes first

⚠️ Common Mistakes:

• Confusing "with" and "without" replacement

• Forgetting to subtract intersection in addition rule

• Assuming events are independent when they're not

• Not simplifying fractions to lowest terms

• Missing overlapping outcomes in OR problems
