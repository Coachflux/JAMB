Gravitational Field: Step-by-Step Explanation

The explanations are structured around the subtopics listed by JAMB Standard, with simple language, key formulas, and examples. We'll cover definitions, derivations where needed, and real-world applications. At the end, I'll provide 10 JAMB-style past questions with step-by-step solutions.

(i) Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation

Newton's law states that every particle in the universe attracts every other particle with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between their centers.

Key Expression: The gravitational force F between two bodies of masses m₁ and m₂ separated by distance r is:
F = G (m₁ m₂) / r²
where G is the universal gravitational constant (G = 6.67 × 10⁻¹¹ N m² kg⁻²).

Step-by-Step Understanding:

  • The force is attractive and acts along the line joining the centers of the two masses.
  • It's a mutual force: the Earth pulls on an apple, and the apple pulls on the Earth with the same force.
  • If distance doubles, force becomes 1/4 (inverse square law). If masses double, force quadruples.

Application: Explains planetary motion, tides, and why objects fall to Earth.

Dimensional Analysis of G: G has dimensions M⁻¹ L³ T⁻² (derived from F = ma, so G = F r² / (m₁ m₂)).

(ii) Gravitational Potential

Gravitational potential at a point is the work done per unit mass in bringing a small test mass from infinity to that point.

Key Expression: For a point mass M at distance r:
V = - G M / r
(Negative sign because work is done by the field, and potential at infinity is zero).

Step-by-Step Understanding:

  • Potential is a scalar (no direction).
  • Gravitational potential energy for mass m is U = m V = - G M m / r.
  • Near Earth's surface, it approximates to m g h (where h is height, but this is for small distances).

Derivation: Work done W = ∫ from ∞ to r F dr = ∫ from ∞ to r (G M m / r²) dr = - G M m / r. So, V = W / m.

Application: Used in calculating energy for satellites or escape from planets.

(iii) Conservative and Non-Conservative Fields

A field is a region where a force acts on an object without contact.

Conservative Field: Work done by the force is independent of the path taken (depends only on start and end points). Energy is conserved.

Example: Gravitational field (e.g., lifting a book up stairs or ramp uses the same energy against gravity).

Test: If work in a closed loop is zero, it's conservative.

Non-Conservative Field: Work done depends on the path.

Examples: Frictional force (sliding a box along rough path uses more energy than smooth one), air resistance.

Step-by-Step Understanding:

  • Gravitational fields are conservative because gravity is a central force.
  • In conservative fields, potential energy can be defined (like gravitational potential).
  • Non-conservative fields dissipate energy (e.g., as heat in friction).

(iv) Acceleration Due to Gravity

Acceleration due to gravity (g) is the gravitational field strength—the force per unit mass.

Key Expression: Near Earth's surface, g = G M / R² ≈ 9.8 m/s² (or 10 m/s² for calculations).

From Newton's law: F = m g, so g = F / m = G M / r².

Step-by-Step Understanding:

  • It's the acceleration of free-falling objects (ignoring air resistance).
  • All objects fall with the same g regardless of mass (Galileo's experiment).

Derivation: For Earth (mass M, radius R), weight m g = G (M m) / R², so g = G M / R².

Application: Motion under gravity (e.g., equations: v = u + g t, s = u t + (1/2) g t²).

(v) Variation of g on the Earth’s Surface

g is not constant everywhere on Earth.

Causes of Variation:

  • Earth's Shape: Earth is oblate (flattened at poles), so radius at equator > poles. g higher at poles (g_p ≈ 9.83 m/s²) than equator (g_e ≈ 9.78 m/s²).
  • Rotation (Centrifugal Effect): At equator, centrifugal force opposes gravity, reducing effective g.
  • Altitude/Depth: g decreases with height (e.g., on mountains) or inside Earth.
  • Local Density Variations: Denser rocks increase g.

Step-by-Step Understanding:

  • Formula: Effective g' = g - ω² R cos² φ (where ω is angular velocity, φ latitude).
  • At poles (φ = 90°), no centrifugal; at equator (φ = 0°), maximum reduction.

Application: Weighing scales show slight differences at different locations.

(vi) Distinction Between Mass and Weight; Escape Velocity

Mass vs. Weight:

  • Mass (m): Amount of matter; scalar; constant; measured in kg; inertial property (resists motion).
  • Weight (w): Force due to gravity (w = m g); vector (downward); varies with location; measured in N.

Key Difference: Mass is invariant; weight changes (e.g., zero in free fall, less on Moon).

Escape Velocity: Minimum speed to escape a planet's gravitational pull without further propulsion.

Expression: v_e = √(2 G M / R) = √(2 g R).

For Earth (R = 6.4 × 10⁶ m, g = 10 m/s²): v_e ≈ 11.2 km/s.

Step-by-Step Understanding:

  • Derivation: Kinetic energy = potential energy difference: (1/2) m v_e² = G M m / R → v_e = √(2 G M / R).

Application: Rockets need this speed to leave Earth; black holes have v_e = c (speed of light).

(vii) Parking Orbit and Weightlessness

Parking Orbit: A stable orbit where a satellite appears stationary relative to Earth (geostationary orbit).

Conditions: Equatorial plane; period = 24 hours (matches Earth's rotation).

Radius: From T = 2π √(r³ / G M), r ≈ 42,000 km.

Application: Communication satellites (e.g., for TV signals).

Weightlessness: Apparent zero weight in free fall or orbit.

Causes: In orbit, centrifugal force balances gravity, so net force feels zero.

Examples: Astronauts in space stations; objects in free-falling elevators.

Not true zero gravity—gravity still acts, but you're in perpetual free fall.

Step-by-Step Understanding:

  • In orbit: Centripetal force = gravitational force: G M m / r² = m v² / r.
  • Weightlessness: Effective g = 0; scales read zero because everything accelerates together.

10 JAMB-Style Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

1. (JAMB 2006): If the radius of the Earth is 6.4 × 10⁶ m, the escape velocity of a satellite from the Earth is? [g = 10 m/s²]
A. 1.13 × 10⁴ m/s B. 9.00 × 10³ m/s C. 8.00 × 10³ m/s D. 1.27 × 10⁴ m/s
Step 1: Escape velocity v_e = √(2 g R).
Step 2: 2 × 10 × 6.4 × 10⁶ = 1.28 × 10⁸.
Step 3: √(1.28 × 10⁸) ≈ 1.13 × 10⁴ m/s.
Answer: A. 1.13 × 10⁴ m/s
2. (Similar to JAMB 1995): What is the minimum escape velocity from Earth's surface? [R = 6.4 × 10³ km, g = 10 m/s²]
A. 14 km/s B. 13 km/s C. 12 km/s D. 11 km/s
Step 1: R = 6.4 × 10⁶ m.
Step 2: v_e = √(2 × 10 × 6.4 × 10⁶) = √(1.28 × 10⁸) ≈ 11,314 m/s ≈ 11 km/s.
Step 3: Matches option D.
Answer: D. 11 km/s
3. (JAMB-style on Force): A force of 200 N acts between two objects at a certain distance apart. The value of the force when the distance is halved is?
A. 50 N B. 100 N C. 400 N D. 800 N
Step 1: F ∝ 1/r².
Step 2: r to r/2, F new = F × (r / (r/2))² = 200 × 4 = 800 N.
Step 3: Matches option D.
Answer: D. 800 N
4. (JAMB 2009): The value of x, y, and z respectively in the expression M^x L^y T^z for the universal gravitational constant G are?
A. 2, -3, -2 B. -1, 3, -2 C. -1, 2, -3 D. -2, -1, 3
Step 1: G = F r² / (m₁ m₂).
Step 2: F = M L T⁻², so G = (M L T⁻²) L² / M² = M⁻¹ L³ T⁻².
Step 3: x = -1, y = 3, z = -2, matches option B.
Answer: B. -1, 3, -2
5. (JAMB 2010): An object is weighed at different locations on the Earth. What will be the right observation?
A. Both mass and weight vary B. Weight constant, mass varies C. Mass constant, weight varies D. Both constant
Step 1: Mass is constant (amount of matter).
Step 2: Weight = m g, g varies with location.
Step 3: Matches option C.
Answer: C. Mass constant, weight varies
6. (JAMB 2010): A stone and a feather are dropped from the same height above the earth surface. Ignoring air resistance, which is correct?
A. Feather blown away, stone steady B. Both reach ground same time C. Feather first D. Stone first
Step 1: g same for all masses.
Step 2: t = √(2h/g), same.
Step 3: Matches option B.
Answer: B. Both reach ground same time
7. (Similar to JAMB): A satellite of mass 500 kg orbits where the gravitational field strength is 2.5 N/kg. What is the gravitational force on it?
A. 1250 N B. 200 N C. 1000 N D. 2500 N
Step 1: Field strength = g = F/m.
Step 2: F = m g = 500 × 2.5 = 1250 N.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 1250 N
8. (JAMB-style on Variation of g): The acceleration due to gravity is maximum at the?
A. Equator B. Poles C. Mountains D. Ocean
Step 1: Poles: smaller radius, no centrifugal.
Step 2: g higher at poles.
Step 3: Matches option B.
Answer: B. Poles
9. (JAMB-style on Potential): The gravitational potential at a point due to Earth is -6.25 × 10⁷ J/kg. What is the potential energy of a 2 kg mass there?
A. -1.25 × 10⁸ J B. -3.125 × 10⁷ J C. 1.25 × 10⁸ J D. 3.125 × 10⁷ J
Step 1: U = m V.
Step 2: 2 × (-6.25 × 10⁷) = -1.25 × 10⁸ J.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. -1.25 × 10⁸ J
10. (JAMB-style on Weightlessness): Astronauts experience weightlessness in space because?
A. No gravity in space B. In free fall around Earth C. Mass is zero D. Air absence
Step 1: Orbit is free fall.
Step 2: Gravity provides centripetal, apparent weight zero.
Step 3: Matches option B.
Answer: B. In free fall around Earth
