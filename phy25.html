📚 Chapter 25: Refraction of Light at Plane and Curved Surfaces

🎯 Learning Objectives: Interpret the laws of refraction, apply refractive index concepts, trace light through triangular and rectangular prisms, calculate minimum deviation, understand critical angle and total internal reflection, apply principles to optical instruments, and solve lens problems using lens formula.

1️⃣ Laws of Refraction 🔬

What is Refraction?

Refraction is the bending of light as it passes from one transparent medium to another of different optical density.

The Two Laws of Refraction (Snell's Laws)

  1. First Law: The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal all lie in the same plane.

  2. Second Law (Snell's Law): The ratio of sine of angle of incidence to sine of angle of refraction is constant for a given pair of media.
    n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r

Key Terms

  • Incident Ray: The light ray approaching the boundary
  • Refracted Ray: The light ray entering the second medium
  • Normal: Line perpendicular to the surface at point of incidence
  • Angle of Incidence (i): Angle between incident ray and normal
  • Angle of Refraction (r): Angle between refracted ray and normal

2️⃣ Refractive Index (n) 💎

Definition

Refractive index is a measure of how much light slows down in a medium compared to vacuum.

n = c/v

Where:
• n = refractive index (no units)
• c = speed of light in vacuum (3 × 10⁸ m/s)
• v = speed of light in the medium

Also: n = sin i / sin r (when light goes from vacuum/air to medium)

Refractive Indices of Common Materials

Material Refractive Index (n)
Vacuum 1.00
Air 1.0003 ≈ 1.00
Water 1.33
Crown Glass 1.52
Flint Glass 1.66
Diamond 2.42
💡 Key Insight: Higher n means light travels slower in that medium and bends more toward the normal when entering.

🔢 Calculation 1: Basic Snell's Law

Question: Light travels from air (n = 1.0) into water (n = 1.33) at an angle of incidence of 40°. Calculate the angle of refraction.

Using n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r:
1.0 × sin 40° = 1.33 × sin r
sin r = (1.0 × 0.6428) / 1.33
sin r = 0.6428 / 1.33 = 0.4833
r = sin⁻¹(0.4833) = 28.9°
Answer: 28.9° (or approximately 29°)

3️⃣ Light Through Prisms 🔺

Triangular Prism

When light passes through a prism, it bends toward the base of the prism. This causes the light to deviate from its original path.

Angle of Deviation (D)

D = (i₁ + i₂) - (r₁ + r₂)
or
D = i₁ + i₂ - A

Where A = angle of prism (apex angle)
At minimum deviation: Dₘ = 2i - A

Minimum Deviation Condition

  • Occurs when light passes symmetrically through the prism
  • i₁ = i₂ and r₁ = r₂
  • Refracted ray inside prism is parallel to the base
Refractive Index from Minimum Deviation:
n = sin[(A + Dₘ)/2] / sin(A/2)

Where:
• A = angle of prism
• Dₘ = angle of minimum deviation

🔢 Calculation 2: Minimum Deviation

Question: A prism with angle 60° produces minimum deviation of 40°. Calculate the refractive index of the prism material.

Using n = sin[(A + Dₘ)/2] / sin(A/2):
n = sin[(60° + 40°)/2] / sin(60°/2)
n = sin(50°) / sin(30°)
n = 0.7660 / 0.5 = 1.532
Answer: 1.53 (crown glass)

4️⃣ Critical Angle and Total Internal Reflection 🔄

Critical Angle (C)

The angle of incidence in the denser medium for which the angle of refraction is 90°.

sin C = n₂/n₁ (where n₁ > n₂)

When going to air (n₂ = 1):
sin C = 1/n

Where n is refractive index of denser medium

Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

  • Occurs when light travels from denser to rarer medium
  • Angle of incidence > critical angle
  • All light reflects back into denser medium
  • No refraction occurs

Applications of TIR

  • Optical fibers: Light signals travel long distances with minimal loss
  • Prismatic binoculars: Compact design using TIR in prisms
  • Diamond sparkle: High refractive index creates small critical angle, causing multiple internal reflections
  • Mirage: Atmospheric TIR creates illusion of water on hot roads

🔢 Calculation 3: Critical Angle

Question: Calculate the critical angle for water (n = 1.33) to air boundary.

Using sin C = 1/n:
sin C = 1/1.33 = 0.7519
C = sin⁻¹(0.7519) = 48.8°
Answer: 48.8° (or approximately 49°)

🔢 Calculation 4: Diamond Critical Angle

Question: Diamond has refractive index 2.42. Calculate its critical angle and explain why diamonds sparkle.

Using sin C = 1/n:
sin C = 1/2.42 = 0.4132
C = sin⁻¹(0.4132) = 24.4°

Explanation: The small critical angle (24.4°) means light entering diamond undergoes multiple total internal reflections before emerging, creating the characteristic sparkle.
Answer: 24.4°

5️⃣ Lenses 🔍

Types of Lenses

Converging (Convex) Diverging (Concave)
Thicker at center than edges Thinner at center than edges
Converges parallel rays to focus Diverges parallel rays from virtual focus
f is positive f is negative
Uses: Magnifying glass, camera, eye Uses: Correcting myopia, peepholes

Lens Formula

1/f = 1/u + 1/v

Linear Magnification:
m = hᵢ/hₒ = v/u

Power of Lens (P):
P = 1/f (in meters)
Unit: Diopter (D)

Note: For lenses, real images have v > 0, virtual images have v < 0 (opposite to mirrors!)

Image Formation by Convex Lens

Object Position Image Position Nature Size
At infinity At F Real, inverted Point
Beyond 2F Between F and 2F Real, inverted Diminished
At 2F At 2F Real, inverted Same size
Between F and 2F Beyond 2F Real, inverted Magnified
At F At infinity - -
Between F and lens Same side as object Virtual, erect Magnified

🔢 Calculation 5: Convex Lens

Question: An object is placed 30 cm from a convex lens of focal length 20 cm. Find the image position and magnification.

Given: u = 30 cm, f = +20 cm

Using 1/f = 1/u + 1/v:
1/20 = 1/30 + 1/v
1/v = 1/20 - 1/30 = (3 - 2)/60 = 1/60
v = 60 cm

Magnification:
m = v/u = 60/30 = 2
Answer: Image at 60 cm on opposite side, real, inverted, magnified 2×

🔢 Calculation 6: Lens Power

Question: A lens has focal length 25 cm. Calculate its power in diopters.

Using P = 1/f (in meters):
f = 25 cm = 0.25 m
P = 1/0.25 = 4 D
Answer: +4 D (converging lens)

6️⃣ Optical Instruments 🔭

Simple Microscope (Magnifying Glass)

  • Convex lens with short focal length
  • Object placed between F and lens
  • Virtual, erect, magnified image formed
  • Angular magnification: M = 1 + D/f (where D = 25 cm, near point)

Compound Microscope

  • Two convex lenses: objective (short f) and eyepiece (longer f)
  • Objective forms real, inverted, magnified image
  • Eyepiece acts as magnifier for this image
  • Total magnification: M = M₀ × Mₑ

Telescope (Astronomical)

  • Two convex lenses: objective (long f) and eyepiece (short f)
  • Objective forms real, inverted image at focal plane
  • Eyepiece magnifies this image
  • Angular magnification: M = f₀/fₑ

🔢 Calculation 7: Microscope Magnification

Question: A simple microscope has focal length 5 cm. Calculate its magnifying power for relaxed eye (image at infinity) and for near point (25 cm).

For relaxed eye (image at infinity):
M = D/f = 25/5 = 5

For near point:
M = 1 + D/f = 1 + 25/5 = 1 + 5 = 6
Answer: 5× (relaxed), 6× (near point)

🔢 Calculation 8: Telescope Magnification

Question: An astronomical telescope has objective focal length 100 cm and eyepiece focal length 5 cm. Calculate its magnifying power.

Using M = f₀/fₑ:
M = 100/5 = 20
Answer: 20×

📋 Quick Revision Tips 🎯

✅ Memory Aids

  • "FAT" — Fast to slow (rarer to denser): bends Toward normal

  • "FAR" — Fast to slow (rarer to denser): Away from normal is wrong!

  • Critical Angle: sin C = 1/n (going to air)

  • Lens vs Mirror: Same formula, but virtual image v is negative for mirrors, positive for lenses!

⚠️ Common JAMB Traps

  1. Sign conventions for lenses vs mirrors — Virtual image v is positive for lenses but negative for mirrors
  2. Critical angle only exists when going from denser to rarer medium
  3. Minimum deviation occurs when light passes symmetrically through prism
  4. Dispersion is the splitting of white light into colors (rainbow formation)
  5. Power of lens is in diopters only when f is in meters
