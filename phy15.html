📘 Chapter 15: Thermal Expansion

🎯 Learning Objectives: Determine linear and volume expansivities, assess effects and applications, determine relationships between different expansivities, determine volume/apparent/real expansivities of liquids, analyze anomalous expansion of water.

PART 1: Foundation Concepts 🔥

When matter is heated, particles vibrate more vigorously, causing expansion. This occurs in solids, liquids, and gases.

Key Principle: Expansion happens in all directions, but we measure it differently depending on situation.

PART 2: Expansion of Solids 🧱

2.1 Three Types of Expansion

Type Symbol Dimensions Formula Relationship
Linear α (alpha) 1D (length) L = L₀(1 + αΔT) Base unit
Superficial/Area β (beta) 2D (area) A = A₀(1 + βΔT) β = 2α
Cubical/Volume γ (gamma) 3D (volume) V = V₀(1 + γΔT) γ = 3α

2.2 The "1-2-3 Rule" (Memory Tip)

1D (Linear) → α
2D (Area) → β = 2α
3D (Volume) → γ = 3α

Think: As dimensions increase, so does the expansivity coefficient!

2.3 Common Expansivity Values

Material Linear Expansivity (α) × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹ Common Use Special Property
Aluminum 23 Cookware, aircraft High expansion
Copper 17 Wires, pipes Good conductor
Brass 19 Bimetallic strips Medium expansion
Iron/Steel 11-12 Rails, structures Strong, moderate α
Glass 9 Containers Low expansion
Invar 0.9 Precision instruments Very low α
Concrete 12 Buildings Needs expansion joints

PART 3: Expansion of Liquids 💧

3.1 The Container Problem

Critical Concept: When heating liquid in container, BOTH expand!

Type Definition Formula Key Point
Real Expansion (γᵣ) Actual expansion of liquid molecules γᵣ = γₐ + γᵍ True molecular expansion
Apparent Expansion (γₐ) What you observe (liquid minus container) γₐ = Apparent volume increase/(V₀ × ΔT) What you see
Container Expansion (γᵍ) Expansion of glass/container γᵍ = 3αᵍ Must be accounted for
MOST IMPORTANT FORMULA:
γᵣ = γₐ + γᵍ

Where γᵍ = 3αᵍ (cubic expansivity of glass/container)

⚠️ Why This Matters

Without considering container expansion:

  • You underestimate the liquid's true expansion
  • JAMB often tests this distinction in calculations
  • Real expansion is always greater than apparent

PART 4: Anomalous Expansion of Water ❄️

4.1 The Exception to the Rule

Water behaves strangely between 0°C and 4°C:

Temperature Range Behavior Volume Change Density
0°C → 4°C CONTRACTS (volume decreases) Decreases Increases
At 4°C MINIMUM VOLUME, MAXIMUM DENSITY Minimum 1000 kg/m³ (max)
Above 4°C Expands normally Increases Decreases

4.2 Why This Saves Aquatic Life

  • Ice forms at surface (0°C) and floats
  • Water at bottom stays at 4°C (densest)
  • Fish survive winter in warmer bottom water!
Water has negative expansivity between 0-4°C:
γ_water ≈ -4.0 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹ (approximate)

PART 5: Applications & Effects 🛠️

5.1 Useful Applications

Application How It Works Where Used
Bimetallic strips Two metals with different α bonded together bend when heated Thermostats, fire alarms, circuit breakers
Expansion gaps Gaps in railway tracks allow expansion without buckling Railways, bridges
Expansion loops Loops in pipes accommodate length changes Hot water pipes, steam lines
Riveting Hot rivets contract when cooled, holding plates tightly Steel construction, ship building
Thermometers Liquid expansion indicates temperature Laboratory, medical

5.2 Problems Caused

Problem Solution Prevention
Sagging power lines Leave slack during installation Proper tensioning in cold weather
Cracking concrete Include expansion joints Design with gaps every 10-15m
Bursting water pipes Insulate pipes, allow drainage Winterization procedures
Glass cracking Use heat-resistant glass, warm gradually Borosilicate glass for labware

🧮 10 Essential Calculations for JAMB

Example 1: Basic Linear Expansion

Question: A copper rod of length 50 cm at 20°C is heated to 100°C. Calculate new length. (α = 17 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹)

Solution:
L = L₀(1 + αΔT)
L₀ = 50 cm, ΔT = 80°C, α = 17 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

L = 50(1 + 17 × 10⁻⁶ × 80)
L = 50(1 + 0.00136)
L = 50 × 1.00136 = 50.068 cm
Answer: 50.068 cm (expansion = 0.068 cm)

Example 2: Finding Original Length

Question: A metal rod expands by 0.5 mm when heated from 20°C to 70°C. If α = 20 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹, find original length.

Solution:
ΔL = L₀αΔT
0.0005 = L₀ × 20 × 10⁻⁶ × 50
0.0005 = L₀ × 0.001
L₀ = 0.0005/0.001 = 0.5 m
Answer: 0.5 m or 50 cm

Example 3: Volume Expansion of Solid

Question: An aluminum cube of side 10 cm at 0°C is heated to 100°C. Calculate increase in volume. (α = 23 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹)

Solution:
γ = 3α = 3 × 23 × 10⁻⁶ = 69 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹
V₀ = 10³ = 1000 cm³

ΔV = V₀γΔT = 1000 × 69 × 10⁻⁶ × 100
ΔV = 6.9 cm³
New volume = 1006.9 cm³
Answer: 6.9 cm³ increase (1006.9 cm³ total)

Example 4: Relationship Between Expansivities

Question: Linear expansivity of metal is 19 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹. Calculate superficial expansivity.

Solution:
β = 2α
β = 2 × 19 × 10⁻⁶
β = 38 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹
Answer: 38 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹
JAMB Tip: Don't confuse with volume expansivity (3α = 57 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹)!

Example 5: Real vs Apparent Expansion

Question: A glass bottle is filled with 500 cm³ mercury at 20°C. When heated to 100°C, mercury appears to rise by 6.5 cm³. Calculate: (a) apparent expansivity (b) real expansivity. (γ_glass = 25 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹)

Solution:
(a) Apparent: γₐ = 6.5/(500 × 80) = 6.5/40000
γₐ = 1.625 × 10⁻⁴ K⁻¹ = 162.5 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

(b) Real: γᵣ = γₐ + γᵍ = 162.5 × 10⁻⁶ + 25 × 10⁻⁶
γᵣ = 187.5 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹
Answer: (a) 162.5 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹ (b) 187.5 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

Example 6: Density Change with Temperature

Question: Density of liquid at 20°C is 800 kg/m³. Find density at 120°C. (γ = 2.0 × 10⁻⁴ K⁻¹)

Solution:
ρ = ρ₀/(1 + γΔT)
ρ = 800/(1 + 2.0 × 10⁻⁴ × 100)
ρ = 800/(1 + 0.02) = 800/1.02
ρ = 784.3 kg/m³
Answer: 784.3 kg/m³

Why: Same mass, larger volume = lower density!

Example 7: Bimetallic Strip

Question: Bimetallic strip: brass (α = 19 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹) and iron (α = 12 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹), each 20 cm at 20°C. Find length difference at 120°C.

Solution:
ΔL_brass = 20 × 19 × 10⁻⁶ × 100 = 0.038 cm
ΔL_iron = 20 × 12 × 10⁻⁶ × 100 = 0.024 cm

Difference = 0.038 - 0.024 = 0.014 cm = 0.14 mm
Answer: 0.14 mm difference

Why it bends: Brass expands more, curves with brass outside!

Example 8: Anomalous Expansion of Water

Question: Water has volume 1000 cm³ at 0°C. At what temperature is volume minimum? Calculate volume at 2°C. (γ = -4.0 × 10⁻⁵ K⁻¹)

Solution:
Minimum volume at: 4°C

At 2°C: V = V₀(1 + γΔT)
V = 1000(1 + (-4.0 × 10⁻⁵) × 2)
V = 1000(1 - 0.00008) = 1000 × 0.99992
V = 999.92 cm³
Answer: Min at 4°C, 999.92 cm³ at 2°C

Example 9: Expansion Gap Calculation

Question: Steel rail is 20 m long at 10°C. Calculate gap needed for max temp 60°C. (α = 11 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹)

Solution:
ΔL = L₀αΔT = 20 × 11 × 10⁻⁶ × 50
ΔL = 20 × 11 × 10⁻⁶ × 50
ΔL = 0.011 m = 11 mm
Answer: 11 mm gap minimum

Example 10: Compound Problem

Question: Glass flask (1000 cm³) filled with mercury at 0°C. At 100°C, 15.3 cm³ overflows. Find: (a) apparent expansivity (b) real expansivity (c) linear expansivity of glass. (γᵣ mercury = 182 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹)

Solution:
(a) γₐ = 15.3/(1000 × 100) = 153 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

(b) γᵣ = 182 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹ (given)

(c) γᵍ = γᵣ - γₐ = 182 × 10⁻⁶ - 153 × 10⁻⁶ = 29 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹
αᵍ = γᵍ/3 = 29 × 10⁻⁶/3 = 9.67 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹
Answer: (a) 153 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹ (b) 182 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹ (c) 9.67 × 10⁻⁶ K⁻¹

🔑 Quick Reference Formula Sheet

Concept Formula When to Use
Linear expansion L = L₀(1 + αΔT) Solids, length changes
Change in length ΔL = L₀αΔT Finding expansion amount
Area expansion A = A₀(1 + 2αΔT) Solids, surface expansion
Volume expansion (solid) V = V₀(1 + 3αΔT) Solids, 3D expansion
Real liquid expansion γᵣ = ΔV_real/(V₀ΔT) True liquid expansion
Apparent liquid expansion γₐ = ΔV_apparent/(V₀ΔT) Observed expansion
Container correction γᵣ = γₐ + γᵍ Liquids in containers
Density change ρ = ρ₀/(1 + γΔT) Finding new density
Relationship β = 2α, γ = 3α Converting between types

📝 JAMB Exam Strategies

  • Focus on Examples 5 & 10 — Most frequent in JAMB
  • Real vs Apparent — Key distinction for top scores
  • Units: α, β, γ in K⁻¹ or °C⁻¹ (same value)
  • ΔT identical in K or °C
  • Be consistent with length units (m, cm, mm)

⚠️ Most Important Distinction

Real expansion = What actually happens to liquid molecules

Apparent expansion = What you observe (liquid minus container)
