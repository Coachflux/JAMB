Introduction to Elasticity

Elasticity is the property of a material that allows it to return to its original shape and size after a deforming force is removed. Materials like rubber bands, springs, and metal wires exhibit elasticity up to a certain point. Beyond that, they may deform permanently or break. This topic covers how materials behave under stretching or compression, based on principles from physics textbooks like New School Physics by M.W. Anyakoha.

We'll break it down step by step for easy understanding, following the JAMB objectives: interpreting force-extension curves, Hooke's law and Young's modulus, using a spring balance, and calculating work done in springs and elastic strings.

Step 1: Understanding Hooke's Law

Hooke's law states that, provided the elastic limit is not exceeded, the extension (or compression) of an elastic material is directly proportional to the applied force.

Mathematical Expression: F = k e

  • F = applied force (in Newtons, N)
  • e = extension (change in length, in meters, m)
  • k = spring constant or force constant (in N/m), which measures the stiffness of the material. A higher k means the material is stiffer and harder to stretch.

Elastic Limit: This is the maximum force or stress beyond which the material does not return to its original shape when the force is removed. Up to this point, deformation is elastic (temporary); beyond it, it's plastic (permanent).

Example in Everyday Life: When you pull a rubber band gently, it stretches and snaps back. If you pull too hard, it stays stretched or breaks.

Step 2: Young's Modulus

Young's modulus (E) is a measure of the stiffness of a material. It tells us how much a material will stretch under a given force, independent of its size or shape.

Definitions:

  • Stress = Force per unit cross-sectional area = F / A (in N/m² or Pascals, Pa)
  • Strain = Extension per unit original length = e / L (no units, it's a ratio)
  • Young's Modulus: E = Stress / Strain = (F / A) / (e / L) = F L / (A e)

Units: N/m² or Pa.

It applies within the elastic limit and is constant for a given material (e.g., steel has a high E, rubber has a low E).

Interpretation: A material with high Young's modulus (like diamond) resists deformation more than one with low modulus (like rubber).

Step 3: Force-Extension Curves

A force-extension graph shows how a material responds to increasing force. Plot force (F) on the y-axis and extension (e) on the x-axis.

Key Points on the Curve:

  • Proportional Limit (P): Up to here, the graph is a straight line (obeys Hooke's law exactly). Slope = k = F/e.
  • Elastic Limit (E): Slightly beyond P, the material still returns to original shape if force is removed.
  • Yield Point (Y): Permanent deformation begins; the material "yields" and stretches more easily.
  • Breaking Point (B): The material snaps or breaks under maximum stress.

Interpretation:

  • Straight line region: Elastic behavior.
  • Curved region: Plastic behavior.
  • Area under the curve represents work done (energy stored or dissipated).

How to Interpret: If the curve is steep, the material is stiff (high k or E). If it has a long plastic region, the material is ductile (can be drawn into wires, like copper).

Step 4: Spring Balance as a Device for Measuring Force

A spring balance (or Newton meter) uses Hooke's law to measure force, like weight.

How It Works:

  • A mass hangs on the spring, causing extension e.
  • The force (weight) = mg, where m = mass, g = 9.8 m/s² (or 10 m/s² in some problems).
  • From Hooke's law: mg = k e, so force is read from the scale calibrated to extension.

Usage: Hang the object, read the extension on the scale, which is marked in Newtons. It's accurate within the elastic limit.

Tip: If two springs are in series (end-to-end), effective k = k₁ k₂ / (k₁ + k₂). In parallel (side-by-side), k = k₁ + k₂.

Step 5: Work Done in Springs and Elastic Strings

When you stretch a spring or elastic string, you do work, which is stored as elastic potential energy.

Work Done (W): Since force increases from 0 to F, average force = F/2.

W = ½ F e

Or, from Hooke's law (F = k e): W = ½ k e²

Work Done Per Unit Volume:

  • Volume of material = A L (cross-section A, length L).
  • Work per unit volume = W / (A L) = ½ × (F e) / (A L) = ½ × stress × strain

Interpretation: This energy is released when the material snaps back (e.g., in a catapult). For strings, it's similar but often treated as 1D.

10 JAMB-Style Examples with Step-by-Step Solutions

1. A spring extends by 0.05 m when a force of 10 N is applied. What is the spring constant k?
A. 50 N/m B. 100 N/m C. 200 N/m D. 500 N/m
Step 1: Use Hooke's law: F = k e.
Step 2: Rearrange: k = F / e = 10 / 0.05 = 200 N/m.
Step 3: Matches option C.
Answer: C. 200 N/m
2. A wire of length 3 m and cross-sectional area 2 × 10⁻⁶ m² is extended by 0.003 m under a force of 50 N. Calculate Young's modulus.
A. 2.5 × 10¹⁰ Pa B. 5 × 10¹⁰ Pa C. 2.5 × 10⁹ Pa D. 5 × 10⁹ Pa
Step 1: E = (F L) / (A e).
Step 2: F = 50 N, L = 3 m, A = 2 × 10⁻⁶ m², e = 0.003 m.
Step 3: E = (50 × 3) / (2 × 10⁻⁶ × 0.003) = 150 / (6 × 10⁻⁹) = 2.5 × 10¹⁰ Pa.
Step 4: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 2.5 × 10¹⁰ Pa
3. In a force-extension graph, the straight-line portion ends at F = 20 N and e = 0.02 m. What is the elastic limit if this is the proportional limit?
A. 20 N B. Slightly above 20 N C. 0.02 m D. Cannot determine
Step 1: Proportional limit is where the line is straight (Hooke's law holds).
Step 2: Elastic limit is slightly beyond this point, where material still returns to shape.
Step 3: Matches option B.
Answer: B. Slightly above 20 N
4. A spring balance reads 5 N when a mass of 0.5 kg is hung on it. What is the extension if k = 100 N/m? (g = 10 m/s²)
A. 0.05 m B. 0.1 m C. 0.5 m D. 5 m
Step 1: Force = mg = 0.5 × 10 = 5 N (matches reading).
Step 2: e = F / k = 5 / 100 = 0.05 m.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 0.05 m
5. Work done in stretching a spring by 0.1 m is 2 J. What is the spring constant k?
A. 200 N/m B. 400 N/m C. 40 N/m D. 20 N/m
Step 1: W = ½ k e².
Step 2: 2 = ½ k (0.1)² → 2 = ½ k × 0.01 → 2 = 0.005 k.
Step 3: k = 2 / 0.005 = 400 N/m.
Step 4: Matches option B.
Answer: B. 400 N/m
6. A material breaks at a stress of 10⁸ Pa. If its Young's modulus is 2 × 10¹⁰ Pa, what is the strain at breaking point?
A. 0.005 B. 0.05 C. 0.5 D. 5
Step 1: E = stress / strain.
Step 2: strain = stress / E = 10⁸ / (2 × 10¹⁰) = 0.005.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 0.005
7. Two springs of constants 100 N/m and 200 N/m are connected in series. What is the effective k?
A. 66.7 N/m B. 150 N/m C. 300 N/m D. 50 N/m
Step 1: For series: 1/k_eff = 1/k₁ + 1/k₂.
Step 2: 1/k_eff = 1/100 + 1/200 = 0.01 + 0.005 = 0.015.
Step 3: k_eff = 1 / 0.015 ≈ 66.7 N/m.
Step 4: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 66.7 N/m
8. The work done per unit volume in an elastic string stretched to strain 0.02 with stress 4 × 10⁶ Pa is:
A. 4 × 10⁴ J/m³ B. 8 × 10⁴ J/m³ C. 4 × 10⁶ J/m³ D. 2 × 10⁴ J/m³
Step 1: Work/volume = ½ × stress × strain.
Step 2: ½ × 4 × 10⁶ × 0.02 = ½ × 8 × 10⁴ = 4 × 10⁴ J/m³.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 4 × 10⁴ J/m³
9. A yield point on a force-extension curve indicates:
A. Start of permanent deformation B. Breaking point C. Elastic limit D. Proportional limit
Step 1: Yield point is where material begins to deform plastically after elastic limit.
Step 2: Matches option A.
Answer: A. Start of permanent deformation
10. A catapult stretches a rubber string by 0.08 m with average force 50 N. What is the work done? (Assume elastic.)
A. 2 J B. 4 J C. 8 J D. 16 J
Step 1: W = ½ F e (average force used in simple calculation).
Step 2: W = ½ × 50 × 0.08 = ½ × 4 = 2 J.
Step 3: Matches option A.
Answer: A. 2 J
