1. Characteristics of Living Organisms Living organisms exhibit certain features that distinguish them from non-living things. These are often remembered using MRS GREN (or similar mnemonics like MR NIGER D for some variations). The key characteristics include: Movement/Locomotion ‚Äî Living things move in response to stimuli or for survival (e.g., animals walk/run, plants grow towards light ‚Äî phototropism). Respiration ‚Äî The process of breaking down food to release energy (aerobic or anaerobic). Nutrition ‚Äî Taking in food/materials for energy and growth (autotrophic like plants via photosynthesis, or heterotrophic like animals). Irritability/Sensitivity ‚Äî Ability to detect and respond to changes in the environment (e.g., withdrawal reflex in humans, closing of Mimosa leaves). Growth ‚Äî Irreversible increase in size/mass due to cell division and enlargement. Excretion ‚Äî Removal of metabolic waste products (e.g., carbon dioxide, urea, excess water). Reproduction ‚Äî Producing offspring to continue the species (sexual or asexual). Adaptation ‚Äî Ability to adjust to environmental changes over time (leads to evolution). Non-living things lack most or all of these (e.g., a stone does not grow, reproduce, or respond to stimuli in a coordinated biological way). 2. Cell Theory (Detailed Explanation) Cell theory is a fundamental unifying principle in biology stating that the cell is the basic structural, functional, and organizational unit of all living organisms. Historical Development: Robert Hooke (1665) first observed "cells" in cork (dead plant tissue) using a microscope ‚Äî named them because they resembled monk's cells. Antonie van Leeuwenhoek (1670s) observed living cells ("animalcules") like bacteria and protozoa. Robert Brown (1833) discovered the nucleus in plant cells. Matthias Schleiden (1838, botanist) concluded all plants are made of cells. Theodor Schwann (1839, zoologist) extended it to animals: all animals are made of cells. Rudolf Virchow (1855) added: "Omnis cellula e cellula" ‚Äî all cells arise from pre-existing cells (refuting spontaneous generation). Modern (Unified) Cell Theory ‚Äî Three core principles: All living organisms are composed of one or more cells (unicellular like Amoeba or multicellular like humans/plants). The cell is the basic (fundamental) unit of structure, function, and organization in living things ‚Äî All life processes (metabolism, growth, reproduction) occur at the cellular level. All cells arise from pre-existing cells through cell division (mitosis/meiosis) ‚Äî No spontaneous generation; continuity of life via heredity (DNA passed during division). Modern extensions (sometimes included): Cells contain hereditary information (DNA); energy flows through cells; similar cells in a species are structurally/chemically alike. This theory explains why cells are studied as the foundation of biology and disproved ideas like spontaneous generation.

3. Cell Structure and Functions of Cell Components

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life (Cell Theory: all living things are made of cells; cells come from pre-existing cells; cells carry out life processes).

There are two main types relevant here: plant cells and animal cells (both eukaryotic). Plant Cell Structure (e.g., from onion epidermis or leaf cells) Cell wall (rigid, made of cellulose) ‚Äî Provides support, shape, and protection; prevents bursting in hypotonic solutions. Cell membrane (plasma membrane) ‚Äî Semi-permeable; controls entry/exit of substances. Cytoplasm ‚Äî Jelly-like fluid where most metabolic reactions occur. Nucleus ‚Äî Contains chromatin (DNA); controls cell activities and heredity. Chloroplasts (contain chlorophyll) ‚Äî Site of photosynthesis. Large central vacuole ‚Äî Stores water, nutrients, waste; maintains turgidity. Mitochondria ‚Äî Site of aerobic respiration (energy production). Ribosomes ‚Äî Protein synthesis. Endoplasmic reticulum (rough and smooth) ‚Äî Transport and synthesis (rough has ribosomes). Golgi apparatus ‚Äî Packaging and secretion of materials. Animal Cell Structure (e.g., cheek cells) No cell wall (only cell membrane). No chloroplasts. No large central vacuole (small or many small ones). Has centrioles (for cell division/spindle formation). Other components similar: nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, ER, Golgi, cytoplasm. Comparison of Plant and Animal Cells Similarities ‚Äî Both have cell membrane, nucleus, cytoplasm, mitochondria, ribosomes, ER, Golgi. Differences: Plant: Has cell wall, chloroplasts, large central vacuole. Animal: Lacks cell wall/chloroplasts/large vacuole; has centrioles. Functions summary: Cell wall (plant only) ‚Äî Mechanical support/protection. Chloroplast (plant only) ‚Äî Photosynthesis (light energy ‚Üí glucose). Mitochondria ‚Äî Energy (ATP) production via respiration. Nucleus ‚Äî Genetic control, cell division. Ribosomes ‚Äî Protein synthesis. Vacuole ‚Äî Storage, turgor pressure (large in plants). 3. Levels of Organization Living organisms show increasing complexity from simple to complex. JAMB syllabus recognizes five levels in logical sequence: i. Cell level ‚Äî Basic unit of life (unicellular organisms perform all life processes in one cell). Examples: Euglena (protist with flagellum for movement; has chloroplast for photosynthesis and eyespot for light detection ‚Äî autotrophic/heterotrophic). Paramecium (ciliate protist with cilia for movement, oral groove for feeding, contractile vacuoles for osmoregulation). Also Chlamydomonas (mentioned under organisms; unicellular green alga with flagella and chloroplast). ii. Tissue level ‚Äî Group of similar cells performing the same function. Examples: Epithelial tissues (in animals) ‚Äî Cover surfaces, protect, secrete/absorb (e.g., skin, lining of gut). Hydra (coelenterate) ‚Äî Simple multicellular with tissue-like layers (ectoderm and endoderm) but not true tissues in complex sense; shows tissue-level organization. iii. Organ level ‚Äî Group of different tissues working together for a specific function. Example: Onion bulb ‚Äî Modified organ (underground stem) with scale leaves (modified for storage); tissues include protective, storage parenchyma, vascular. iv. System level ‚Äî Group of organs working together for major life function. Examples: Reproductive system ‚Äî Organs like ovaries/testes, uterus, etc., for reproduction. Digestive system ‚Äî Mouth, stomach, intestines, etc., for food breakdown and absorption. Excretory system ‚Äî Kidneys, ureters, bladder for waste removal. v. Organism level ‚Äî Whole living individual (complete functional unit). Examples: Unicellular like Chlamydomonas, multicellular like humans, plants, or animals. Logical sequence: Cell ‚Üí Tissue ‚Üí Organ ‚Üí System ‚Üí Organism. This hierarchy shows how complexity increases: unicellular (e.g., Euglena) stop at cell level; simple multicellular (e.g., Hydra) reach tissue; higher animals/plants reach all five levels.
